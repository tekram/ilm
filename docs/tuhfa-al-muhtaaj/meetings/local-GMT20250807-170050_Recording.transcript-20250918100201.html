Agency and Sale Rules - August 07, 2025

Start: 2025-08-07T17:00:50

Topic

Rules of agency (wakālah) in sales — rights and limits of an agent when the agency is absolute or not specifically restricted.

Purpose

Translate and consolidate lesson segments into a single clear English fiqh summary emphasizing key rulings, definitions, evidences, differences of opinion, and practical action items.

Key definitions

  • Absolute agency (wakāla mutlaqah): authorization to “sell” (or act) without specification of place, currency, time, buyer, or other conditions. Default meaning is broad but subject to limits of custom and protection of the principal.
  • Cash of the country (naqd al‑bilād): whatever the inhabitants commonly use as money in that place — coin, a particular currency, or commodities functioning as money by local custom.
  • Ghubn (unfairness): manifest/excessive injustice in price (ghubn fāḥish) — a gross undervaluation (e.g., selling at 2 when worth 10); minor variations are judged by customary tolerance (ʿurf).
  • Khiyār: option/choice to rescind a sale for valid reasons (defect, ignorance, etc.).
  • Fi al‑dhimmah: purchase recorded as a debt/obligation (credit purchase in the principal’s name).
  • Amīn: a trustworthy, competent person required for appointment or sub‑agency.

Main rulings and legal points

General rule on unspecified/absolute authorizations

  • If an agent is authorized merely to “sell” without limits, the agency is generally taken as absolute and the sale is valid, but subject to protective limitations (custom, prohibition of gross loss, and the principal’s presumed interest).
  • Default constraints arise from: local custom (ʿurf), avoidance of ghubn fāḥish, and basic duty not to act against the principal’s interest.

Currency and place rules

  • When selling in a particular country/market, the agent should transact in the currency or medium customary in that place (naqd al‑bilād).
  • If multiple currencies/forms circulate:
  • Use the currency most commonly used (the prevalent one).
  • If equally used, prefer what is most beneficial to the principal; if still unclear, the principal decides or both may be accepted.
  • If the agent takes goods to another country without authorization, he may not validly sell there in a foreign currency unless specifically authorized for that place.

Credit sales, deferment, and timing

  • An absolute/unspecified mandate does not normally authorize selling on credit (nasiya). Deferred sales are risky and usually disallowed unless:
  • The principal expressly permitted credit sales; or
  • Selling on credit is necessary to preserve the asset (necessity), judged by custom and urgency.
  • If a sale on a specified deferred term was authorized, the appointed term binds parties; if unspecified, apply the customary term for that market or choose what benefits the principal.
  • Agent should not deliver goods before receiving the price unless expressly allowed; premature delivery makes the agent liable.

Ghubn (unfair price) and permissible variance

  • Agent must avoid ghubn fāḥish (gross unfairness). If sale results in manifest, excessive loss, the agent is liable and the sale may be defective.
  • There is no universally fixed percentage applicable to all goods. Some jurists mention approximate tolerances (e.g., ~10% in certain contexts), but the preferred approach is to apply local custom and the commodity’s nature (e.g., food/cash vs. valuables).
  • Minor customary differences are permitted and judged by ʿurf.

Warranty, liability, and remedies

  • If agent breaches limits (wrong currency, unauthorized credit, gross undervaluation, selling to himself without permission):
  • The agent is liable to restore the principal’s rights: return proceeds, make good shortfall, or recover the sold item (or its fair equivalent/market value).
  • If the principal received money and spent it, the agent still remains liable to compensate the principal.
  • For fungible goods, remedies follow principles of equivalence (mithliyya) and market valuation; the agent’s liability persists until the principal’s right is restored.
  • If agent was ignorant of a defect (ʿayb) at time of contract, many scholars treat the sale as binding on the principal and excuse the agent; if the agent knew or was negligent, the agent is culpable and the sale may not bind the principal.

Ambiguous authorizations and risky phrases

  • Phrases like “sell as you wish” (bi‑mā shiʾta) or “do whatever you want” are ambiguous and problematic due to gharar (uncertainty) and potential abuse.
  • Cautious position: do not treat such wording as authority to undertake risky or loss‑causing transactions (no permission for long credit or manifest loss unless explicitly stated).
  • Lenient position: allows broader discretion; however, the lesson favors the cautious reading to protect the principal’s interests.
  • Ambiguous words should be interpreted by apparent intention and local custom.

Self‑dealing and representing both sides

  • General rule: an agent should not act for both buyer and seller (i.e., buy for himself from the principal or represent both parties) because it undermines the two‑party nature of sale and risks conflict of interest.
  • Exceptions/nuances:
  • If the principal expressly permits the agent to buy for himself, or fixes the price and conditions to remove suspicion, some jurists allow it.
  • Transactions with independent adult relatives may be less problematic (juristic opinions differ).
  • Trustees, guardians, and waqf managers face stricter prohibitions against self‑dealing.

Sub‑agency (delegation) and wording effects

  • An agent may appoint a sub‑agent only if:
  • The principal authorized delegation; or
  • The task is such that the agent cannot reasonably perform it personally (lack of capacity, exceptional burden).
  • The legal relation of the sub‑agent depends on wording:
  • If the principal told the agent to appoint “on my behalf” (wakil ‘anni), the sub‑agent becomes agent of the principal.
  • If the instruction was “appoint on your own behalf” (wakil ‘an nafsak), the sub‑agent is agent of the agent (true sub‑agent).
  • The agent must appoint an amīn (trustworthy person); appointing a known fāsiq (corrupt) is invalid/binding on the agent, not on the principal.

Termination and removal

  • Agency ends on dismissal by the principal, death, insanity, or other incapacitating events.
  • If an agent appoints a person as the principal’s agent (via wording), the first agent cannot unilaterally dismiss a person the principal appointed directly.

Delivery, dependents (ʿayāl), and coercion

  • Delivery/receipt by household dependents (ʿayāl — e.g., competent adult household members) is generally valid where customary trust exists; scholars differ on precise scope.
  • Coercion (ikrah):
  • If an agent is forced by an unlawful oppressor to deliver or collect, liability regimes vary: some scholars treat the agent as liable (deposit/guarantee) while others excuse him; compulsion by legitimate judicial authority more readily excuses the agent.
  • Document and seek adjudication when coercion occurs.

Differences of opinion (summarized)

  • Broad permissive phrases: whether they suffice to allow sales at a loss or long credit — majority cautious; minority more permissive.
  • Measurement of acceptable price deviation (ghubn): some jurists propose fixed tolerances in certain contexts; others reject numerical limits and rely on ʿurf and commodity type.
  • Agent buying/selling to himself: majority disallow; some permit under explicit authorization/fixed price/no suspicion.
  • Sub‑delegation: majority forbid without permission but allow when agent lacks capacity; differences on whether agent may delegate after appointment if circumstances change.
  • Liability under coercion: disagreement whether coercion by an unlawful oppressor renders the agent liable or excused.
  • Use of household dependents (ʿayāl) for delivery: permissibility and scope vary among commentators.

Evidences and sources

  • No explicit Qurʾānic verses or hadiths were cited in the recorded segments; rulings rely on juristic principles, analogical reasoning, and reliance on ʿurf.
  • Classical jurists/commentators referenced in the lessons: Ibn Abī Damm, al‑Ruyyān, Ibn al‑Rifaʿah, al‑Jūrī, Ibn Hajar, al‑Ramli, al‑Nawawī, al‑Sabkī, among others. The school orientation of the lesson is largely Shāfiʿī with comparative notes.

Practical action items — checklist for principals and agents

For principals (muwakkil)

  • Draft clear written authorization specifying:
  • Purpose (sell/buy), place/market, and permitted currency (naqd al‑bilād or named currency).
  • Whether credit sales (nasiya) are allowed; if allowed, specify maximum deferment (ajal) or state that customary market terms apply.
  • Whether the agent may delegate (sub‑agency) and whether sub‑agents act for the principal or for the agent.
  • Whether the agent may sell to or buy for himself or relatives.
  • Limits on acceptable price deviation or instruction to follow local custom (ʿurf).
  • Whether the agent may deliver goods before receiving payment and whether the agent may receive payment on principal’s behalf.
  • Warranty and risk allocation until delivery (who bears loss if damage/theft occurs before handing over proceeds).
  • Require agents to appoint only trustworthy persons (amīn) as sub‑agents.
  • Include continuity provisions for partnerships and authority to replace/remove agents.

For agents (wakīl)

  • Use the currency/custom of the authorized place; avoid selling in a foreign currency without express permission.
  • Do not sell on credit or deliver before receiving the price unless expressly authorized or necessary to prevent loss — document reasons if done by necessity.
  • Avoid self‑dealing; obtain explicit written permission before buying for yourself or representing both sides.
  • Avoid gross undervaluation (ghubn fāḥish); follow local custom for normal price variation; consult the principal before significant discounts.
  • Do not appoint a known corrupt/untrustworthy sub‑agent; seek written permission for delegation and name the appointee if necessary.
  • Keep receipts, records, and notify the principal promptly of defects, coercion, or unusual events.
  • If coerced, document facts and seek a judicial ruling; do not assume automatic exculpation.

Practical steps when problems arise

  • If agent exceeded authority and sold the principal’s item:
  • Principal may reclaim the item if still recoverable, or demand compensation (equivalent market value) from the agent.
  • If replacement of the sold item of the same genus/quality is offered by purchaser, acceptability depends on the principal’s consent and applicable juristic position.
  • If defect discovered in purchased goods:
  • Determine whether agent knew/was negligent; if ignorant, the buy generally binds the principal; if agent knew, agent is liable.
  • Check whether purchase was in the principal’s name (fi al‑dhimmah) or made with specific money (muʿayyan) — remedies differ.

Short practical summary

  • Clear, specific agency clauses prevent disputes: specify place, currency, credit permissions, delegation rights, quality warranties, and limits on discounts/self‑dealing.
  • If the agency is silent, interpret by local custom (ʿurf): use the customary money, avoid grossly unfair prices, and act in the principal’s best interest.
  • Ambiguous permissive wording should be read restrictively to avoid gharar and potential loss to the principal.
  • Agents who exceed authority, act negligently, or knowingly conceal defects are liable to restore the principal’s rights; sub‑agency and self‑dealing require express permission and trustworthy appointees.

Follow‑up / recommended drafting language (brief)

  • If desired, sample concise agency clauses can be drafted for common cases: (1) sale in local currency cash only; (2) sale allowing up to X days credit; (3) prohibition on self‑purchase; (4) permission to sub‑delegate only to named or qualified amīn. (Ask if you want such sample clauses.)
