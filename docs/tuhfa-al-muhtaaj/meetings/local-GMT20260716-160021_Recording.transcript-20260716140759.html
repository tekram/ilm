Kitāb al-Ijāra: Post-Lease Obligations, Overloaded Cargo, Tailor Disputes & Wages for Teaching — July 16, 2026

📅 2026-07-16 🕐 16:00

Session Overview

This session continued the study of Kitāb al-Ijāra (Book of Hire and Lease) from the Tuḥfat al-Muḥtāj of Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī with Shaykh Ibn Qāsim's commentary. Topics spanned the nature of the lessee's liability, post-lease obligations, overloaded cargo, tailor-client disputes, and an extended discussion on the permissibility of taking wages for teaching Quran and religious knowledge.

1. The Lessee's Hand: A Hand of Amāna (Trust)

Background. The text (Tuḥfat al-Muḥtāj) establishes that the lessee's (mustāʾjir's) hand over the leased item (al-ʿayn al-muʾajjara) is a yad amāna — a hand of trust, not a hand of liability. This applies to both a lease of a specific item (ijārat al-ʿayn) and a lease of a guaranteed service (ijārat al-dhimma).

The Tuḥfa notes the difference from a sale where the buyer holds the item during the majlis al-khiyār (option period): the buyer's hand is a hand of liability because he takes possession for his own purposes (li-gharaḍ nafsihi). The lessee's hand, by contrast, is not exclusively for his benefit — it is also for the benefit of the owner.

Ruling. The lessee does not owe any compensation for damage to the leased item that occurs without transgression (taʿaddī) on his part.

2. Travel with the Leased Item

Ruling. The lessee may travel with a leased item wherever he wishes, as long as there is no unusual risk from the travel. He owns the manfaʿa (usufruct) for the duration of the lease and may exercise it wherever he chooses. If the lessee travels after the lease expires, his liability shifts to the rules governing one who keeps a wadīʿa (deposit) beyond the original arrangement.

3. Post-Lease Obligations: Takhlia vs. Active Return

Background. After the lease period ends, what does the lessee owe?

The Tuḥfa adopts — against al-Subkī — the position that the lessee owes only takhlia (relinquishing hold; ceasing use; not preventing retrieval). He is not required to proactively deliver the item back or notify the owner.

Key distinction — the ḥānūt (shop) vs. the dābba (animal):

Al-Baghawī: someone leases a shop for one month, locks it and disappears for two months — owes ajr musammā for month 1, ajrat al-mithl for month 2. Al-Qaffāl's contrasting ruling on a leased animal: if the lessee ties it up and neither uses it nor prevents retrieval, he owes nothing for the second day.

Ibn Ḥajar's analysis:

  • Animal: The owner can retrieve it at will without the lessee's cooperation. Mere non-use without obstruction creates no liability.
  • Shop: If the lessee locks the shop and leaves, the owner literally cannot access it — the locked door is itself a ḥabs (detention). The ghayba (absence) combined with the lock is what creates liability for month 2, because it prevents the owner from exercising control over his property.

Implication. If the lessee remains reachable, his obligation is only to comply with requests for return. Disappearing with the key makes him liable for ajrat al-mithl for the period of effective ḥabs.

4. Lease for Building or Planting: Options at Expiry

Ruling. If a lease for building or planting expires with structures/crops still on the land, the lessee may choose qalaʿ (removal). If he does not, the landlord gets three options: (1) take ownership by paying qīmat mustahaqq al-qalaʿ; (2) demand removal and pay arsh al-nuqs (compensation for removal damage to the land); (3) a third option in some texts, not the primary relied-upon position.

5. Liability of the Ajīr for Loss of Entrusted Items

Background. The text distinguishes the ajīr mushtarak (shared laborer committed to a task in his dhimma, e.g., tailor) from the ajīr munfarid (exclusive hire for a period).

Three positions on the ajīr mushtarak's liability: (1) Aẓhar: no liability without transgression — like the muḍāraba partner; (2) liable, analogized to the mustaʿīr; (3) al-Māwardī: liable at the item's value on the day of loss.

Special case — the watchman (ḥāris al-sikka). Al-Qaffāl: if a street watchman fails to prevent theft, he is not liable categorically — items were never in his possession. The Zarkashī extracts that the khafīr generally owes no ḍamān.

Transgression creates liability: handing an animal to a third party, a teacher striking beyond tāʾdīb, a husband striking beyond permitted bounds — all create liability for resulting harm.

6. Overloading a Hired Animal

Ruling. Hiring an animal for 100 raṭl of wheat but loading 100 raṭl of barley: barley distributes differently across the animal's back (lighter per unit volume, spreads more), creating different ḍarar. Sub-cases:

  • Different substances, same weight: liability depends on meaningful difference in effect (cotton vs. iron at same weight — different ḍarar).
  • Overload by 10 raṭl: owes ajrat al-mithl for the extra 10; if the animal perishes, bears ḍamān yad for the overloaded portion (proportional if owner present; full if absent).
  • Owner weighed and loaded himself: no liability on lessee; owner must return the extra to its original location.
  • Owner delivered 110 raṭl to the animal-owner who loaded unknowingly: lessee (muttajir) bears proportional liability by the madhhab position.

7. Dispute Between Tailor and Customer Over Garment Type

Background. A tailor cuts a garment as a qabāʾ (open-fronted robe) and the customer says he ordered a qamīṣ (closed shirt). The garment is already cut.

Ruling. Aẓhar: the customer (mālik) is believed with an oath. No wage is owed to the tailor since his authorization is unestablished. The tailor must pay arsh al-nuqs (difference in value: garment as currently cut vs. as it should have been cut).

Disagreement. Second position: mutual oaths (yataḥālfān). The Isnawī's critique: tailor's oath establishes absence of permission, meaning no wage and arsh al-nuqs falls on the tailor for the deviation.

Before cutting: mutual oaths by consensus of all positions. Same rules extend to copyists deviating from chapter order, or anyone delivering a specification-violation that cannot be undone.

8. Taking Wages for Teaching Quran and Religious Knowledge

Background. The ḥadīth "he used the Quran as a means of livelihood" (ittakhadha Qurānahu mutākallan) has been cited against taking wages for religious teaching.

Ruling. The Shaykh explained this ḥadīth refers to a scholar who corrupts his fatwa for money — affirming what patrons want rather than what the Quran says. It does not prohibit wages for teaching.

Historical practice: scholars throughout Islamic history received stipends and fees for teaching Quran, fiqh, and other religious sciences. Al-Nawawī commended a teacher who teaches with sound intention and accepts fair compensation: such a person receives double reward — for the teaching itself, and for seeking ḥalāl livelihood (which is a farīḍa).

The Shaykh added: a doctor or businessman who accepts a teaching wage may earn even greater reward than a full-time volunteer scholar, combining two noble intentions — teaching and pursuing ḥalāl employment.

American context. Qualified scholars are few in the US; for many, teaching requires leaving lucrative employment. A teacher who accepts payment with sound intention earns both rewards. One participant observed that students who pay a fee attend more consistently — a teacher who charges partly to enforce commitment likely earns the full teaching reward since the fee serves a legitimate pedagogical purpose.

Referenced Scholars & Texts

  • Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī: Tuḥfat al-Muḥtāj fī Sharḥ al-Minhāj — primary text throughout
  • ʿAbd al-Ḥamīd ibn Qāsim: Ḥāshiyat Ibn Qāsim on the Tuḥfa — extensively cited; dispute with Shabrāmallisī on lease contract validity after Friday prayer begins
  • Shabrāmallisī: cited in disagreement with Ibn Qāsim on the lease-after-adhan question
  • Al-Baghawī: the ḥānūt case — foundational narration on post-lease liability when lessee disappears
  • Al-Qaffāl: the dābba case and the watchman's exemption from liability
  • Al-Isnawī: critique of the Aẓhar position on tailor-customer disputes
  • Al-Zarkashī: principle that the khafīr (watchman) generally bears no ḍamān
  • Al-Subkī: minority position that depositor-equivalent must actively notify the owner
  • Al-Nawawī: commendation of teachers who take wages with correct intention
  • Al-Rāfiʿī (al-Anwār): referenced on the shop-key question
