Ijāra for Nursing & Childcare, Physician Liability, Landlord Duties & Talfīq Q&A — June 25, 2026

📅 2026-06-25 🕐 16:00

Overview

This Tuḥfat al-Muḥtāj session continues the chapter on ijāra (hire/lease), covering nursing contracts (ijārat al-riḍāʿ), the distinction between nursing (riḍāʿ) and large childcare (ḥaḍāna kubrā), mandatory materials in hire contracts, liability of the hired physician (al-ṭabīb al-ajīr), and landlord vs. tenant obligations. The session closes with a Q&A on lending to a riba debtor, talfīq across separate acts, and whether a woman switching madhhabs mid-menstrual cycle owes makeup prayers.

1. Ijāra for Nursing and Childcare

Validity and Conditions

A woman — whether free or enslaved, Muslim or non-Muslim — may validly be hired for nursing (riḍāʿ) and childcare if she is trustworthy (amīna). The contract must specify three things: (1) duration (mudda), (2) location (makān), and (3) identification of the child (taʿyīn al-mursaʿ) — all three are required.

Small vs. Large Ḥaḍāna

Small ḥaḍāna (ḥaḍāna ṣughrā): Holding the infant for nursing — subsumed within the nursing contract.

Large ḥaḍāna (ḥaḍāna kubrā): Comprehensive childcare — washing (ghasl), oiling (tadhin), swaddling (taqmīṭ), and attending to the infant's bodily needs. This is a separate and entirely distinct contract from nursing; the hire of a wet nurse does not include large ḥaḍāna.

Obligations of the Wet Nurse

The wet nurse must eat foods that increase milk production and avoid foods that would harm the milk. Intercourse with her husband is suspended (mahrūm) during the nursing period if it would harm the infant. If the milk dries up mid-contract: only the nursing portion (jihat al-riḍāʿ) fails; the childcare component survives independently.

2. Mandatory Materials in Hire Contracts (Al-Āla fī ʿAqd al-Ijāra)

The Stronger Opinion

The stronger view: auxiliary materials are not owed by the hired party (al-āla laysat ʿalā al-ajīr) — ink is not owed by the scribe, thread is not owed by the tailor, kohl is not owed by the eye-doctor. The hired party's obligation is the labor (ʿamal), not the inputs.

The Role of Custom (ʿUrf)

Al-Rāfiʿī clarified: custom (ʿurf) governs this question. If it is customary for the hired party to supply the materials, this custom is incorporated into the contract. If custom is disputed or ambiguous, the contract must explicitly specify who provides the materials; failure to do so renders the contract void for gharar. This applies to dhimma hire; in ʿayn hire only the labor is owed regardless.

3. Physician Liability (Ḍamān al-Ṭabīb)

A skilled physician (ṭabīb māhir) who treats a patient in accordance with his skill owes no liability for failure to cure — the contracted benefit is the treatment itself, not the cure. The physician earns the agreed wage even if the patient does not recover.

If cure is stipulated as a condition: the contract is void (fāsid), because it makes the wage contingent on an outcome outside the physician's control.

Alternative: Jiʿāla on recovery. A jiʿāla ("I will pay you X if I recover") is valid — payment only upon recovery, and no gharar problem arises.

Unskilled physician: An unskilled practitioner who causes harm bears full liability (ḍamān), earns no wage, and must return the cost of any medications provided.

4. Landlord vs. Tenant Obligations

Landlord's Duties

  • Deliver the key (taslīm al-miftāḥ) and enable access.
  • Undertake major repairs that enable the contracted use.
  • Clear snow from the outer roof (saqf khārij / jamlun) if relevant.
  • Reclaim the property from usurpers; defend against fire or plundering.
  • Deliver with the cesspit (bāliʿa) empty.
  • Note: the landlord cannot be compelled to repair, but if he refuses, the tenant acquires a right of cancellation (khiyār).

Tenant's Duties

  • Clear snow from the courtyard (fināʾ) and the usable roof (saqf yustaʿmal).
  • Remove ordinary refuse (qumāma ʿādiyya).
  • Clean the cesspit during the period of occupancy.
  • Wind-blown soil: neither party is responsible.

5. Q&A

Lending to a Riba Debtor

Question: Is it permissible to lend money to a person who intends to open another interest-bearing bank account with the borrowed money?

Ruling: Not permissible. Lending to someone who will use the loan to facilitate a clear and ongoing sin constitutes assistance in that sin (iʿāna ʿalā al-maʿṣiya), which is itself prohibited.

Talfīq Across Separate Acts: Witr as One Rakʿa After Shortened ʿIshāʾ

Question (Tashfeen Ekram): A traveler performs ʿIshāʾ shortened to two rakʿas following the Ḥanafī madhhab, then performs witr as a single rakʿa following the Shāfiʿī madhhab. Is this talfīq permissible?

Discussion: This appears permissible, because the two acts are separate — each with its own beginning, intention, and completion. The problematic talfīq is combining two madhhabs in a single act such that the act would be invalid under both. Here each act was completed fully under one school — analogous to performing wuḍūʾ under the Ḥanafī school then praying under the Shāfiʿī school.

Woman Switching Madhhabs Mid-Cycle for Menstrual Rulings

Question: A woman follows the Shāfiʿī madhhab and therefore treats days 1–10 as menstruation (ḥayḍ). On day 11, she switches to the Ḥanafī madhhab. Does she owe makeup prayers (qaḍāʾ) for the days she omitted prayers under the Shāfiʿī ruling?

Ruling: No makeup prayers are owed — she was legitimately following a recognized legal opinion at the time. The two rulings operate on non-overlapping periods; she validly omitted prayers for days 1–10 under the Shāfiʿī school, and validly resumes from day 11 under the Ḥanafī school.
