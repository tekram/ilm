Guarantee and Suretyship Formulations - June 26, 2025

📅 2025-06-26 🕐 17:00

Comprehensive Summary of Islamic Jurisprudence Lessons on Guarantee (Dhaman), Suretyship (Kafalah), Debt, and Repayment

1. Definitions and Fundamental Concepts

  • Guarantee (Dhaman): A contractual commitment involving money or debt, where the guarantor assumes responsibility for another's financial obligation.
  • Suretyship (Kafalah): A commitment concerning a person or object, such as guaranteeing the presence or delivery of someone or something.
  • Guarantee generally applies to monetary obligations; suretyship applies to persons or specific items.
  • Guarantee is a joining of one liability to another, distinct from a pledge (rahn), which involves an asset as security.

2. Conditions and Validity of Guarantee and Suretyship Contracts

  • Verbal Expression (Lafz): A clear, explicit statement of guarantee or suretyship is required to establish a binding contract.
  • Common explicit phrases:
  • "I guarantee you" (ضمنتك)
  • "Your debt is on him" (دينك عليه)
  • "I took responsibility for him" (تحملتُه)
  • "I am surety for so-and-so" (تكفلت ببدني لفلان)
  • Implied or Indirect Expressions (Kinayah): May be valid if the intention and context clearly indicate guarantee, but explicit wording is preferred.
  • Promises vs. Contracts: Imperfect verb forms (e.g., "I will pay") are considered promises, not binding contracts unless accompanied by clear commitment or intention.
  • Ambiguity (Iham): Should be resolved in favor of establishing the guarantee if possible, considering the speaker's intention.
  • Prohibition of Conditional Withdrawal: Guarantee cannot be conditioned on options or delays that allow unilateral revocation without creditor consent.
  • Timing: Guarantee can be immediate or deferred but cannot be conditional on uncertain future events.

3. Evidences and Scholarly Opinions

  • Classical scholars such as Ibn Hajar, Al-Adra'i, Al-Balqini, Al-Mutawalli, and Ibn Taymiyyah emphasize:
  • Necessity of clear wording and intention.
  • Guarantee is a voluntary commitment (tabarru') invoked by necessity.
  • Guarantee is generally indivisible; debt remains singular even with multiple guarantors.
  • Release of the guarantor does not automatically release the principal debtor, but release of the debtor releases the guarantor.
  • Differences exist regarding:
  • Whether guarantors share liability proportionally or each is liable for the entire debt.
  • The validity of implied guarantees.
  • The effect of joint mortgage (rahn) on guarantee division.

4. Guarantee and Multiple Guarantors

  • When multiple guarantors exist:
  • The debt remains one and indivisible.
  • Each guarantor is liable for the entire debt, but practically the debt is divided according to shares or agreement.
  • Creditors should not demand full payment from all guarantors simultaneously.
  • If one guarantor pays, the others' liability reduces accordingly.
  • Joint mortgage on shared property covers the entire debt jointly, not just individual shares.
  • Scholars differ on whether guarantee can be divided proportionally or must be treated as indivisible.

5. Guarantee and Debt Postponement

  • Guarantee can be immediate or deferred to the debt's due date.
  • Extending the debt's due date after guarantee does not release the guarantor; liability follows the new due date.
  • The guarantor is not obligated to accelerate payment unless the debtor agrees.
  • Death of the principal debtor before the earliest due date delays guarantor's liability; after that date, guarantor's obligation becomes immediate.
  • Guarantor and debtor (or heirs) can be jointly or separately demanded to pay.

6. Rights and Obligations of Guarantor and Principal Debtor

  • Guarantor who pays the debt with the principal's permission has the right to reclaim the amount.
  • Payment without permission generally does not grant the right to reclaim.
  • If payment is made from zakat funds allocated for debtors (sahm al-gharimin), reclaiming is not allowed.
  • Guarantor cannot detain debtor's property unlawfully; detention is only permissible to ensure payment.
  • Guarantor cannot demand payment from debtor before creditor demands payment from guarantor.
  • Release (ibra') of the guarantor does not release the debtor; release of the debtor releases the guarantor.
  • Guarantor's liability continues until debt is fully discharged or lawfully terminated.

7. Debt Payment, Intention, and Reclaiming

  • Intention (Niyyah): Essential for valid payment of debt; payment without intention to discharge debt is invalid.
  • Permission and Conditions:
  • If permission to pay is granted with condition of return, payer can reclaim payment.
  • Absolute permission without condition means payment is a gift; no reclaim allowed.
  • Difference Between Settlement and Sale:
  • Settlement usually involves forgiving part of the debt; payer cannot reclaim forgiven portion.
  • Sale involves exchange; if sold for less, payer returns the lesser amount.
  • Payment Without Guarantee or Permission: No right to reclaim unless under compulsion or necessity.
  • Witnessing Payment:
  • Payment should be witnessed by two just persons or equivalent testimony to be valid.
  • Without witnesses or debtor's acknowledgment, guarantor remains liable.
  • Disputes: Evidence and testimony are crucial to confirm payment and discharge guarantee.

8. Joint Mortgage (Rahn) and Guarantee

  • Joint mortgage on shared property covers the entire debt jointly.
  • Guarantee is generally indivisible; creditor cannot claim full debt from one co-owner exclusively.
  • Release of principal debtor releases guarantor; release of guarantor does not release debtor.
  • Guarantor who pays has right to claim reimbursement but cannot imprison debtor; must seek judicial remedy.
  • Scholars differ on division of guarantee liability; final ruling depends on evidence and judicial discretion.

9. Practical Action Items

  • Use clear, explicit language when entering guarantee or suretyship contracts.
  • Avoid ambiguous or conditional statements that may invalidate guarantee.
  • Clarify whether guarantee is immediate or deferred; document any extensions of debt due date.
  • When multiple guarantors exist, specify shares and avoid joint full liability claims.
  • Obtain permission and clear terms before paying debt on behalf of others to secure right of reimbursement.
  • Always witness payments or obtain debtor's acknowledgment to protect guarantor's liability.
  • Document any release (ibra') of guarantors or debtors clearly.
  • Avoid unlawful detention of debtor's property; follow legal procedures.
  • Consider local customs ('urf) in financial dealings to determine rights and obligations.
  • Heirs of deceased debtors or guarantors should be informed of liabilities and rights.

10. Summary of Key Jurisprudential Principles

  • Guarantee and suretyship require clear commitment and intention.
  • Debt and guarantee are indivisible; multiple guarantors share liability but debt remains singular.
  • Guarantee is a voluntary commitment and cannot be revoked unilaterally once established.
  • Payment of debt requires intention and preferably witnesses.
  • Release of debtor releases guarantor; release of guarantor alone does not release debtor.
  • Guarantor who pays with permission can reclaim; without permission, no reclaim.
  • Joint mortgage covers entire debt; guarantee liability division is subject to scholarly differences.
  • Ambiguity in wording is resolved in favor of establishing guarantee if intention is clear.
