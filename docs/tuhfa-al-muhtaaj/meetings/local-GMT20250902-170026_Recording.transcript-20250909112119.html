## Sale of Land with Trees and Water Rights - September 02, 2025

Start: 2025-09-02T17:00:26

Comprehensive Summary of Islamic Fiqh Lessons on Property Sales, Impurity, Intercession, and Related Issues

1. Sale of Land with Trees, Water, and Related Rights

Case and Issue

  • Sale of land including trees, stones, and a water spring ("ain"), with small palm trees ("nakhil sagheer") explicitly excluded.
  • Seller wished to irrigate excluded small palms using spring water sold to buyer.
  • Question: Does buyer have the right to prevent seller’s use of spring water for excluded trees?

Key Rulings

  • Water spring sale validity: Must include existing water at contract time; mixing old and new water ownership is invalid.
  • Once sold, seller loses all rights to spring water.
  • Buyer can prevent seller from using spring water, even for excluded small palms.
  • Irrigation without buyer’s permission is trespassing ("dham").
  • Exclusion of trees does not imply exclusion of water rights if water is sold.

Definitions

  • Small Palm Trees (Nakhil Sagheer): Young palms not yet separated or independently planted.
  • Water Spring (Ain): Natural water source with existing water at sale time.

Evidences and Opinions

  • Author states seller loses rights if sale conditions met.
  • Ifada al-Hadramiyya allows seller access to excluded trees if no other access exists, by analogy to reserved house rooms.
  • For unknown water quantities, claimant must prove usage or swear oath.

Practical Actions

  • Explicitly state inclusion of water present at sale.
  • Seller cannot irrigate excluded trees with buyer’s water without permission.
  • Seller may have right of passage to excluded trees if no other access.
  • In disputes, claimant must provide evidence or oath.

2. Sale of Property Surrounded by Seller’s or Others’ Land (Right of Passage)

Core Issue

  • Validity of sale when property is completely surrounded by seller’s or third-party land without access.

Definitions and Conditions

  • Surrounded Property (محفوفة): Enclosed on all sides by seller’s or others’ land.
  • Right of Passage (ممر): Legal access route for buyer.

Five Cases of Passage Rights

1. Seller grants passage from all sides → Sale valid.

2. Seller grants passage from one unspecified side → Sale invalid.

3. Seller sells "with its rights" (بحقوقها) → Sale valid, passage from all sides.

4. Seller silent on passage → Sale valid; passage presumed except if adjacent to buyer’s land or street.

5. Seller denies passage → Sale invalid.

Additional Points

  • If property borders buyer’s land or street, passage limited to those borders.
  • No access at sale time → Sale invalid, even if passage can be created later.
  • Seller reserving part of property (e.g., a room) retains passage rights if no other access.
  • Surrounded by third-party land with no access → Sale invalid.

Evidences

  • Classical fiqh texts (تحفة, الأنوار) emphasize necessity of access.
  • Ambiguity in passage invalidates sale.

Practical Actions

  • Specify buyer’s passage rights explicitly.
  • Avoid selling inaccessible property.
  • Secure passage rights before sale if surrounded by third-party land.
  • Clarify passage rights when reserving parts of property.

3. Sale of Ambiguous Property and Right of Passage (Mawarr)

  • Ambiguous or unspecified property sales are invalid until passage is defined.
  • Passage from one side must be specified; otherwise, sale void.
  • Selling part of a house grants passage unless explicitly excluded.
  • Buyer’s passage depends on adjacency to street or buyer’s land.
  • Clear passage rights are essential for valid sale.

4. Impurity (Najasa) and Its Effects on Food and Animals

Watermelon Irrigated with Urine

  • Permissible to eat watermelon watered with urine if no impurity taste or smell.
  • If taste/smell present, some say disliked, others say prohibited.
  • Watermelon acts as filter preventing impurity transfer.

Concept of Jallalah (Impurity Transfer by Feeding/Nursing)

  • Animals fed on impurity are permissible but disliked (makruh tanziih) by dominant opinion.
  • Some scholars consider such animals prohibited.
  • Fruits do not transmit impurity taste/smell; if they do, eating is disliked.

Impurity in Agricultural Soil and Crops

  • Crops grown on impure soil generally permissible unless impurity taste/smell present.
  • Use of animal dung and urine in construction/agriculture is permissible.
  • Washing/cooking does not remove impurity taste; feeding on pure substances required.

Waiting Period for Animals Fed on Impurity

  • Camels: 40 days; Cattle: 30 days; Sheep/Chickens: 7 days.
  • Ensures impurity taste removed before slaughter.

Feeding Animals on Haram Substances

  • Animals fed haram substances remain permissible to eat.
  • Prohibition applies to feeding act, not animal meat.

Practical Actions

  • Eating produce watered with impurity allowed if no taste/smell.
  • Observe waiting periods for animals fed on impurity.
  • Use of dung in construction/agriculture allowed.
  • Avoid relying on washing/cooking to remove impurity taste.

5. Liability for Consuming Others’ Food and Mosque Construction Materials

Liability for Consuming Others’ Food

  • Consuming another’s food without permission is haram.
  • Consumer liable to compensate owner.
  • Debt remains until paid; estate cannot be sold or slaughtered before debt settled.

Mosque Construction with Impure Materials

  • Building mosques with impure materials (e.g., mud mixed with impurities) generally prohibited.
  • Some scholars allow under necessity but consider it disliked.
  • Prayer on impure surfaces is disliked or invalid; barriers required.
  • Impure materials should be removed or purified.

Purification of Impure Materials

  • Cooking/washing may purify some impurities.
  • Severe impurities (dog/pig excrement) require washing.
  • Impurities mixed in mud/bricks remain impure if not purified.

Practical Actions

  • Compensate owners if consuming their property unlawfully.
  • Avoid building mosques with impure materials.
  • Purify or remove impure materials in religious spaces.
  • Prevent prayer on impure surfaces.

6. Managing Impurity in Mosques and Permissibility of Tawassul and Istighatha

Impurity Management in Mosques

  • Spreading impurity inside mosques is forbidden.
  • Clay/mud mixed with impurities is impermissible inside mosques.
  • Washing required for heavy impurity contamination.
  • Some impurities mixed with water may be excused.

Tawassul and Istighatha Explained

  • Tawassul: Seeking Allah’s help through a means (e.g., Prophet’s status).
  • Istighatha: Directly seeking help from the Prophet or righteous people.
  • Permissible to say phrases like "O Allah, fulfill my need through Prophet Muhammad."
  • Addressing Prophet or saints is asking them to intercede with Allah, not worship.
  • Misbelief arises only if divine power is attributed to them.

Evidences

  • Historical practice of companions and scholars.
  • Sheikh Nabhani’s works on Istighatha.
  • Qur’anic monotheism and hadith emphasizing Allah as sole source.

Practical Actions

  • Avoid spreading impurity in mosques.
  • Wash impure objects thoroughly.
  • Seek intercession properly, maintaining Tawhid.
  • Educate on correct understanding of tawassul and istighatha.

7. Seeking Help from the Prophet (Istighatha) and Related Issues

Definitions

  • Istighatha: Asking Prophet for help/intercession.
  • Tawassul: Seeking means to Allah.

Scholarly Opinions

  • Permissible to address Prophet directly as intercessor.
  • Some caution against misunderstanding leading to shirk.
  • Figurative speech (majaz ‘aqli) explains phrases like "O Prophet, heal me."

Differences of Opinion

  • Scholars permit such expressions if understood correctly.
  • Laypeople advised to avoid ambiguous phrases.
  • Declaring users of such phrases as disbelievers is a grave error.

Practical Actions

  • Laypeople should ask Allah directly or seek intercession clearly.
  • Scholars should educate on distinctions.
  • Avoid takfir over such issues.
  • Maintain proper etiquette in supplication.

8. Devotional Expressions, Wahhabi Impact, and Theological Clarifications

Key Points

  • Devotional poetry and phrases often contain implied meanings.
  • Wahhabi critiques led to clarifications to avoid accusations of shirk.
  • Stars ("naw’") do not cause rain; Allah alone causes it.
  • Intercession is spiritual, not natural causation.
  • Laypeople advised to avoid ambiguous or misleading phrases.

Evidences

  • Quranic injunctions against ambiguous speech.
  • Scholarly consensus on intercession and monotheism.

Practical Actions

  • Explain devotional phrases to prevent misunderstanding.
  • Avoid attributing natural phenomena to created beings.
  • Educate public on tawhid and intercession.

9. Euphemisms, Ambiguity in Speech, and Spiritual Realities

Issues

  • Dislike of euphemisms for sons/daughters linked to pagan customs.
  • Ambiguous requests for intercession discouraged.
  • Ignorance among laypeople causes inappropriate expressions.
  • Distinction between spiritual realities (karamat) and legal proof emphasized.

Scholarly References

  • Sheikh Fadl, Sheikh Muhammad Ba’udan, and stories illustrating limits of legal evidence.
  • Affirmation of seeking help from Prophet and saints as legitimate.

Practical Actions

  • Avoid ambiguous or euphemistic language.
  • Clarify intercession requests respecting Prophet’s status.
  • Educate community on spiritual vs. legal matters.
  • Uphold authentic Islamic teachings without extremes.

Overall Practical Recommendations

  • When selling land or property, explicitly state inclusion/exclusion of water, trees, and passage rights.
  • Ensure buyer has clear, accessible right of passage at sale time.
  • Seller cannot use sold water without buyer’s permission.
  • Compensate owners if consuming their property unlawfully.
  • Avoid building mosques with impure materials; purify or remove impurities.
  • Manage impurity in mosques carefully; prevent prayer on impure surfaces.
  • Seek intercession through Prophet and saints properly, maintaining Tawhid.
  • Educate public on distinctions between tawassul, istighatha, and shirk.
  • Avoid ambiguous or euphemistic language that may cause misunderstanding.
  • Respect spiritual realities while adhering to legal evidences and Islamic law.
