Comprehensive Summary of Islamic Jurisprudence Lessons on Agency (Wakalah), Oaths, Liability, and Related Financial Transactions

1. Fundamental Concepts and Definitions

  • Agent (Wakil): Authorized person acting on behalf of the principal.
  • Principal (Muwakkil): Person delegating authority to the agent.
  • Compensation (Ju‘r/Wajl): Fee or reward for the agent’s service.
  • Oath (Yamin): Sworn statement used as evidence.
  • Evidence (Bayyina/Burhān): Proof such as witnesses, confession, or documentation.
  • Trustee (Wadi‘): Person entrusted with property, liable under specific conditions.
  • Usurper (Ghasib): Person who wrongfully seizes property, always liable for damages.
  • Guardian (Qayyim): Judicially appointed custodian of orphans or incapacitated persons.

2. Agency Disputes: Credibility, Liability, and Evidence

#### a. Disputes Over Authorized Transactions

  • If the agent claims to have performed a transaction (e.g., sale or debt payment) but the principal denies it:
  • The principal’s oath is accepted by default, as the agent must prove performance.
  • The agent can establish proof via witnesses or swearing an oath.
  • During the agency period, the agent is considered trustworthy (amin), so their claims about loss or return are accepted on oath.
  • After dismissal (azl), the principal’s statements and oaths take precedence; the agent’s claims are generally rejected unless supported by evidence.

#### b. Agent’s Liability for Loss or Damage

  • The agent’s claim of loss or damage to entrusted property is accepted on oath, relieving them of liability unless proven otherwise.
  • If the agent denies receipt or sale, the principal’s oath is accepted, and the agent is liable for breach of trust.
  • Liability is stricter if the property is mortgaged or rented, as rights are attached to the property itself.
  • Usurpers (ghasib) are always liable for damages regardless of claims.

#### c. Agent’s Right to Compensation (Ju‘r)

  • The agent is entitled to compensation only upon proving performance of the authorized transaction.
  • The agent’s oath can establish entitlement to the stipulated fee.
  • Without proof, the agent’s claim for compensation is invalid.

3. Oaths and Evidentiary Standards

  • An agent’s oath denying delivery or performance acts as a confession, clearing them of debt liability.
  • Claims for fees or denial of debts require evidence, typically two witnesses or one witness plus oath.
  • After agency termination, the agent’s oath or acknowledgment is binding; the principal cannot reclaim debts if the agent has cleared themselves by oath.
  • Oaths are critical in resolving disputes between agent and principal.

4. Acceptance of Statements Before and After Agency Termination

  • Before dismissal: Agent’s statements about returning property or loss are accepted.
  • After dismissal: Majority opinion rejects agent’s claims unless supported by evidence; some scholars accept claims if uncontradicted.
  • The principal is not obligated to believe the agent’s claims post-dismissal without proof.
  • Claims of returning property to the principal or their representative require witnesses or testimony.

5. Disputes Involving Sale Transactions

  • When the agent sells property and the principal disputes the sale price:
  • The buyer’s testimony is generally accepted over the principal’s, assuming no proof of agent’s betrayal.
  • Delivery of goods before payment implies consent to the sale; if goods are not delivered, the principal’s denial of payment is accepted.
  • The agent must provide evidence of payment receipt; otherwise, the principal’s denial stands.
  • Guardians (qayyim) and judges have special authority in managing others’ affairs, and their financial dealings require evidence.

6. Agency, Custodianship, and Payment of Debts

  • Payment to a custodian or third party requires proof of agency to discharge the debtor.
  • Without proof, payment to a custodian does not absolve the debtor.
  • Oaths and evidence determine whether payment is binding.
  • Guardians of orphans must provide evidence when claiming payment on behalf of the orphan.
  • Claims of spending on orphans or debts transferred require verification.

7. Liability Differences Among Trustees

| Trustee Type | Claim of Loss Accepted on Oath? | Liability for Loss/Damage |

|-----------------------|---------------------------------|----------------------------------|

| Agent (Wakil) | Yes | Not liable if loss proven by oath |

| Custodian (Wadi‘) | Yes | Not liable if loss proven by oath |

| Usurper (Ghasib) | No | Always liable for damages |

| Borrower/Lessee | No | Liable for safety of property |

| Mortgagor/Lessee | No | Claims not accepted without proof |

8. Returning Money to Orphans and Related Issues

  • Returning money to a mature orphan requires proof; the orphan must provide evidence of entitlement.
  • Verbal claims are accepted only from trusted persons (partners, agents), not from wrongdoers or borrowers.
  • Payment to an agent requires proof of agency; otherwise, the holder is not obligated to pay.
  • Debt transfers (hawala) require debtor acknowledgment.
  • Ownership transfer in sales requires possession; selling goods before possession is invalid.

9. Practical Action Points

  • Agents:
  • Maintain witnesses and documentation to prove performance and entitlement to fees.
  • Avoid breaches of trust to preserve credibility.
  • Provide evidence when claiming loss or return of property.
  • Principals:
  • Do not accept agent’s claims without evidence, especially after dismissal.
  • Require witnesses or documentation for transactions and payments.
  • Use oaths strategically to resolve disputes.
  • Guardians and Custodians:
  • Provide clear proof when claiming payments or managing others’ property.
  • Avoid incurring debts without authorization.
  • All Parties:
  • Understand that oaths denying delivery or debt act as confessions.
  • Recognize differing liabilities among trustees.
  • Ensure possession before transferring ownership in sales.
  • Verify agency authorization before accepting payments or claims.

10. Islamic Legal Principles and Evidences

  • The rulings align with Qur’anic injunctions on trust and justice (e.g., Surah An-Nisa 4:58).
  • The acceptance of oaths and evidence is grounded in Prophetic traditions and classical fiqh.
  • The principle that an oath denying delivery is a confession is a key legal maxim.
  • Scholarly opinions vary on acceptance of agent’s claims post-dismissal, reflecting jurisprudential diversity.

Conclusion

These lessons clarify the balance between trust in agents and protection of principals’ rights in Islamic law. They emphasize the critical role of evidence, oaths, and clear authorization in agency disputes, liability for entrusted property, and financial transactions. Adherence to these principles safeguards justice and prevents conflicts in commercial and fiduciary relationships.
