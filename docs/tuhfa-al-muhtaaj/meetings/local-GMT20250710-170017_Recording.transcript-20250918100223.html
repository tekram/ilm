Commercial Partnership Rules - July 10, 2025

📅 2025-07-10 🕐 17:00

Partnership (sharika) — Core definitions and principles

  • A partnership (mushārakah) does not require equal capital contributions. Partners may contribute unequal sums and the partnership remains valid.
  • Default rule: profit and loss follow the proportion of each partner’s capital (monetary value), unless the partners validly agree otherwise.
  • Partners are mutual agents (wakīl) for partnership affairs; agency (wakāla) rules generally apply to their acts, authority, revocation, and termination.

Profit and loss — key rulings

  • Profit and loss are apportioned pro rata to capital contributed, not by headcount or necessarily by amount of work, unless an express valid agreement provides otherwise.
  • It is not required that exact amounts be known at contract formation if proportions or a method to determine amounts can be ascertained later (e.g., weighing/counting, agent reporting).
  • A stipulation that contradicts the capital-proportion principle (for example, fixing equal profit despite unequal capitals) can render the contract defective or void (fadāḍ). Remedies in such a case:
  • Return of capitals to owners; and
  • Compensation to the managing/working partner for labor (ujrat al‑mithl) where appropriate.

Agency, authority, and limits of a partner

  • Each partner may act for the partnership within the ordinary scope of partnership business; valid acts bind the partnership to the extent of the acting partner’s authority/share.
  • Acts beyond a partner’s authority:
  • May be valid only as to that partner’s share; or
  • May sever the partnership with respect to the asset transacted, creating separate relations between buyer and remaining partners.
  • Explicit authorization is required for risky, exceptional, or unusual undertakings (e.g., taking partnership goods on a sea voyage, guaranteeing exchange‑rates, selling on credit). Vague or overly broad phrases (“do as you wish”) are legally problematic.
  • Prohibited or restricted dispositions include selling on credit or using illiquid/non-local currency without consent if that harms other partners; grossly unfair sales (ghubn fāḥish) are generally not binding on the other partners and may be valid only for the acting partner’s share.

Custody, ownership kinds, and evidentiary rules

  • Possession (yad) of partnership property is treated as a trust (amāna). The partner in custody has strong evidentiary standing.
  • The holder’s statement or oath about possession is given weight; possession creates probative effect in disputes.
  • A purchase made by a partner creates an independent contractual right in what he bought (distinct from heirs’ indivisible inheritance shares). Thus a partner‑purchaser may create separable ownership rights by his acquisition.
  • In disputes over loss (talf):
  • For apparent causes (e.g., visible fire) the claimant should prove the apparent event; then an oath may be accepted.
  • For hidden causes (e.g., theft), an oath and corroboration may suffice where appropriate.
  • Mixing unlawfully seized funds with lawful funds requires separation (afirāz) and accounting; rightful owners may reclaim their portions.

Dissolution, incapacity, death, and revocation

  • Partnership/agency is revocable and generally dissolves upon death, insanity, prolonged unconsciousness, bankruptcy (fālūs, as applicable), or other loss of legal capacity.
  • Practical outcomes when a partner dies:
  • Heirs may accept continuation (imtamām), demand return of capital, or renew/recontract; many jurists treat heirs’ continuation with the assent of other partners as continuation of the original contract.
  • There is jurisprudential variation: the majority treat death itself as terminating agency/partnership even without notice; some Maliki/Hanbali reports require knowledge of death for revocation to take effect.
  • A unilateral revocation by a partner dissolves partnership rights under agency principles; procedure and effects should be specified contractually to avoid dispute.

Special practical scenarios

  • Unauthorized delivery of partnership assets (e.g., giving tenants property, emigrating with a share) exceeds authority; assets taken without consent generally sever partnership rights over those assets.
  • If one partner converts joint money to merchandise or entrusts it to third parties without consent, the conversion may be valid in form but the acting partner bears liability (daman) for any resulting loss.
  • Arrangements where parties contribute heterogeneous inputs (land + seed + tools + labor) require careful structuring:
  • If there is no mixing of capital and no clear partnership terms, the arrangement may be treated as wages (ujrah) or separate ownership of inputs rather than a profit‑sharing mushārakah.
  • To be treated as mushārakah, capital mixing or explicit partnership terms must be established.

Dealing in foreign currency and exchange‑rate guarantees

  • Trading/investing in a foreign currency is permissible if agreed and practically usable; however:
  • Fixing a future return in a foreign currency at today’s exchange rate (an exchange‑rate guarantee) shifts risk to the manager/worker and may be impermissible or legally problematic (resembles gharār/speculation or an unauthorized guarantee).
  • Local statutory restrictions on currency use may render such arrangements unenforceable; confirm applicable state law and convert to usable currency when necessary.
  • Operators should avoid accepting guaranteed exchange‑rate obligations unless fully hedged and contractually clarified.

Guardianship / protected persons (maḥjūr `alayh) — special caution

  • Disposition of a guardian/protected person’s immovable property requires a higher standard of maṣlaḥah (clear necessity or substantial benefit), not mere probability of benefit.
  • General travel permission does not automatically authorize taking a protected person’s money on a risky voyage; customary commercial practice (ahl al‑naj‘ah) may permit taking capital on journeys in some trades.

Differences of opinion (brief)

  • Capital equality: majority/authoritative juristic view permits unequal capitals; profit follows capital proportion — equality is not a condition for validity.
  • Death/incapacity: majority treat death as terminating agency/partnership; some Maliki/Hanbali positions emphasize knowledge-of-death for termination.
  • Treatment of clauses contradictory to essential partnership rules: majority hold such stipulations may render the contract defective; remedies differ (return of capitals, wage to worker).
  • Minor madhhab differences exist on combining salary with profit share, heirs’ rights, and procedural matters — specify governing school/position if important.

Practical action items / drafting checklist

  • Record in writing:
  • Each partner’s capital (or a clear method to determine it later) and the agreed profit‑sharing ratio.
  • Explicit scope of authority for each partner (e.g., permission to sell on credit, to travel, to use partnership goods on sea voyages, to convert currency).
  • Whether any partner receives an extra share for labor and how that is calculated.
  • How taxes, customs, court costs, and recovery expenses are allocated (default: pro rata to capital).
  • Consequences and procedure for unilateral revocation, death, incapacity, bankruptcy, and heirs’ continuation or withdrawal.
  • Avoid vague powers or open‑ended delegations; use clear, specific language for risky activities (sea travel, foreign exchange guarantees, credit sales).
  • If accepting foreign‑currency arrangements:
  • Avoid guaranteed future exchange rates unless contractually and legally safe; prefer payments in usable/local currency or include explicit risk allocation and hedging measures.
  • Maintain custody and accounting records and receipts; possession is strong evidence in disputes.
  • For mixed‑input ventures (land/seed/tools/labor), explicitly state whether the arrangement is a partnership or a set of hire/loan arrangements; if intended as mushārakah, ensure capital mixing or clear profit‑sharing terms.
  • In case of dispute, document contemporaneous intentions; be aware that agency rules and evidentiary principles (holder’s oath, possession) strongly affect outcomes. Seek juristic/legal counsel for complex or cross‑jurisdictional currency issues.
