Contractual Guarantees and Obligations in Sales - June 12, 2025

Comprehensive Summary of Islamic Jurisprudence Lessons on Debt, Guarantee, Discharge, Wage, Contracts, and Speech Etiquette

1. Conditions of Debt Valid for Guarantee (Suretyship) in Islamic Law

Key Definitions

  • Necessary (Lazim) Debt: A debt the debtor cannot revoke or annul arbitrarily.
  • Stable (Mustaqir): Cannot be revoked or nullified by any means.
  • Unstable (Ghair Mustaqir): Cannot be revoked by debtor but may cease due to external reasons (e.g., defect, loss).
  • Examples:
  • Price before delivery (unstable necessary debt).
  • Dowry before consummation (unstable necessary debt).
  • Price after delivery (stable necessary debt).
  • Dowry after consummation (stable necessary debt).

Main Rulings

  • Guarantee (kafalah) is valid only on necessary debts.
  • Guarantee on unstable necessary debts (e.g., price during option period, dowry before consummation) is valid but may be nullified if conditions change.
  • Guarantee on stable necessary debts (e.g., price after delivery, dowry after consummation) is firmly valid.
  • Guarantee is invalid on non-debt obligations or debts that can be waived by debtor.
  • Guarantee differs from assignment (hawala); guarantee requires debt necessity, assignment does not.

Evidences and Scholarly Opinions

  • Classical scholars emphasize necessity as a condition for guarantee.
  • Some allow guarantee on debts with unknown attributes if the debt is established; others reject this.
  • Views extending guarantee beyond necessary debts are considered weak.

Practical Actions

  • Verify debt is necessary before guaranteeing.
  • Distinguish between stable and unstable necessary debts.
  • Avoid guaranteeing uncertain or non-debt obligations.
  • Recognize guarantee validity during buyer's option period or before dowry consummation.

2. Guarantee and Mortgage in Employment and Work-Related Debts

Key Concepts

  • Debts owed by employer to worker can be waived by worker; guarantee on such debts is invalid.
  • Guarantee on debts owed by worker to employer or third parties is valid.
  • Guarantee on price during buyer's option period is valid (debt will become binding).
  • Guarantee on reward (ja'alah) before work completion is invalid.
  • Guarantee and mortgage valid only after debt becomes fixed and necessary.

Evidences

  • Guarantee depends on debt necessity.
  • Price during option period is necessary after option expires.
  • Reward before completion is neither necessary nor settled.

Practical Actions

  • Guarantee only known, fixed debts.
  • Avoid guaranteeing waivable debts.
  • Guarantee price during option period permissible.
  • Guarantee reward before completion invalid.

3. Discharge of Debt (Ibraa) and Related Transactions

Key Rulings

  • Discharge must be explicit and based on knowledge of debt amount, kind, and condition.
  • Discharge from unknown or uncertain debts is invalid in worldly matters but may be valid spiritually.
  • Discharge transfers ownership of debt from creditor to debtor.
  • Conditional or temporary discharge invalid if debt unknown.
  • Discharge by proxy valid if creditor knows debt.
  • Ignorance invalidates discharge due to lack of consent.

Evidences and Scholarly Opinions

  • Ibn Hajar and others rule discharge from unknown debt invalid for worldly matters.
  • Scholars differentiate between discharge (requires knowledge) and vows (nadr) which may be valid without exact knowledge.

Practical Examples

  • Discharging debt without knowing amount is invalid.
  • Employment without clear wage agreement may be considered gift, not contract.
  • Taxi fares based on meter accepted as customary but require clarity.

Practical Actions

  • Ensure clarity and knowledge before discharge.
  • Avoid vague or unknown debt discharge.
  • Clarify wage terms in employment contracts.
  • Seek judicial guidance in uncertain cases.

4. Wage (Ujrah) in Transportation and Service Contracts

Key Issues

  • Validity of wage contracts without explicit agreement.
  • Role of custom (al-'adah) vs. explicit contract.
  • Complexity of wages based on distance, time, and traffic.

Rulings

  • Without explicit agreement, no binding wage due (Shafi'i view).
  • Customary payment is considered voluntary favor (ihsan), not contract.
  • Combining distance and time in one fare without clear terms causes uncertainty (gharar), invalidating contract.
  • Fixed wage per kilometer or per hour separately is acceptable.
  • Initial fare (e.g., door opening) is valid small wage.
  • Invalid contracts do not obligate payment beyond what was given.
  • Judges may assign fair wage if dispute arises.

Practical Actions

  • Specify wage clearly (per km or per hour).
  • Avoid ambiguous contracts dependent on unpredictable factors.
  • Treat payments without contract as favors.
  • Formal business contracts must be clear and documented.
  • Repent for unclear or invalid dealings.

5. Intention in Contracts and Backbiting (Ghibah)

Intention in Contracts

  • Contract validity depends on explicit wording, not hidden intentions (Shafi'i position).
  • Formal contracts (banks, companies) are binding as written.
  • Hidden intentions cannot override explicit terms.

Backbiting (Ghibah)

  • Speaking ill of someone behind their back about faults they dislike.
  • If backbiting does not reach the person: repentance requires regret and seeking forgiveness.
  • If it reaches the person: must explicitly retract statement before repentance accepted.
  • Seeking forgiveness is recommended or obligatory according to scholars.
  • Backbiting minors is less clear; emphasis on awareness.

Criticism vs. Backbiting

  • Criticizing opinions or fatwas is not backbiting if focused on ideas, not persons.
  • Personal attacks constitute backbiting.
  • Avoid labeling scholars unjust without certainty.

Slander (Buhtan)

  • Falsely attributing faults is more severe than backbiting.
  • Requires repentance and possibly legal consequences.

Practical Actions

  • Rely on contract wording; avoid hidden intention claims.
  • Repent sincerely if backbiting unknowingly.
  • Retract statements if backbiting is known to the person.
  • Critique ideas respectfully, avoid personal attacks.
  • Avoid slander and false accusations.

6. Speech Etiquette, Guarantees, and Avoiding Harmful Talk

Backbiting and Limits

  • Avoid speaking ill of absent persons except for sincere advice or clarification.
  • Repent and retract if backbiting occurs.
  • Warning about harm (e.g., spouse impurity) is permissible.

Guarantees and Liability

  • Guaranteeing unknown items (e.g., water quality) is valid.
  • Guarantee covering a range (e.g., 1 to 10) covers entire range; scholars differ on upper limit.
  • Guarantee depends on original debtor's liability; guarantor not liable if debtor dies with debt unpaid.
  • Releasing debtor based on assumption about guarantor is invalid.

Speech Etiquette

  • Avoid excessive talk about others.
  • Praise in absence allowed; speaking ill forbidden regardless of reaction.
  • Be cautious even with trusted confidants.
  • Avoid discussing known faults; it remains backbiting.
  • Mentally detach from grudges to prevent backbiting.

Practical Actions

  • Speak about others only within limits of sincere advice.
  • Retract and repent for backbiting.
  • Understand guarantee conditions and limits.
  • Avoid fueling disputes or repeating grievances.
  • Maintain fairness and justice in speech.

7. Etiquettes on Speech, Backbiting, and Dealing with Opinions

Key Points

  • Exercise caution when quoting or criticizing others, especially publicly.
  • Discussing opinions for correctness is not backbiting if personal attacks are avoided.
  • Excessive disputation invites satanic whisperings; moderation advised.
  • Dreams related to speech are personal reminders, not obligations to act.
  • Seek forgiveness and maintain humility.

Practical Actions

  • Focus on ideas, not persons, when discussing opinions.
  • Avoid harsh judgments without certainty.
  • Limit disputes to protect spiritual well-being.
  • Treat dreams as personal guidance; no need to inform others.
  • Repent and maintain respectful discourse.

Conclusion

  • The lessons emphasize the importance of clarity, knowledge, and intention in contracts, guarantees, and discharges.
  • Guarantee validity hinges on debt necessity and stability.
  • Discharge requires knowledge and explicit consent.
  • Wage contracts must be clear to avoid invalidity.
  • Backbiting and slander are serious sins requiring repentance and sometimes retraction.
  • Speech etiquette demands caution, fairness, and avoidance of harmful talk.
  • Criticism of ideas is permissible; personal attacks are forbidden.
  • Spiritual health is protected by moderation in disputes and careful speech.
