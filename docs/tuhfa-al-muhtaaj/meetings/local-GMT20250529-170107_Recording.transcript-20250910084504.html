Comprehensive Summary on Guarantee (Dhaman) and Suretyship (Kafala) in Islamic Law

1. Definitions and Key Concepts

  • Guarantee (Dhaman): A commitment by a guarantor to fulfill an obligation if the original debtor fails. Linguistically means a binding commitment.
  • Types of Guarantee:
  • Debt Guarantee: Commitment to pay another’s financial obligation.
  • Physical Presence Guarantee: Commitment to bring or produce a person.
  • Property Guarantee: Commitment to deliver or safeguard an item.
  • Terminology for Guarantor:
  • Dhamin, Hamila: Usually for debt guarantee.
  • Za'im: For large financial obligations.
  • Kafil: Guarantee related to a person’s self.
  • Sabir: General term covering all types.

2. Essential Elements (Arkan) of a Valid Guarantee Contract

1. Guarantor (Dhamin): The person who undertakes the guarantee.

2. Obligation Guaranteed (Madhmun): The debt or item guaranteed.

3. Person to Whom Guarantee is Given (Madhmun lahu): The creditor or beneficiary.

4. Person from Whom Guarantee is Taken (Madhmun 'anhu): The debtor.

5. Formula or Wording of Guarantee (Sigha): Explicit expression of guarantee.

3. Conditions for Validity of Guarantee

  • Sanity and Maturity (Rushd): Guarantor must be sane, mature, and legally competent.
  • Invalid Guarantors: Minors, insane, mute persons unable to understand, intoxicated, or coerced individuals cannot validly guarantee.
  • Slaves: Guarantee by a slave without master’s permission is invalid; valid if permission is granted, especially if necessary.
  • Persons Under Guardianship (Mahjur): Generally invalid unless the guarantee concerns the ward’s own assets and within limits.
  • Explicitness: Guarantee must be explicit; ambiguous or indirect expressions (kinaya) do not suffice.
  • Knowledge: Guarantor must know the debt and creditor; debtor’s consent is not required.
  • Waqf Property: Guarantee of waqf assets requires permission from the waqf manager and is limited to authorized amounts.

4. Evidence from Sunnah and Jurisprudence

  • The Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) guaranteed a debt of 10 dinars for a man, establishing the permissibility and virtue of guarantee.
  • Guarantee is considered a recommended (Sunnah) act, ideally undertaken by trustworthy persons to protect family and community.
  • Classical jurists (e.g., Ibn Hajar, Ibn al-Rif’a) emphasize conditions of competence and explicitness.
  • Guarantee is an additional commitment, not requiring acceptance by the debtor.

5. Guarantee in Specific Contexts

#### a. Guarantee by Persons Under Guardianship

  • Invalid if the ward lacks capacity.
  • Guardians may guarantee on behalf of wards within asset limits.
  • Guarantee involving debts (dīn) is more flexible than guarantee involving present assets (ain).

#### b. Guarantee by Slaves and Workers

  • Requires master’s explicit permission.
  • Guarantee for the slave’s own transactions is valid if permitted.
  • Guarantee for master’s debts without permission is invalid.

#### c. Guarantee of Waqf (Endowment) Property

  • Requires permission from waqf manager.
  • Valid only up to permitted amounts.
  • Invalid if waqf is transferred or permission withdrawn.

6. Guarantee and Sale Transactions

  • Guarantee applies only to known, due debts (e.g., price owed by buyer).
  • Covers defects or deficiencies in sold items (e.g., mortgage, prior sale, defects in measure or quality).
  • Does not cover losses not due to seller’s responsibility or future/uncertain obligations.
  • Guarantee of possession (Daman al-Darak) protects buyer’s price or item after delivery.
  • Guarantee of maintenance expenses (e.g., wife’s future maintenance) is generally invalid as these are acts of kindness, not debts.

7. Guarantee and Debt vs. Maintenance

  • Debts are not extinguished by time; maintenance is obligatory and not waived by lapse.
  • Maintenance for relatives is voluntary charity, not a debt, thus not subject to guarantee.
  • Guarantee of trust or custody (Daman al-Ahdah) applies when seller holds buyer’s money or goods, ensuring protection against defects or claims.

8. Guarantee in Modern Banking Context

  • Banks act as guarantors via agents (managers/officers) on behalf of shareholders.
  • Personal knowledge of the guaranteed party by the guarantor is ideal but often impractical.
  • Guarantee is valid without consent or acceptance of the guaranteed party.
  • Debt must be fixed, known, and obligatory; future or uncertain debts are invalid.
  • Legal and institutional frameworks support bank guarantees despite limited personal knowledge.

9. Differences of Opinion

  • Some scholars require guarantor’s full knowledge of creditor; others accept superficial knowledge.
  • Consent of debtor is generally not required; some minority opinions differ.
  • Validity of guarantee by incapacitated persons and slaves varies among scholars.
  • Writing alone, even with indications, may not suffice for explicit guarantee; explicit wording preferred.

10. Practical Action Items

  • Verify guarantor’s sanity, maturity, and legal competence before accepting guarantee.
  • Ensure guarantee contracts are explicit, preferably written, and witnessed.
  • Avoid guarantees by minors, insane, intoxicated, or coerced persons.
  • Obtain master’s permission before slaves or workers guarantee debts.
  • For waqf properties, secure permission from waqf manager and limit guarantee accordingly.
  • Guarantor must be aware of debt amount and creditor identity.
  • Debtor’s consent is not necessary for guarantee validity.
  • Avoid guaranteeing uncertain, future, or charitable obligations (e.g., maintenance, gifts).
  • In sales, guarantee only covers known debts and defects; clarify terms to avoid disputes.
  • Banks and institutions should rely on legal agency and frameworks for guarantees.

11. Key Evidences and Jurisprudential Bases

  • Sunnah example of the Prophet’s guarantee.
  • Classical fiqh principles on contract validity, guardianship, and agency.
  • Legal maxims: Guarantee is an additional commitment, not a transfer of rights.
  • Juristic opinions from Ibn Abdul Salam, Imam Nawawi, Al-Ghazali, Ibn al-Salah, and others.

Summary

Guarantee (Dhaman) in Islamic law is a serious, binding commitment by a competent guarantor to fulfill another’s obligation if unmet. Valid guarantee requires explicit wording, knowledge of the debt and creditor, and legal capacity of the guarantor. It excludes minors, insane, coerced persons, and slaves without permission. Guarantee covers known debts and defects in sales but excludes uncertain or charitable obligations like future maintenance. In modern contexts, banks act as guarantors via agents, with legal frameworks supporting such guarantees despite limited personal knowledge. The debtor’s consent is not required, and the guarantor may pay on their behalf. Proper documentation and clarity prevent disputes and uphold the rights of all parties.
