Kitab al-Ijarah: Foundations, Contract Forms, and Wage Conditions - April 30, 2026

📅 2026-04-30 🕐 16:00

1. Definition and Linguistic Origin of Ijarah (الإجارة)

Linguistic definition

  • Al-Ijarah (الإجارة) — linguistically a noun meaning al-ujrah (الأجرة), i.e. the wage or compensation; derived from the root ajara / ya'jiru
  • The word is then conventionally applied to the contract itself by extension (majaz)
  • Variant forms: ijarah (همزة مكسورة) is the more eloquent form; ajarah (بالمد) is a valid alternative

Shar'i (legal) definition

  • Tamlik manfa'atin bi-'iwadin bil-shurut al-atiyah (تمليك منفعة بعوض بالشروط الآتية) — transferring ownership of a benefit (manfa'ah) for a consideration, subject to conditions to follow
  • Key conditions mentioned: (1) the consideration ('iwad) must be known (ma'lum); (2) the manfa'ah must be licit and capable of being bestowed (qabilah lil-badhl wal-ibahah)
  • The condition of ma'rifat al-'iwad (knowing the consideration) excludes contracts like musaqah and ju'alah, where the compensation may be unknown
  • The condition of qubulaha lil-badhl wal-ibahah excludes the manfa'at al-bud' (منفعة البضع) — the husband does not own the wife's manfa'ah per se; rather he owns the right to benefit from her; accordingly, a slave-girl cannot be hired out for intercourse

Evidence for permissibility

  • Qur'anic verses, including: "fa-in arda'na lakum fa-atuhunna ujurahunna" (if they nurse for you, give them their wages) [al-Talaq 6] — cited as indicating that hiring out services for compensation is recognised by the Qur'an
  • The Prophet ﷺ and Abu Bakr hiring a guide (dalil) for the Hijrah — evidence from Sunnah
  • The Prophet's ﷺ command to hire out labour (mu'ajarah) and his prohibition of mukhabarah
  • Necessity (darura) and universal need (hajah): people cannot function without engaging the labour of others for compensation; even without explicit textual proof it would be permissible by necessity
  • Scholarly consensus (ijma') preceded by the Qur'anic evidence

2. Pillars (Arkan) of Ijarah

  • Four pillars: (1) Sighah (صيغة) — the offer and acceptance; (2) Ujrah (أجرة) — the wage/rent; (3) Manfa'ah (منفعة) — the benefit/service; (4) 'Aqid (عاقد) — the contracting parties
  • Unlike Bab al-Bay', where the sighah is discussed first, the author begins here with the contracting parties ('aqidayn); the sheikh explains this as intentional variation (tafannun) in presentation style, and notes there are scholarly reasons for both orderings

3. Conditions of the Contracting Parties (al-'Aqidayn)

General rule

  • Ijarah is a subspecies (sinf) of bay' (sale); therefore the conditions of the contracting parties in sale apply here: rushd (legal capacity / sound discretion) and absence of unlawful coercion ('adam al-ikrah bi-ghayr haqq)

Ruling on a kafir hiring a Muslim

  • It is valid (sahih) for a non-Muslim to hire a Muslim, even for ijarah al-'ayn (hiring the person's specific labour)
  • However it is makruh (disliked)
  • The imam/authority can compel the person to rent to a Muslim instead (ujbira 'ala ija'ratihi li-muslim)
  • Sheikh Salem notes: this applies to ijarah al-'ayn (specific person); for ijarah al-dhimmah (obligation-based hire) there is no compulsion because no specific Muslim is designated for the work
  • Contemporary relevance discussed: Muslims working for non-Muslim corporations (Google, Amazon, etc.) — these fall under the general rule; compulsion to work for a Muslim employer rests with the imam/state, not the individual

Hiring out the safih (السفيه — person under interdiction for fiscal incompetence)

  • A safih may hire himself out for work that is not his intended occupation (i.e. not the skilled trade he is known for), provided he is self-sufficient in wealth and does not need the earnings for maintenance
  • Analogy: since a safih may volunteer (yatabarru') his labour for Hajj, he may also hire himself out for such work
  • Al-Mawardi and al-Ruyani: even if he is wealthy enough not to need the earnings, ijarah of non-intended work is permissible, since voluntary donation of labour is valid
  • For his intended/skilled trade (e.g. a craftsman in his own craft), it is not valid even according to al-Mawardi and al-Ruyani, because the guardian (wali) can compel him to earn a living even if wealthy

A master selling his own slave to himself

  • A master may sell his slave's freedom (baya' al-sayyid qinnahu nafsahu) — this leads to emancipation ('itq), and the sharia strongly encourages emancipation
  • However, a master may not hire out the slave to himself (la ijaratuha iyyaha), because ijarah does not lead to emancipation, and the tolerance extended by the sharia for emancipation does not apply here

Two guardians (nazirun) for a waqf, one hiring from the other

  • If each nazir is independent in his authority, one nazir may lease waqf land to the other — valid
  • If their authority is joint, it is not valid (as researched by Abu Zur'ah)
  • Distinguished from two awsiya' (executors) required to act jointly over the estate of their ward: one may purchase from the other on behalf of his ward, because: (a) they act jointly (meeting the condition), and (b) there is no conflict of interest since each acts for his own ward, not for himself

4. The Sighah (Offer and Acceptance)

General principles

  • Sighah in ijarah is like sighah in bay'; therefore the debate over mu'atah (exchange without formal utterance) applies here as well
  • Sighah is required; the contract is concluded by offer (ijab) and acceptance (qabul), including by written form and by intelligible sign of a mute person

Explicit (sarih) forms

  • Ajartu-ka hadha (أجّرتك هذا) — I have rented this to you
  • Akraytu-ka hadha (أكريتك هذا) — I have leased this to you
  • Mallaktu-ka manafi'ahu sanatan (ملّكتك منافعه سنة) — I have given you ownership of its usufruct for a year; here "sanah" is not a temporal adverb modifying the verb but is connected to an implied verb meaning "benefit from it for a year" — the contract is an insha' (performative), completed at the moment of utterance

Kinayah (implicit/indirect) forms

  • Uskun dari shahran bi-kadha (اسكن داري شهراً بكذا) — Dwell in my house for a month for such an amount
  • Ja'altu laka manfa'ataha sanatan bi-kadha (جعلت لك منفعتها سنة بكذا) — I have made its usufruct yours for a year for such an amount
  • Written form (kitabah) is also valid as kinayah

Disputed: using the word "bay'" for ijarah

  • Correct opinion (al-Tuhfah / Ibn Hajar following Shaykh al-Islam Zakariyya): saying "bi'tu-ka manfa'ataha" (I sold you its usufruct) constitutes kinayah and the ijarah is valid — because ijarah is itself a sale of usufruct
  • Opposing view (al-Mughni, al-Nihayah / al-Ramli): the word "bay'" is set for the sale of 'ayn (corpus); using it for manfa'ah is invalid even as kinayah, because a word that is sarih in one domain cannot serve as kinayah in another domain
  • Sheikh Salem's comment: logically, a term that is sarih in its own domain should not even be kinayah elsewhere, because it implies permanence (ta'bid), while ijarah requires a defined term (ta'qit bi-mudda)
  • Disagreement noted between Ibn Hajar/Zakariyya al-Ansari (valid as kinayah, following Sharh al-Rawdh and Sharh al-Minhaj) vs. al-Ramli/al-Khatib (invalid, following al-Mughni and al-Nihayah)

5. Types of Ijarah: 'Ayn vs. Dhimmah

The two categories

  • Ijarah al-'ayn (إجارة العين) — hire of a specific identified thing or person: the contract is bound to a particular named entity
  • Ijarah al-dhimmah (إجارة الذمة) — obligation-based hire: a person commits in his dhimmah (legal capacity/obligation) to provide a service or deliver work, without necessarily doing it himself

Sub-categories of ijarah al-'ayn

  • Hire of immovable property (iqar / 'aqar) — e.g. a house: cannot be ijarah al-dhimmah because immovables cannot be established as a debt in the dhimmah
  • Hire of an animal (dabbah) or human being (adami) — when specified, both ijarah al-'ayn and ijarah al-dhimmah are possible
  • Al-Jalal al-Bulqini's research: ships are analogous to animals and humans (not to immovable property), so a specific ship can also be the subject of either form of ijarah
  • Sheikh Salem: this view appears to be adopted by Ibn Hajar based on his manner of citing it without refutation

Determining which type applies

  • If the offer is addressed to a named person — "ista'jartu-ka li-ta'mala kadha" (I have hired you to do X) — this is ijarah al-'ayn because the address (khitab) ties the contract to the specific person
  • Minority view: even direct address is ijarah al-dhimmah because the intent is to get the work done, not necessarily from that specific person
  • If the offer is "alzamtu dhimmata-ka 'amala kadha" (I have obligated your dhimmah to do X) — this is ijarah al-dhimmah; the person may perform it himself or delegate
  • Contemporary application discussed: airline ticket = ijarah al-dhimmah (the airline is obliged to transport you; if one aircraft is unavailable another or a partner airline may be used); employment contract = ijarah al-'ayn (the specific employee is engaged by name)
  • A person hired for a waqf position (teaching, leading prayer, adhan) may deputise (istanaba) someone equivalent or superior to him in an emergency — this is an established exception even within ijarah al-'ayn

6. Conditions Specific to Ijarah al-Dhimmah

  • If concluded with the lafz (wording) of ijarah or of salam, the ujrah must be handed over in the contract session (tassim al-ujrah fil-majlis) — analogous to the capital (ra's mal) in a salam contract, because ijarah al-dhimmah is in effect a "salam in usufruct"
  • This requirement applies whether or not the work commences immediately after the contract
  • Deferral of the wage (ta'jil al-ujrah) is not permitted in ijarah al-dhimmah — unlike ijarah al-'ayn
  • Substitution (istibdal), assignment (hawalah), and remission (ibra') of the wage in ijarah al-dhimmah are all forbidden — as with the capital of a salam
  • Work in ijarah al-dhimmah need not commence immediately after the contract — it may be deferred; in contrast, ijarah al-'ayn requires continuity (the hire begins at the point of contract)
  • Rationale: ijarah al-dhimmah is inherently weaker than ijarah al-'ayn because the manfa'ah (usufruct) is received in instalments; the requirement of immediate payment of the wage compensates for this weakness, just as salam requires immediate payment of the capital

Ijarah al-'ayn: rules on the wage

  • The wage in ijarah al-'ayn is like the price in bay': no requirement to hand it over at the contract session
  • If the wage is unspecified as to timing (mutlaq), it is treated as immediately due (mu'ajjal) — analogous to an absolute sale price
  • Deferral (ta'jil), acceleration (ta'jil), substitution (istibdal), assignment (hawalah), and remission (ibra') are all permitted in ijarah al-'ayn when the wage is in the dhimmah
  • If the wage is specified as a particular physical object (mu'ayyanah), it cannot be deferred — physical objects do not admit deferral
  • The lessor owns the wage at the moment of the contract, even if deferred; however, this is a mulk mura'an (contingent/anticipated ownership) — as each period of time passes without damage to the leased object, the lessor's ownership of the corresponding portion of the wage becomes absolute (mustaqirr)
  • Practical implication for zakat: if someone leases property for four years at 400 dirhams, paying upfront, the lessor must pay zakat annually on each year's portion as it becomes mustaqirr; full possession of the upfront amount is treated as mulk mura'an until earned
  • If the leased property is destroyed before the period ends, the remaining unearned portion of the wage must be returned

7. Conditions of the Ujrah (Wage/Rent)

Knowledge of the ujrah

  • The ujrah must be known in genus, quantity, and description (ma'lumah jinsan wa-qadran wa-sifatan) if it is in the dhimmah
  • If the ujrah is a specified physical object (mu'ayyanah), it is sufficient that the parties have seen it (mu'ayanah) — its genus, quantity and description need not be stated verbally
  • Exception: Hajj performed for rizq (provisions) — treated as a tawsi'ah (concession) for facilitating worship, not a standard ijarah

Invalid wage arrangements

  • Hiring for repairs in exchange for use of the property (bi-'imaratih) — not valid: the repair work is unknown in extent, making the wage unknown (majhulah); leads to dispute
  • Hiring a tanner to skin an animal in exchange for the hide — not valid (prohibited by the hadith): the hide is produced by the worker's own labour, making it analogous to qafiz al-tahhan
  • Hiring a miller to grind grain for a share of the flour or bran — not valid: forbidden by the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ prohibiting qafiz al-tahhan (قفيز الطحان) — giving the miller a measure of the very flour produced by his work as his wage; the rule (dabit): the wage must not be something that comes into existence only through the worker's labour on the very object being worked
  • Hiring a tax collector (jabi al-'ushr) for a share of what he collects — included in the principle of qafiz al-tahhan; therefore: (a) per al-Subki: invalid ijarah, worker gets ujrat al-mithl; (b) per Ibn Hajar: valid as ju'alah (reward contract), worker gets ujrat al-mithl due to uncertainty; (c) per al-Ramli and al-Khatib: invalid, worker gets ujrat al-mithl — all agree the result is ujrat al-mithl, differing on whether to categorise it as fasid ijarah or valid ju'alah
  • Hiring a woman to breastfeed a slave infant for a portion of that same slave — valid on the correct opinion (al-sahih) provided the portion is specified (e.g. "a third of him, now"), because the wage is known; no objection that the work is performed on something partly owned by a non-contracting party, since it is a consequential matter (tabi')
  • Al-Subki's nuance: if the contract covers the whole slave or is absolute (mutlaq), it is not valid; only valid if directed specifically at the hirer's share
  • The wage (portion of slave) must be specified as payable immediately (fi al-hal) — deferred delivery of a specified object is not valid (la tu'ajjal al-ujrah al-mu'ayyanah)
  • Hiring a goat/ewe to suckle an infant — not valid: (a) no necessity justifying it (unlike a woman); (b) inability of the lessor to guarantee delivery of the service (animals cannot be compelled the way a capable woman can)
  • Hiring a male animal for stud service (dirab al-fahl) — not valid for the same reason: the lessor cannot guarantee delivery

8. Q&A: Zakat and the Person with Uncollectable Debts

The question

  • A person has reached poverty but holds debts owed to him from past trade; the debtors are delaying payment (mumatalah) and the debts are currently due (hallah). May he receive zakat?

Sheikh Salem's ruling

  • If he is genuinely unable to collect the debts — either because the debtors are poor, or because he cannot easily access the courts (qada') to enforce collection — then he may be given zakat
  • If collection is easily achievable through the courts with minimal difficulty, the debt is treated as effectively in his possession, and he may not receive zakat

Analogous principle from Tuhfah/Nihayah

  • A wife whose husband is wealthy but refuses to spend on her, and she cannot access a judge to enforce her rights, may take zakat — by analogy the same applies to an owner of debts who cannot enforce collection
  • Nizar Mashal raised: if the debts are due but the debtors are refusing and the creditor can use the courts — the answer is that easy court access means no zakat entitlement; it is only when judicial enforcement is itself difficult or impossible that zakat may be given

Allah knows best.
