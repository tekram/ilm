1. Agency (Al-Wikalah) in Islamic Law: Conditions and Restrictions on the Agent

Definitions and Context

  • Agency (Wikalah): A contract where a principal (muwakkil) authorizes an agent (wakeel) to act on their behalf.
  • Focus on restrictions imposed by the principal on the agent’s authority regarding person, time, and place.

Key Rulings on Restrictions

  • Restriction to a Specific Person:

The agent must act only with the specified individual (e.g., "sell to Zayd"). Acting otherwise invalidates the transaction.

  • Restriction to a Specific Time:

The agent cannot act before or after the specified time (e.g., "sell on Friday").

  • Applies to sales and emancipation (manumission).
  • For divorce, scholarly opinions differ: majority hold divorce outside the specified time is invalid; minority allow validity but discourage it.
  • Restriction to a Specific Place:

The agent must act only in the designated place unless:

  • The price is fixed, and
  • The principal did not forbid selling elsewhere, in which case selling outside may be valid.

Restrictions may serve hidden purposes like concealing the transaction or targeting a market.

Evidences and Scholarly Opinions

  • The agent’s hand is a hand of trust (يد أمانة), requiring strict adherence to instructions (Al-Balqini, Al-Azra'i).
  • Qur’anic principle: fulfilling trusts and contracts (general basis, no direct verse cited).
  • If restriction is for a specific purpose (gharaḍ), it must be respected; if conventional or accidental, some flexibility is allowed.

Practical Guidelines

  • Clearly specify any restrictions when appointing an agent.
  • Agents must strictly follow restrictions; violations invalidate transactions.
  • For sales/emancipation restricted by time/place, do not act outside specified limits.
  • For divorce restricted by time, follow majority opinion disallowing divorce outside that time.
  • Honor hidden purposes behind restrictions even if unstated.

2. Sale and Agency: Conditions, Validity, and Ethical Considerations

Sale in Another’s Name and Market Context

  • Agency sales are valid even if conducted in third-party markets.
  • Certain markets (e.g., Souq Abdullah Baalawi in Tarim) are traditionally considered blessed (سوق مبارك) based on scholarly reports and signs of barakah.

Conditions on Sale and Price

  • Designated Sale: If sale is for a specific purpose/item, it must be fulfilled; otherwise, designation is conventional and non-binding.
  • Price Fixing: Selling below the agreed price is invalid; even minor reductions are prohibited if price is fixed.
  • Slight Undervaluation: Allowed only in unspecified sales.
  • Price Increase: Allowed and sometimes obligatory if a willing buyer offers more, even during option periods (خيار المجلس أو خيار الشرط).

Substitution and Description

  • Substituting items or buying more/less than specified without permission invalidates the sale for the principal.
  • Such sales may bind the agent personally but not the principal.

Agent’s Compliance

  • Agent must follow instructions exactly (e.g., payment method).
  • Acting contrary to instructions invalidates the sale for the principal.

Agency in Gifts and Loans

  • Naming the principal is necessary for validity in gifts and loans, unlike sales.
  • Ownership transfer depends on contract and naming.

Contracts Involving Freedom (إعتاق)

  • Freeing a slave as part of sale requires owner’s consent; owner may refuse before payment.

Ethical Considerations

  • Assisting in sin (e.g., helping break option periods) is sinful but may be excused if for third-party benefit.
  • Agents must avoid facilitating wrongdoing.

Practical Guidelines

  • Follow principal’s instructions on price, item, and payment strictly.
  • Avoid unauthorized substitutions or price reductions.
  • Name the principal in gifts and loans.
  • Be cautious with contracts involving emancipation.
  • Recognize the special status of blessed markets but do not rely on it for legal validity.
  • Prioritize principal’s interest in unclear cases.

3. Sale, Option Period (Khiyar al-Shart), and Related Transactions

Ownership Transfer and Option Period

  • Ownership is transferred conditionally during the option period; if option is exercised, sale completes; if not, sale voids.
  • Selling the same item to another during option without permission is prohibited.

Selling on Top of Another Sale

  • Allowed if it serves principal’s interest and does not violate instructions.

Price Increase and Substitution During Option

  • Price increase or offering different kind is allowed unless explicitly forbidden.
  • Textual evidence overrides customary practice.

Favoritism (Muhabaha) in Sale

  • Selling to a specific person at fixed price below market value may indicate favoritism.
  • Scholars differ:
  • Al-Ghazali and Al-Ramli condemn favoritism as a "crime."
  • Ibn Hajar permits some favoritism if buyer could have paid more.
  • Favoritism prohibits price increase or selling to others at higher price.

Buying Multiple Items Instead of One

  • Validity depends on whether each item equals the specified price.
  • If not, purchase invalid even if combined value exceeds price.

Buying on Credit vs. Immediate Payment

  • Buying on credit when instructed to pay immediately invalidates sale for principal but valid for agent personally.
  • Buying immediately when instructed to buy on credit invalidates sale for both.

Naming the Principal in Contracts

  • Essential to name principal to bind them, especially in gifts and loans.
  • Failure results in agent being personally liable.

Intention vs. Legal Effect

  • Agent’s intention does not override lack of authorization or naming; legal effect depends on contract terms.

Practical Guidelines

  • Agents must follow instructions on payment method, item, price, and substitutions.
  • Always name principal in contracts to bind them.
  • Clarify conditions on price increases and sales to specific persons.
  • Avoid selling same item to multiple buyers during option period without permission.
  • Ensure substituted items meet value and description conditions.

Importance of Clear Communication

  • Speech and wording in contracts must not deviate except for the benefit of the addressee or principal.
  • Example: "I sold your agent Zaid" illustrates how wording affects rulings.

Practical Action Items for Agents and Principals

  • Principals:
  • Clearly specify restrictions on agent’s authority (person, time, place).
  • Specify price conditions, payment terms, and substitution permissions.
  • Require naming in contracts, especially gifts and loans.
  • Agents:
  • Strictly adhere to principal’s instructions.
  • Do not act outside specified restrictions.
  • Name principal in contracts to bind them.
  • Avoid unauthorized substitutions or price changes.
  • Be aware of ethical implications and avoid facilitating sin.
  • Both:
  • Maintain clear communication and precise wording in contracts.
  • Seek scholarly guidance on complex contemporary issues.
  • Prepare for detailed discussions on urgent fiqh questions related to current events.
