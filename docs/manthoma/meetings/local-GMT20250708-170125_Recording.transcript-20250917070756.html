Zakat Cash vs Goods - July 08, 2025

📅 2025-07-08 🕐 17:01

Overview

This summary merges the lessons on: substituting goods for monetary zakāh (including zakāt al‑fiṭr), zakāh when wealth is in mixed coins/metal/currencies, distribution rules (relatives abroad, merchants), disallowed sale‑and‑refund stratagems, rulings on insects/animals in food and drink (including the fly narration), purity of human corpses, and wasting edible wealth (including gold leaf on food).

Key rulings (concise)

  • Zakat on money (zakat al‑nuqūd / trade capital)
  • Shafi‘i position: zakāh on cash must be paid in cash (like‑for‑like). Giving food, garments or other goods does NOT discharge monetary zakāh.
  • Hanafi and some early authorities (and a narration recorded in Sahih al‑Bukhari) permit giving goods (staples, clothing) in place of cash; such giving discharges the cash zakāh.
  • You may not discharge a zakāh liability by substituting a clearly lower‑value coin or item.
  • Zakat al‑fitr
  • Classically: give the prevailing staple food of the locality.
  • In urban contexts, cash is often preferred for beneficiaries’ welfare; one should respect madhhabic cautions and local practice.
  • Gold/silver and mixed metals/currencies
  • Cannot discharge zakāh due on gold by giving silver (or vice versa); nor by giving base metals (copper, brass).
  • Varieties of the same metal may substitute only if equal in market value/content; if types differ materially, pay from median or higher‑value types, not from the lowest.
  • Modern fiat: you may pay zakāh due in one currency by giving equivalent market value in another currency — ensure recipient obtains equal value (use current exchange, account for transaction costs).
  • Distribution and recipients
  • Main Shafi‘i teaching referenced: zakāh is generally to be distributed among eligible recipients within the locality where it is due; giving zakāh to emigrant children or to relatives abroad is not automatically allowed under that principal view (some jurists permit transfer under conditions).
  • Merchants and goods as zakāh
  • In the Shafi‘i view: trade zakāh should be paid in cash. Handing over stock as zakāh is generally not accepted; the proper method is to sell the goods (or convert them to cash) then give the proceeds as zakāh.
  • Prohibited/undesirable stratagems
  • Sale‑and‑refund tricks (sell to a poor person, take cash, then return it to treat it as zakāh) are blameworthy (makrūh) and discouraged; they resemble legal stratagems and may be invalid according to stronger positions.

Definitions and concepts

  • Nisāb: the threshold for zakāh liability (e.g., classical example 40 Majidi riyals for silver).
  • Like‑for‑like (jins bi‑jins): principle that zakāh of an item should be discharged by that same kind/value (money→money, gold→gold).
  • Difference of type (jins) vs attribute (sīfah): type = intrinsic value difference (different metal/content); attribute = minor differences (mint, slight wear) where market value is effectively equal.
  • Middle/median (awsaṭ): choose a representative or median type/value when many varieties make separate calculation impractical.

Evidences cited

  • Hadith — fly in a drink: “If a fly falls into the drink of one of you, dip it then remove it…” (Sahih al‑Bukhari; variants exist). Used to permit dipping/removal for flies in drinks.
  • Hadith — rodent in fat: Narrations in Sunan (e.g., Abu Dawud and others) about a mouse/rat falling into ghee/samn instruct discarding the mouse and affected fat or pouring out the liquid; used to rule certain liquids/fats contaminated.
  • Qur’an: “And We have certainly honored the children of Adam…” (Q 17:70) — cited to support that human corpses are not ritual najāsah (impure) in themselves.
  • Sahihayn / Prophetic reports: hadiths stating “the believer is not najis” used to support purity of the deceased believer.
  • Juristic texts: classical Shafi‘i works (e.g., al‑Tuhfa), and jurists like Ibn Hajar, al‑Kurdī, Imam al‑Ghazali referenced for specific legal refinements.

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • Zakat substitution
  • Shafi‘i: strict like‑for‑like; money must be paid in money.
  • Hanafi and some narrations: permit giving goods instead of cash.
  • Practical local harmonization: some Shafi‘i teachers allow following the permissive view in urban contexts for beneficiaries’ welfare, but advise caution.
  • Mixed metals/currencies
  • Majority Shafi‘i: cannot discharge gold by silver or by base metals; equal market value/content is required to substitute.
  • Some flexibility recognized when separation is impractical — paying from the median or higher is allowed by some jurists.
  • Flies/insects in food
  • Majority apply the fly hadith specifically to flies; extending dipping to all insects is disputed (Ibn Hajar limited it; al‑Damimi broadened it).
  • Oil/fat contaminated by rodents
  • Some Maliki reports more permissive in large vats; many other jurists advise discarding affected fat/liquid.
  • Zakat distribution to relatives abroad
  • Some jurists allow sending zakāh to eligible poor outside locality; the Shafi‘i position presented is restrictive, especially concerning emigrant children.

Practical examples and clarifications

  • Cannot discharge 40 Majidi riyals of zakāh by handing over a French riyal of the same weight but much lower market value.
  • If many coin types exist and dividing is hard: calculate total market value and pay equivalent zakāh amount, or pay from the median/higher category rather than the cheapest.
  • Merchant who wants to give goods as zakāh: sell goods, then give proceeds as zakāh; directly handing over stock is generally invalid in the stricter view.
  • Zakat al‑fitr: prefer staple food; in cities cash may better serve recipients — follow local madhhab guidance.

Purity rulings — insects / small creatures in food and drink

  • Fly in drink: follow the Prophetic prescription — wet/dip then remove the fly (Sahih Bukhari); this indicates permissibility for such a case.
  • Other small insects:
  • If an insect falls into liquid and the liquid is altered or cannot be purified: discard/pour out.
  • If in solid food and no perceptible change occurred: removing the carcass generally suffices according to many jurists; caution allowed.
  • Rodent in oil/fat:
  • Stronger readings: discard affected fat or pour out liquid if contaminated (hadith evidence).
  • Some permissive local rulings exist for large vats; when in doubt, follow your madhhab or a local scholar.
  • Contaminated food sale/gift:
  • If selling/giving food that contains insects, inform the buyer/recipient; otherwise the server is blameworthy if the recipient would have refused.
  • If no human will accept it, feeding to animals that will eat it is permitted.

Human corpses and purity

  • A human corpse is not ritual najāsah in the sense that would bar normal handling for burial; Qur’an 17:70 and Prophetic reports support that believers are not najis.
  • Practical caveat: if a corpse emits visible filth that soils a carrier’s clothes/body, that visible filth must be cleansed before prayer (ordinary impurity rules apply to the soiling, not to the corpse per se).

Gold leaf / edible precious metals and wasting food

  • Using edible gold as decorative food ornament:
  • Not intrinsically haram, but likely blameworthy and potentially forbidden if it is pure ostentation and waste of wealth.
  • Permissible if there is a legitimate benefit (therapeutic, accepted cultural practice without extravagance) and no sinful intent.
  • Destroying edible wealth:
  • Deliberate waste of edible food without valid reason is blameworthy and impermissible.
  • If food becomes spoiled, discard; otherwise prefer storage, redistribution to those in need, or feeding animals.

Practical action items (how to act)

  • Zakat payments
  • Pay zakāh on cash in cash when following Shafi‘i rulings; if following Hanafi or permissive narrations, giving staple goods may discharge zakāh — apply such leniency cautiously.
  • Do not substitute a clearly lower‑value coin/item to discharge a higher‑value liability.
  • For mixed currencies/coins: compute actual market value at the time of payment; pay equivalent value or use the median/higher type if dividing is impractical.
  • Zakat al‑fitr: preferably give the local staple; in urban contexts cash may be more beneficial — follow local scholarly guidance.
  • Merchants: convert goods to cash (sell) before giving zakāh.
  • If you wish to send zakāh to relatives abroad, consult your local Shafi‘i authority; consider voluntary charity when zakāh transfer is doubtful.
  • Avoid legal stratagems (sale‑and‑refund) to discharge zakāh — give zakāh transparently.
  • Food and purity
  • Fly in a drink: dip/wet and remove the fly (follow the hadith).
  • Insect/rodent contamination: discard liquids clearly altered; remove carcasses from solids when permissible; when in doubt, consult local madhhab rulings and avoid consumption if repelled.
  • Disclose contamination when selling or gifting food; feed contaminated but usable food to animals if no human will accept it.
  • Gold/wealth use
  • Avoid extravagant use of precious metals on food purely for ostentation.
  • Do not intentionally destroy edible wealth; prefer preservation, giving to needy, or feeding animals as appropriate.
  • Purity and corpses
  • Perform funeral washing/shrouding as required; treat the corpse with dignity — do not regard it as elevating ritual impurity. Clean any visible filth transferred to carriers before prayer.

Authorities, cautions and methodological notes

  • Primary juristic authorities cited: Shafi‘i texts (e.g., al‑Tuhfa), Ibn Hajar, al‑Kurdī, Imam al‑Ghazali, and reports in Sahih al‑Bukhari and other hadith collections (Abu Dawud, Sunan compilations).
  • Use of lenient/exceptional opinions: permitted only with caution and preferably when genuine hardship or benefit justifies it; avoid widespread reliance on weak/isolated reports that undermine established obligations.
  • When in doubt about classification of modern instruments (paper money, banknotes) as “gold” or “silver” for zakāh, consult a qualified local jurist to determine customary/legal treatment and ensure equivalent value is delivered.

Short checklist for immediate practice

  • Determine nisāb and calculate zakāh on actual market value at the due time.
  • Pay like with like when possible (money→money, gold→gold, silver→silver).
  • If using another currency, ensure recipient receives equivalent market value (account for exchange costs).
  • Sell trade goods before using proceeds as zakāh rather than handing over stock (unless following a reliable permissive ruling after consultation).
  • Do not use sale‑and‑refund tricks.
  • Follow the fly hadith for drinks; discard clearly contaminated liquids; remove carcasses from solids where allowed.
  • Avoid wasteful use of precious items as food decoration; prefer moderation and legitimate benefit.

(For complex cases — mixed coins, foreign currencies, zakāh distribution to relatives abroad, or disputed contamination scenarios — consult a qualified local Shafi‘i scholar with full details of your holdings and context.)
