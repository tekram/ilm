Sale of land that included trees, rocks, and a spring — exception: “except the small palm saplings”

Facts

  • Contract described sold parcel as including “trees, stones, a spring (ayn), and palms” but added: “except the small palms (nakhil saghīr)” which were not divided/included.
  • Seller retained small planted palm saplings and attempted to draw water from the spring on the sold land; buyer prevented him.

Key legal rulings

  • If the sale is valid and the contract includes the spring and the water present at contract time, then the water (existing at time of contract) belongs to the buyer; the seller has no right to take it without the buyer’s permission.
  • Drawing water without buyer’s permission is wrongful (usurpation) and may be prevented by the buyer.

Definitions and legal reasoning

  • When an ayn (spring) is sold “with what it contains of water at the time,” water present at contract time becomes the buyer’s property to avoid later confusion between old and newly occurring water.
  • “Small palm (nakhil saghīr)” distinction:
  • Separately planted saplings explicitly excluded are not part of the sale.
  • Offshoots still attached to the mother tree count as part of the main tree and are included.

Alternative (Hadrami) view and scope

  • If the seller sold only the well but retained palms that necessarily depend on that well for irrigation, the Hadrami text (Ifādah al‑Hadramiyya) allows the seller a right of passage through the buyer’s land to water the excluded palms — but only if no alternative route exists. This is a specific exception, analogized to reserving a room in a sold house.
  • The primary ruling presented remains: valid sale including the water allows the buyer to bar the seller; the Hadrami allowance is a limited exception.

Evidence/procedure in disputes

  • If quantity taken is unknown, the claimant should specify an estimated quantity to make the claim concrete.
  • Usual evidentiary rules apply: claimant provides proof; defendant may deny and be required to swear; if defendant swears, claimant’s case fails.

Practical action items

  • Sellers: if retaining saplings reliant on a sold water source, secure an express written irrigation/right‑of‑access in the sale contract.
  • Buyers: explicitly state in contract whether water present at time of sale is included.
  • Drafters: specify which offspring/trees are excluded vs included (distinguish separate saplings from attached offshoots).
  • Disputes: collect measurements and witnesses; be prepared to state concrete quantities.

Sale of plots completely surrounded (muḥtaf) — access and right‑of‑way (mamar)

Definitions

  • Muḥtaf: property surrounded on all sides by others’ or seller’s land with no immediate access.
  • Mamar: right of passage / easement.
  • Istidāma (continuity): seller sells part and retains part (e.g., sells house but reserves a room).

Main legal principle

  • A sale of land/house that is inaccessible at the time of contract (no outlet) generally invalidates the sale because buyer is deprived of immediate benefit/possession.
  • If access can reasonably be created later, the sale may be upheld in some cases (continuity/possibility of later access treated more leniently than initial lack).

Five contract formulations & rulings (concise)

1. Seller grants passage “from all sides”: sale valid; buyer has those passages.

2. Seller grants passage “from one side” but does not designate which: condition void for ambiguity → sale invalid.

3. Seller says “I sold it with its rights (bi‑huquqihā)”: sale valid; buyer taken to have customary access rights unless property adjoins buyer’s land or a public way.

4. Seller simply sells without mentioning way: generally valid; buyer assumed to have access from all sides except when the property abuts buyer’s land or a public road (in which case no additional right over seller’s land is assumed).

5. Seller denies any right of passage: sale invalid (buyer cannot benefit).

Cases with obstacles / third‑party land

  • If property is blocked at time of contract (no present way), sale is invalid even if passage could be made later.
  • If surrounded by third‑party land with no present access, generally sale is invalid.

Special: seller reserves part (istidāma)

  • Reservations treated more leniently: if seller reserves a room, seller may have right to pass through sold portion to reach reserved part if no other access exists.

Practical action items

  • Confirm and document legal access or secure easement before buying.
  • If selling enclosed land, grant in contract a specific right‑of‑way or disclose and resolve access.
  • Specify which side/path is granted when multiple routes exist.
  • If property abuts buyer’s land or public road, clarify whether buyer may also pass over seller’s land.

Authorities / evidence

  • Jurisprudential reasoning cited; no explicit Qur’an/ḥadith quoted in the recorded segment. Dominant scholarly position: lack of present access at contract time undermines validity.

Purity and impurity of produce and animals (watered with urine, animals fed on filth)

Definitions

  • Najāsa: ritual impurity (e.g., urine, dung).
  • Karāhah tanẓīhiyya: disliked but permissible (mild disapproval).
  • Ayn al‑najāsa: identifiable core/essence of impurity.
  • “Filtering” property: some plants/fruits do not transmit taste/smell of soil impurity to edible part.

Fruits/produce irrigated with urine (e.g., watermelon)

  • Ruling presented: generally permitted (rukhsah), especially when the fruit shows no bad taste, odor, or visible change. Many fruits are “filtered” and do not take on impurity.
  • If fruit bears taste or smell of impurity:
  • Majority/teaching: becomes disliked (karāhah), not outright forbidden.
  • Minority (e.g., Imam al‑Rāfiʿī in some readings): treats as impure/prohibited.
  • Practical test: check taste, smell, and appearance.

Animals that fed on filth or suckled from dog/pig

  • Majority position taught: such animals are ritually pure (tāhir) but their consumption is disliked (karāhah tanẓīhiyya); avoid their milk/meat if taste/odor persists.
  • Minority stricter view: consider such animals impure and prohibit consumption.
  • Purification method for animals: feed them pure fodder for an established period (periods vary by species) until the impurity’s taste/odor is gone; washing/cooking alone insufficient.

Use of manure/excrement in agriculture or building (including mosques)

  • Jurists differ:
  • Some forbid building a mosque with bricks/clay kneaded with impurity (impermissible; remove if used).
  • Others allow under necessity or consider it disliked.
  • If the impurity transmits taste/odor to produce or remains identifiable in building material, stricter rulings apply.
  • Historical practice: many communities used manure; jurists varied between permissibility with dislike and prohibition if taint was apparent.

Purification principles

  • If impurity remains as an identifiable ayn, it remains impure; cooking or superficial washing may not suffice.
  • For mixtures where water can reach all parts (e.g., dough), thorough washing/immersion that reaches interior may purify.
  • For heavy impurities (dog/pig) stricter washing and remedial measures are required.

Differences of opinion

  • Majority: permissive with karāhah for many produce-cases; stricter minority forbids consumption when impurity involved.
  • Authorities referenced: Imam al‑Nawawī (permissible with dislike), Imam al‑Rāfiʿī (stricter), Ibn al‑ʿAbd al‑Salām and al‑Ghazālī (lenient regarding animals fed on forbidden food).

Practical action items

  • For suspect produce: inspect for taste/odor — if none, permissible per majority; if present, avoid or follow stricter local madhhab.
  • For suspect animals: prefer to rehabilitate (feed pure fodder) until taint gone; avoid products if doubt persists.
  • For use in mosques/building: avoid materials visibly containing impurity; if built with such materials, consult local scholars — removal may be required.
  • When in doubt, follow local madhhab or ask a qualified scholar.

Use of impure materials in mosques and building — liability and remedies

Key rulings

  • Spreading impure material (dung, contaminated clay) inside a mosque is impermissible; prevent and remove.
  • Building mosque walls/floors with bricks/clay mixed with identifiable impurity is generally not allowed; removal (dismantling) is recommended by stronger opinions.
  • Where impurity is truly mixed/altered and not present as an ayn, some jurists allow leniency, especially under necessity.

Purification method for mixed materials

  • Purification requires water to reach all parts; for dough/clay, kneading/immersion/washing until pervasion is necessary.
  • Solid lumps of filth in bricks remain impure; superficial cooking/washing does not purify them.

Practical action items

  • Do not build or finish mosques with knowingly impure clay/bricks.
  • Prevent and remove impure mats or coverings in prayer spaces.
  • Replace or dismantle impure building material in sacred spaces where feasible.

Liability when someone eats or uses another’s property

Key rulings

  • If someone consumes another’s food/animal, they owe compensation equal to its value (ḍamān debt).
  • The debt is enforceable against the eater; no automatic lien attaches to the eater’s property while he is alive, but the judge may prevent sale or require security.
  • If the eater dies, the debt is satisfied from the estate before distribution to heirs.

Practical action items

  • Repay market value if you consumed someone else’s property.
  • Owners: seek judicial redress and provide evidence.
  • Executors/heirs: settle such debts from estate funds prior to inheritance distribution.

Tawassul and Istighātha (seeking intercession) — permissibility, limits, and precautions

Definitions

  • Tawassul: seeking to approach Allah by a means (e.g., invoking the Prophet’s intercession).
  • Istighātha: calling for help; may mean asking an intermediary to intercede.
  • Al‑īhām: ambiguous phrasing that may imply impermissible meaning.

Main rulings taught

  • Tawassul and asking the Prophet or righteous persons to intercede with Allah are permissible provided:
  • The petitioner affirms Allah is the sole granter of benefit/harm.
  • The petitioner intends the intermediary only as a means (not as an independent agent).
  • Addressing the Prophet at his grave or verbally asking him for help is permissible when understood as a request for his intercession before Allah.
  • It becomes kufr only if one attributes independent divine agency or worships the intermediary.

Evidences and practice

  • Historical practice and juristic precedent cited: companions and later jurists used intercessory language; classical authors and devotional poets employed direct address.
  • No direct Qurʾanic or hadith texts were quoted in the recorded segments, but scholars invoked the doctrinal concept of shafāʿah (intercession) and historical practice.

Differences of opinion and audience considerations

  • Learned speakers: may use devotional direct address with acceptable theological intent.
  • Laypeople: scholars advise avoiding ambiguous formulations that mimic pagan idioms or could be misread as shirk.
  • Some scholars accept figurative interpretation to avoid takfīr, others permit explicit practice with caution; many counsel restraint in public language.

Practical action items

  • Prefer clear formulations: e.g., “O Allah, by the intercession of Your Prophet, grant me…,” or “O Prophet, pray to Allah for me.”
  • Educate laypeople about the correct intention and avoid ambiguous public phrases that may mislead.
  • Do not declare believers disbelievers for imprecise devotional language; correct gently and teach.
  • Avoid attributing creative power to intermediaries; speak of them as means ordained by Allah.

Short decision/checklist for practitioners and contract drafters

  • Sale contracts:
  • Explicitly state inclusion/exclusion of water present at contract time and specify which trees/offspring are excluded.
  • If land is enclosed, state the precise right‑of‑way (which side/path), or secure an easement in writing.
  • When reserving part of a property, expressly document any right of passage needed by the reserving party.
  • Purity & food:
  • Inspect produce for bad taste/odor/visible taint; if none, majority permits consumption; if present, avoid or follow stricter madhhab.
  • For animals fed on impure food: rehabilitate by feeding pure fodder for the time customary in the school before consuming.
  • Mosques & building materials:
  • Do not use bricks/clay visibly mixed with filth in mosque construction; remove if used and feasible.
  • Prevent spreading or praying upon known impurities in the mosque.
  • Tawassul:
  • Use safe, unambiguous formulations when addressing the Prophet or saints; emphasize Allah as sole grantor.
  • Teach correct intention to avoid misunderstandings; prefer explicit phrasing in public contexts.
  • Disputes:
  • Gather witnesses and measurements; make concrete claims when quantities are uncertain; be prepared for oath/evidentiary procedures.
