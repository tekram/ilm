Crescent Sighting Rules - July 15, 2025

Start: 2025-07-15T17:00:32

Subject

Acceptance of crescent (hilal) sighting vs. astronomical calculation — when to accept or reject eyewitness testimony (focus: morning sighting on the 29th, evening/30th conflicting testimony)

Key definitions and legal concepts

  • ʿAdl (justice): outward, known uprightness of a witness; required in classical Shāfiʿī testimony-acceptance.
  • Imkān al-ruʾyah (possibility of sighting): whether it was physically possible to see the crescent from the witness’s location/time.
  • Tawātur: mass, concordant transmission producing near-certainty; if reached, it is decisive.
  • Hisāb (calculation): astronomical computation about moon visibility.
  • Qatʿī vs. zannī: certain (definitive) proof cannot be opposed by probabilistic testimony.
  • Darūra (necessity): legitimate grounds to relax usual evidentiary rules (permitted in real necessity, narrowly defined).
  • Ghālabat al-dhann: predominance of probability — insufficient to overturn a qatʿī proof.
  • Al‑afdal (the better of available witnesses): minority allowance in some schools to accept the best witnesses when full ʿadl is lacking (limited, contested).

Evidences cited

  • Hadith: “If you see it, fast; and if you do not see it, complete thirty” — invoked as the primary rule favoring sighting (reported in authentic collections).
  • Qur’an: distinction between muhkam and mutashabih (Q 3:7) — used methodologically to prioritize decisive texts/evidence over doubtful appearances.
  • Classical juristic authorities referenced: Ibn Hajar, Ibn ʿAbd al‑Salam, al‑Nawawī, Abu Hanīfa’s school, Ibn ʿAllān, Habib Tahir, Habib Alawi al‑Haddad, and regional Hadramawt scholars. Their opinions structure the legal debate.

Core legal questions

  • Can eyewitness testimony of the crescent be accepted when:
  • it contradicts astronomical calculation showing impossibility; or
  • a morning sighting (before sunrise) was reported earlier on the same 29th day; or
  • witnesses are not fully ʿadl or are few (less than tawātur)?

Main rulings and legal reasoning (concise)

  • Evening testimony by non‑ʿadl witnesses: invalid and should not be relied upon.
  • Morning sighting reported the morning of the 29th (before sunrise) usually undermines later evening claims of a prior night sighting; judges should annul conflicting evening testimony.
  • If calculation (hisāb) by competent calculators establishes with certainty (qatʿī) that sighting was impossible, eyewitness testimony that contradicts it should be rejected — a probabilistic testimony cannot overturn a certain demonstration.
  • Where calculation is not definitive or is contested, eyewitness testimony from known ʿadl witnesses may be given weight — but acceptance remains disputed among jurists.
  • Tawātur of reliable witnesses or of calculators’ reports is decisive: if present, it establishes the fact regardless of opposing probabilistic claims.
  • Darūra: relaxing standards (accepting non‑ideal testimony or "the best available") is permitted only for true necessity (e.g., preserving lineage, essential financial rights). Ordinary hilal determination is generally not such a necessity in the Shāfiʿī framework.
  • Optical/psychological errors are common; when ordinary experience or expert calculation shows impossibility, one must prefer the impossibility and complete the month.

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • Unanimous/majority points:
  • Do not accept testimony from non‑ʿadl witnesses.
  • Reject testimony that contradicts decisive calculation or clear impossibility.
  • Divided issues:
  • Whether to accept eyewitness testimony when calculation indicates improbability but not definitive impossibility:
  • Some jurists accept testimony (favoring sight).
  • Others reject it.
  • Ibn Hajar’s middle position: reject the testimony only when the calculators’ conclusion is established (preferably with strong transmission/tawātur); otherwise the testimony may stand.
  • Whether judges may accept “the best available” witnesses (al‑afdal) when full ʿadl is lacking:
  • Accepted by some later jurists (in limited contexts); rejected by many classical Shāfiʿīs for hilal matters.
  • Reliance on contemporary astronomers vs. transmitted classical rulings:
  • Some require modern expert hisāb; others accept established classical texts/consensus when impossibility is already documented.

Practical action items (for judges, muftis, community leaders, individuals)

  • Always assess physical possibility (imkān) before accepting a crescent report:
  • Consult competent astronomers/calculators when calculation is decisive.
  • Use classical transmitted rulings/experience where the impossibility is well established and documented.
  • Do not accept:
  • Evening testimonies from witnesses who are not known ʿadl.
  • Testimony claiming a night sighting when a credible morning sighting (before sunrise) was already established, except where the night testimony reaches practical certainty (tawātur by distribution/status making collusion impossible).
  • Testimony that claims sighting when ordinary experience or decisive calculation shows it was impossible (e.g., moon too close to sun).
  • In doubt, adopt the precautionary rule: complete the month to 30 days rather than begin the next month on dubious testimony.
  • Examine witnesses’ motives and past behavior; exclude known reckless or self‑seeking testifiers unless they meet conditions of repentance and verification.
  • Preserve communal unity:
  • Follow the established local scholarly/madhhab practice.
  • Communicate transparently to the public why certain testimonies are accepted/rejected.
  • When necessary (e.g., marriage), follow the school’s rules allowing acceptance of the best available witnesses only where the law requires testimony and no real darūra is otherwise present.
  • Reconcile conflicting reports if a plausible temporal/locational explanation exists; if reconciliation is impossible, treat mutual impossibility as cancelling both testimonies.

Practical examples & illustrative cautions

  • Optical illusions (bright planets, vapors, sun reflections, birds, tree ridges) frequently cause honest mistakes; mass assertion is not proof.
  • Historical cases show even pious, respected witnesses may be mistaken; careful verification is required.
  • Hadramawt practice: many local Shāfiʿī scholars historically rejected accepting evening testimony when a morning sighting had been established.

Bottom line (concise)

  • Priority: decisive evidence (tawātur or qatʿī calculation/common-sense impossibility) over probabilistic eyewitness testimony.
  • Sight is the default criterion per the Prophetic formula, but it does not trump a demonstrable impossibility proved by reliable calculation or overwhelming transmission.
  • Judges should be cautious: reject non‑ʿadl and mutually impossible testimonies, prefer completion of 30 days in doubt, consult specialists and local scholarly consensus, and avoid decisions that rely on dubious appearances.
