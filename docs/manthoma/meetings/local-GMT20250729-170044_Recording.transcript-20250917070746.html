Hajj Hiring and Wages - July 29, 2025

📅 2025-07-29 🕐 17:00

Case: Hired pilgrim (ajir) died after standing at Arafah

  • Facts
  • Employer hired a man (from Hadhramaut) to perform Hajj/Umrah for him for 150 qirsh.
  • The hired man performed ihram and wuqūf (standing at Arafah) but died thereafter. Question: how to apportion the wage, what remains to be done for completion, and whether the deceased’s statement that he completed the rites is accepted.

Key rulings (concise)

  • Principal acts done (ihram and wuqūf/Arafah) carry the most weight; the ajir (or his heirs) is entitled to most of the agreed wage.
  • Value the remaining unperformed acts (stoning, overnight stay, tawaf, sa‘y). Deduct that reasonable value from the agreed wage; pay the balance to the deceased’s heirs.
  • Illustrative example from class: total 150 qirsh → remaining acts valued ~15 → heirs receive ~135 (figures illustrative, not prescriptive).
  • If the ajir had already performed Umrah before Hajj (tamattu‘), less remains to be done → heirs receive more.
  • If heirs cannot pay to finish the remaining rites, it is preferable to hire a local pilgrim (e.g., from Mecca/Jeddah) to complete the remaining rites and pay from the residual funds or other available money so the rites are definitely completed.

Acceptance of the ajir’s testimony

  • General rule: the ajir’s own report that he performed the rites is accepted when:
  • He performed personally (was not merely an intermediary);
  • The hire was a valid ijarah (classical texts apply rule to both ajir ayn and ajir dhammi).
  • Exceptions — do not accept his bare statement when:
  • The claim is manifestly impossible by circumstance (e.g., claiming to be at Arafah at an impossible time/place);
  • He acted as a wakīl/agent (deputized another) — an agent’s bare report about legal acts is not automatically proof;
  • The hiring contract was invalid/vitiated — classical rules about accepting ajir’s claim may not apply.

Definitions & concepts

  • Ajir: a person hired to perform acts of worship (Hajj/Umrah) on behalf of another.
  • Ajir ayn vs ajir dhammi: hire where worker personally performs (ayn) vs a hire framed as payment of a debt (dhammi). Classical jurisprudence treats the evidentiary rule as applying to both.
  • Wuqūf at Arafah: the principal pillar (rukn) of Hajj — carries decisive weight in determining completion.
  • Tamattu‘: performing Umrah during the Hajj season before Hajj; if done by the ajir, it reduces outstanding obligations.

Ijarah (hiring) — validity, wages, and omissions

  • Conditions of a valid ijarah:
  • Parties must know the pillars (arkan), obligatory acts (wajibat), and relevant sunnah for the ritual contracted.
  • If essential contractual conditions are missing or parties are ignorant of required acts, the ijarah may be void.
  • Wage entitlement
  • Valid contract + correct performance → worker entitled to agreed (named) wage.
  • Void contract but worker unaware of invalidity → entitled to fair wage (ujrat al-mithl) for work done.
  • If worker knew contract was void:
  • Ibn Hajar: no wage due.
  • al-Ramlī: entitled to fair wage (difference of opinion).
  • Omissions and their legal consequences
  • Pillars (arkan) omitted → pilgrimage invalid for the principal; no wage for a pilgrimage that failed in its pillar.
  • Obligatory acts (wajibat) omitted → require dam (sacrifice). Cost/value of dam is to be borne (deducted from wage or owed by worker).
  • Sunnah omitted → no dam, but the wage should be proportionally reduced.
  • Liability for hiring an incompetent/non-ʿadil worker
  • If the hired person is not reliable and this causes harm, responsibility may revert to the original employer (or the one who engaged the incompetent person). Verify competency before contracting.

Agent (wakīl) testimony: worship vs transactions

  • Transactions/legal acts: an agent’s bare declaration that he completed an act is generally not accepted as proof; the principal must provide evidence (document/witness).
  • Acts of worship: scholars in these lessons adopt a more lenient, practical stance — an agent’s (or hired pilgrim’s) report about worship performed may be accepted more readily, even if he deputized someone else. Still, key exceptions (impossibility, manifest invalidity) apply.
  • Practical rule: accept agent’s report for worshipal delegations when reliable and not impossible; be strict (demand proof) in financial/transactional disputes.

Hiring a pilgrim for someone prevented (maḥjūz) or by bequest

  • It is permissible to hire a just (ʿadl) and trustworthy pilgrim from Jeddah/Makkah to perform Hajj on behalf of a person unable to go (deceased or prevented), including via an ijārat adh-dhimmah arrangement.
  • Executor/guardian should seek a reliable pilgrim who accepts the bequest amount; document the arrangement; the reward is counted for the prevented person.
  • If bequest amount is manifestly insufficient, seek a practical solution (hire locally, adjust compensation) while preserving the deceased’s interest.

Zakat/charity and agent priorities

  • Agents/executors must prioritize deserving recipients (the truly poor, pious, needy, scholars) over relatives who manufacture needs.
  • Do not use zakāh income to pay relatives’ private debts unjustifiably when other eligible recipients exist.
  • If funds are the owner’s property (not zakāh), the owner’s explicit instructions govern; otherwise act for the deceased’s best spiritual/material interest.

Purity issue: foam/bubbles (zubd/raghwa) after urine in abundant water

  • Two main positions
  • Impurity view: foam that clearly originates from urine (indicated by color, smell, taste, witness) is najis; droplets from it render clothes impure.
  • Purity view: foam rising on a large body of water is treated as part of the abundant water and is tahir unless there is proof it came from the impurity.
  • Criteria and principles
  • Large water (kathrat al-ma’): dilution and dispersal tend to preserve purity unless impurity predominates or is evident.
  • Change (ghayr mutaghayyir vs mutaghayyir): if foam is materially changed by contamination (visible/smellable), treat as impure.
  • Standards of proof differ: some jurists require certainty (yaqīn); others accept strong probability or reliable witness.
  • Practical guidance
  • If foam shows signs of impurity (color, smell) → treat as impure and wash.
  • If no sign and water is abundant → majority position: treat as pure.
  • If in doubt and the matter affects ritual purity needed now, prefer using clearly clean water or avoid the suspicious foam.
  • Small sealed containers briefly immersed remain impure; prolonged mixing in large water may purify.

Methodology for issuing fatwas (pedagogical points)

  • Start with a concise verdict then give a short reason.
  • Use Qur’an and hadith when helpful to reassure laypeople; cite madhhab reasoning and juristic precedent for students.
  • Do not present transmitted fatwas as automatically conclusive — indicate strength and differences of opinion.
  • Tailor detail level to the questioner: simple rulings for laypeople, textual/scholarly evidence for students.
  • When evidence is disputed or technical, offer a practical compromise and a clear precaution.

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • Acceptance of ajir’s testimony: majority accepts ajir’s personal report; exceptions for impossibility, deputization, or invalid contract.
  • Wage when worker knew a contract was void: Ibn Hajar (no wage) vs al-Ramlī (fair wage).
  • Purity of foam: juristic split between impurity when foam clearly stems from urine vs treating foam from abundant water as pure absent proof.
  • Agent testimony: stricter in transactions, more lenient in worship matters (practical tolerance), but not unconditional.

Practical checklist / action items (for employers, heirs, executors, muftis)

  • For a hired pilgrim who died after Arafah:
  • Itemize which pillars/obligatory acts were completed and which remain.
  • Estimate the reasonable market cost/value of remaining rites; deduct that from the agreed wage; pay the balance to heirs.
  • If heirs/employer cannot fund completion, hire a local pilgrim to finish rites and pay from residual/new funds.
  • Accept the deceased ajir’s report if he acted personally and the claim is not impossible; require proof if he deputized someone or circumstances make the claim improbable.
  • Before hiring a pilgrim:
  • Specify in contract: the pillars, obligations, agreed wage, and consequences for omissions (who pays dam; how reductions calculated).
  • Verify the pilgrim’s competence and ʿadl (integrity).
  • In disputes over agent reports:
  • For worshipal matters, accept a reliable agent’s report with caution; for transactions, require documentary/witness proof.
  • For purity cases involving foam:
  • Inspect for signs; if none and water is large, rely on presumption of purity; if signs present or strong witness, treat as impure and clean.
  • For muftis:
  • Give succinct verdicts, note differences of opinion, and advise reasonable practical precautions.
