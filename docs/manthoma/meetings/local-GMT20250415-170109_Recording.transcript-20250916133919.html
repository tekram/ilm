Impurity of Dead Bones - April 15, 2025

Start: 2025-04-15T17:01:09

Topic and definitions

  • Topic: Setting a broken bone (jabr kassar al-ʿaẓm) using bone or other materials from dead animals or otherwise impure sources — rulings, conditions, evidences, differences, and practical guidance.
  • Key terms:
  • Jabr kassar al-ʿaẓm: splinting/grafting to set or repair a broken bone.
  • ʿAẓm mayyita: bone of a dead animal (non‑fish/non‑locust); includes discussion of dog and pig bones.
  • Carrying impurity “in ghayr maʿdinah”: impurity located outside its original receptacle (on limb, clothing, not internal).
  • Darura (necessity): juridical principle permitting what is otherwise forbidden to avert serious harm.
  • Zahir / Batin: external (visible) vs internal (covered/incorporated) placement.

Main ruling — permissibility and limits

  • Necessity basis:
  • Setting a human broken bone with material from a dead animal (including, in exigency, a dog’s bone) is permissible when necessary and no pure alternative is available — necessity (darura) excuses the normally prohibited impurity to the extent required.
  • Order of preference:
  • Prefer the patient’s own bone and pure/halal-sourced materials first.
  • Prefer human bone grafts over animal bone when feasible.
  • Prefer temporary/biodegradable aids (fat, gut sutures, dissolving adhesives, threads) over permanent rigid implants when effective.
  • Use animal bone only as last resort if delay would cause loss of organ/function.
  • Dog and pig bones:
  • Both are generally classed impure; using them is only allowed under strict necessity with no pure alternative and medical justification. Jurists differ on priority between dog and pig due to different textual supports.
  • Human bone:
  • Many jurists permit using human bone (cadaveric or other) in necessity; some scholars restrict or forbid transference of human parts (minority positions exist).

Prayer, purification, and ritual consequences

  • Prayer validity:
  • If the impure splint is necessary and removal would cause harm or destroy therapeutic benefit, the person’s prayer is valid; removal is not required in that case.
  • A person who used an impure splint out of necessity and without blame may serve as imam (subject to local scholarly guidance).
  • When removal is required:
  • If removal can be done without harming the patient or destroying the medical benefit, the impure splint must be removed; leaving it is not permitted solely because of bearable pain.
  • Private individuals should advise; only the public authority (al‑hakim) may enforce compulsory removal when required.
  • Concealed vs manifest impurity:
  • If the impurity is external/manifest (visible), carrying it in a non‑original place generally invalidates wudu/prayer and must be removed if removal is feasible.
  • If the foreign bone is fully internal and incorporated by flesh (covered), many jurists treat it as not obligating removal or invalidating prayer; there is dispute and case-by-case assessment is required.
  • Water and contact:
  • If an unattached impurity contacted water before being covered, the water becomes impure and should not be used for purification.
  • If a person is legitimately excused in keeping the impurity (e.g., removal would harm), touched wet things are treated leniently as part of the person.

Conditions and limits for removal (summary of juristic tests)

  • Removal is obligatory only if all the following hold:
  • The person is mukallaf (adult, sane).
  • The impurity is external (or not truly incorporated) and carries ritual impurity.
  • Removal is possible without causing significant harm or destroying medical benefit.
  • The impurity was not incorporated (covered by flesh) or otherwise rendered part of the body.
  • Excuses that permit leaving the impurity:
  • Removal would cause significant harm or loss of function.
  • The impurity has become incorporated/covered by skin/flesh.
  • The person was coerced, a child at time of attachment, insane — many jurists excuse these statuses.
  • Authority:
  • Only the ruler or his lawful agent may enforce removal when required; family members may advise but not forcibly extract.

Evidences and legal basis

  • Primary legal method: principle of necessity (darura), qiyas, and juristic precedent.
  • Hadith/Qurʾanic references mentioned in the lessons:
  • Dog impurity: hadith on washing a vessel licked by a dog — wash seven times, one with earth (Sahih Muslim) supports ritual impurity of dog saliva.
  • Pig prohibition: Qur’anic prohibition of pork cited as basis for pig’s ritual disallowance (e.g., Qur’an on forbidden foods); jurists differ on pig’s ritual impurity vs. prohibition to eat.
  • General necessity principle in Qur’an (e.g., “He has only forbidden you what dies of itself…” and the dispensation in cases of necessity; see Qur’an 2:173 as the overarching principle).
  • No single explicit textual hadith about bone grafting; rulings are juristic applications of general principles and transmitted scholarly opinions (Ibn Hajar, al‑Ramli, Majmaʿ al‑Fiqh al‑Islami, etc.).

Differences of opinion (major points)

  • Use of human bone from corpse for another person: many permit in necessity; some scholars prohibit transfer of human parts.
  • Priority between dog vs pig bone: disputed — some prioritize avoiding dog (explicit hadith), others treat pig as worse by Qur’anic prohibition.
  • Whether an impurity fully covered/incorporated invalidates prayer: jurists disagree; many treat incorporated impurity as part of the body and excused.
  • Obligation to remove when attachment occurred without fault:
  • Ibn Hajar: removal becomes obligatory once feasible and harmless.
  • al‑Ramli/al‑Khatib: if attachment was made when no pure option existed, later availability of pure option does not necessarily obligate removal.
  • Whether touching bone nullifies wudu: disputed; apply local madhhab rulings or consult a scholar.

Practical action items (medical & lay guidance)

  • Emergency/clinicians:
  • In emergencies where delay causes serious harm, use the nearest effective material — if only impure bone is available, necessity permits its use.
  • Prefer patient’s own bone or human grafts; document medical necessity and attempts to seek pure options.
  • Prefer temporary biodegradable materials when medically effective.
  • For patients and families:
  • Do not forcibly remove medical devices or grafts yourself; seek professional and legal guidance.
  • If removal is medically safe and does not compromise healing, remove the impure material to restore ritual purity.
  • If removal would harm, keep it and consider the prayer valid; consult a local scholar.
  • If you refuse removal despite no impediment, inform/seek the public authority (ruler/judge) to enforce removal according to sharʿī rules.
  • For imamate and community worship:
  • A person excused by necessity may lead prayer; confirm with local scholar if contested.

Related topic: Tattoos (al‑washm) — definition and rulings

Definitions and evidences

  • Tattooing (washm): piercing the skin until blood appears and implanting dye so the skin remains colored.
  • Hadith: the Prophet cursed the tattooer and the tattooed (authentic collections); hadiths on the pen lifted from children and pardon for coercion are used to excuse children/coerced persons.

Rulings

  • Adult voluntary tattoo (after mukallaf): removal (kashṭ/scraping or medical removal) is required when removal causes no harm.
  • Child, coerced, insane, unconscious when tattooed: excused; removal is not required if harmful.
  • If a tattoo prevents water reaching the skin for wudu/ghusl or contains impure mixture, removal is required unless removal causes real harm.
  • If removal would cause serious harm, leniency applies (may rely on tayammum if needed).

Differences and practical items

  • Ibn Hajar: removal obligatory if feasible without harm.
  • al‑Baghawī: medical treatment that later removes the mark suffices.
  • Practical: arrange medically safe removal for voluntary adult tattoos; document coercion/childhood cases; consult local scholar regarding imamate and rituals if marks remain.

Related: Embedded impure substances and healed contaminated wounds

  • If a wound was treated/covered with najis substance and later flesh grew over it (adhesion): treated akin to tattooing.
  • Removal is required if voluntary and removal feasible without harm; excused if removal would harm or if incorporation occurred.
  • Practical: consult both doctor and scholar; if removal risks health, prioritize medical safety and seek scholarly dispensation.

Related: Cosmetic practices (hair, henna, piercings)

  • Hair extensions (wasl): generally disfavored/forbidden by many jurists when it involves attaching another person’s hair or deception; synthetic and pure materials are more acceptable.
  • Henna (khidb): generally permitted for women (hands/feet); black dye (sawd) treated more strictly and often prohibited.
  • Eyebrow plucking / permanent alteration: plucking often regarded as disliked or forbidden; opinions vary by method and madhhab.
  • Ear piercing: juristic differences — many allow for girls, dispute for boys.
  • Practical: prefer permissible, non‑deceptive beautification; consult local scholarly guidance for contested practices.

Inheritance case (found money in deceased’s house) — concise rulings

  • The 32,000 dinars found belongs to the deceased’s estate and must be distributed per inheritance law.
  • Money previously taken:
  • If a wife took rightful maintenance (nafqa) during illness, that is allowed; excess taken unlawfully must be returned.
  • If an entitled heir took unlawfully, they are liable to return the excess.
  • Remedies:
  • Prefer legal recovery through courts when feasible.
  • If recovery is impractical, distribution of the found money among heirs is permissible with accounting and adjustment against those who previously took unlawfully (pragmatic “dhafr”).
  • Charity given by the taker does not replace restitution.
  • Practical steps: document amounts and evidence; pursue judicial recovery if possible; if not, distribute estate fairly and record claims against takers; consult a local scholar and civil lawyer as needed.

Brief note: Lying under necessity

  • Principle: lying is generally prohibited but narrowly permitted in genuine necessity after exhausting lawful alternatives (silence, evasion, hinting).
  • Evidence: limited permissive hadiths (e.g., categories where conciliatory deception is allowed — Bukhari/Muslim); Qur’anic principle of dispensation in necessity (e.g., 2:173).
  • Practical: attempt evasive/indirect means first; only permit a proportionate falsehood when it is the only effective means to avert real, imminent harm and it will not produce greater injustice. Consult a scholar when in doubt.

Quick practical checklist (decision flow for bone-graft cases)

  • Is the patient’s life/organ/function at risk if you delay? If yes → necessity may permit using available material.
  • Can a pure/human graft be reached or prepared in time without causing harm? If yes → prefer that.
  • Will removal later (if you used impure material now) destroy benefit or cause serious harm? If yes → leaving it is excused; prayer valid.
  • Is the impurity external/visible and removable now without harm? If yes → removal is required for ritual purity.
  • Is the impurity fully incorporated/covered by flesh? If yes → many jurists treat it as part of the body and removal is not required.
  • Document medical necessity and consult a local mufti for contested cases (dog/pig bone, human-to-human transference, imamate).
  • Do not forcibly remove medical devices; involve treating physicians and legal authority if enforcement is needed.

Final summary (core takeaway)

  • The guiding rule: save life and limb first — necessity permits use of otherwise prohibited impure materials to prevent serious harm, but preserve ritual purity when removal is feasible and safe. Many juristic differences exist on details (dog vs pig, human-bone transfers, when removal becomes obligatory, and effect on wudu/imamah). In all contested or risky cases consult medical experts and a competent local scholar; document decisions and prioritize patient safety.
