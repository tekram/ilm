Definitions & technical terms

  • Kharz / khaf makhruz (الخَرز / الخف المخروز): stitching or pegging of a shoe (the sewn shoe).
  • Askāf / askāfī (الأسكاف / الأسكافية): shoemaker.
  • Līf (الليف): plant fiber (palm fiber, coir) used as substitute thread.
  • Shīta / mashṭ (شيته / مشط): comb-like tool used in processing flax/linen (sometimes made with pig hair).
  • Najāsah (النجاسة): ritual impurity; wet contact typically transmits impurity.
  • Istinjāʼ: cleaning after relieving oneself.
  • Taʻāfu / ʿAfw (تَعَافُ/عَفْو): legal excusal/leniency for very small amounts of impurity.
  • Istihālah: transformation that may alter legal status of a substance.
  • Al‑asl fil‑ashyāʼ al‑ṭahārah: legal presumption that things are pure unless impurity is proven.

Topic 1 — Use of pig hair in shoemaking and related tools

Question

Is it permissible for a shoemaker to stitch shoes with pig hair, or to use combs/tools made of pig hair, given the pig is ritually impure?

Key rulings (three main positions)

  • Lenient/professional exception:
  • Some reports (attributed in parts to Abu Hanifa or transmitted within Hanafi circles) permit the craftsman (askāf) to use pig hair out of necessity for sewing shoes. This is a narrow professional/necessity allowance.
  • General prohibition (stronger view):
  • Pig hair is considered najis (impure); its use in stitching or in tools that touch garments is forbidden when impurity is established because it remains impure and is difficult to purify (analogy to hadithic prescriptions requiring multiple washings). Many commentators favour prohibition.
  • Restricted leniency / compromise:
  • Permit only in necessity or for the craftsman; otherwise require pure alternatives (līf, linen, cotton). This is a common practical compromise in Hanafi post-classical discussions.

Evidences and reasoning cited in the lesson

  • Pig hair classified as impurity; purification would be difficult — analogy made to the hadithic rule of washing seven times (one with earth) for certain severe impurities (lesson cites the hadith about washing a vessel licked by a dog).
  • Transmission and practical manuals: some Shāfiʿī and Hanafi commentators adopt practical allowances in daily craft, but treat certainty of impurity as decisive—wet contact transmits najāsah.
  • Presumption of purity (asl) used where only doubt exists: mere suspicion does not automatically render the product impure.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • Abu Hanifa: reports vary — some allow the craftsman in need, others record prohibition.
  • Hanafi commentators: mixed—some allow limited professional use; others insist on prohibition when impurity is proven.
  • Imam Aḥmad (Ibn Hanbal): reported to forbid sewing with pig hair; recommends līf as substitute.
  • Shāfiʿī commentators: practically more lenient in some manuals, but they require proof for impurity and rely on presumption of purity in doubtful cases.

Practical action items

  • Prefer pure materials (līf, linen, cotton) for stitching shoes, combs, and any tool that will touch clothing or items used in worship.
  • If pig hair is used:
  • Treat it as impure when impurity is established (especially if it contacted cloth while wet); avoid using such footwear in prayer unless your school permits in necessity or the impurity has been removed.
  • If only doubtful, apply your school’s rule: many schools keep presumption of purity—do not assume contamination without certainty.
  • When dealing with a known pig-hair comb applied to textiles: wash the affected textile if wet contact occurred; if only doubtful, prefer replacement or professional consultation.
  • Consult a qualified scholar of your madhhab for local rulings and, where feasible, replace suspect materials with unambiguous substitutes.

Topic 2 — Excusal (ʿAfw) for small amounts of impurity

Main question

Are very small quantities of najāsah excused so purification is not required before prayer?

Two principal positions

  • Broad excusal (reported to Abu Hanifa): any impurity below a dirham‑al‑baghli measure (classical small coin-size) is excused.
  • Restricted excusal (majority practical position): limit excusal mainly to small amounts of blood; for urine and feces (and major impurities) there is no general excusal — even small traces must be removed.

Measures and practical criteria

  • Classical measures cited in transmissions: palm-size, dirham-size, a span (shibr), and in some extreme reports a cubit — the palm/dirham measures are most commonly adopted practically.
  • Where quantitative measurement is uncertain, rely on local custom (ʿurf) and err on the side of purification.

Evidences cited in the lesson

  • Hadith referenced (as cited in the lesson): report in the Ṣaḥīḥayn about two graves and punishment, with one cause linked to failure to purify from urine — used to argue against broad leniency for urine.
  • Hadithic practice of washing seven times (one with earth) cited as proof of severity for some impurities (analogy to dog-licked vessel hadith).

Rulings on animal excrement (rooth)

  • Dung of animals whose meat is eaten: some Hanafi reports allow leniency for very small amounts; mainstream practical rulings are cautious.
  • Dung of animals whose meat is not eaten (predators, etc.): generally treated as impure with no broad excusal.

Practical action items

  • Do not adopt broad leniency for urine and feces; clean any trace you can before prayer.
  • For blood: small amounts below the local dirham/palm measure may be excused in common Hanafi practice; larger amounts require washing.
  • For animal dung: determine animal type; if meat is customarily eaten and stain is very small, some leniency exists—otherwise clean.
  • When in doubt about size or removal, prefer purification (clean the spot, change/clean garments or khuffs).
  • Follow your school/region’s practiced position; consult a scholar for difficult cases.

Topic 3 — Additional Hanafi purity points from the lesson (summary)

Internalized impurity (eaten impurities)

  • If an impurity is eaten and undergoes transformation (istihālah) in the stomach, many Hanafi texts treat the resulting substance like ordinary food; rulings are more lenient for the person.
  • For clear major impurities (najāsa mughallaẓa, e.g., pork), stricter rules apply: rinse mouth; for utensils follow classical prescriptions (seven washings, one with earth) as cited in the lesson.
  • If impurity was consumed deliberately, classical advice includes expelling it if possible; if by compulsion/forgetfulness the ruling is mitigated.

Eggs of animals whose meat is not eaten

  • Eggs are generally tahir (pure) and permitted even if the parent animal’s flesh is not eaten (e.g., some birds, reptiles), unless there is real fear of harm (poison). Eggs may be sold and eaten by default.

Istinjāʼ methods and dog-skin

  • Valid methods: stones (ḥijārah) and water are accepted for istinjāʼ.
  • Dog-skin as a cleaning method was transmitted in a minority report but is rejected in practice by the lesson — do not rely on dog‑skin for purification.

Presumption of purity & reputation (shuhrah)

  • Default legal principle: things are presumed pure unless impurity is proven. Mere rumor or suspicion that pig fat/skin was used (e.g., in cheese) does not automatically make the product impure.

Practical action items

  • Use stones or water for istinjāʼ; do not rely on dog skin.
  • Eat or sell eggs by default unless credible harm is suspected.
  • When buying cheese or garments from non‑Muslims, maintain presumption of purity unless you have firm evidence of impurity.
  • If you consumed a clear major impurity, act according to the stricter classical guidance (clean mouth, cleanse utensils, repentance).

Consolidated practical checklist (daily practice)

  • Avoid pig-hair threads/combs for items touching clothing or used in worship; prefer palm-fiber, linen, cotton.
  • If pig hair was used and impurity is certain (especially wet contact), treat the contact as najis and clean or avoid using the item in ṣalāh.
  • For small stains:
  • Urine/feces: clean regardless of smallness when possible.
  • Blood: small amounts may be excused in Hanafi practice; larger amounts require washing.
  • Animal dung: identify animal; if meat is eaten and stain extremely small, leniency may apply; otherwise clean.
  • After ingesting doubtful food: avoid initially; if discovered to be haram, repent and avoid repeating. For definite major impurities (pork) follow stricter cleansing of mouth/utensils.
  • Use stones or water for istinjāʼ. If you mistakenly used dog-skin, repeat proper cleaning when possible.
  • In doubtful cases, follow your madhhab and consult a qualified scholar.

Evidences and sources (as cited in the lesson)

  • Hadiths cited in the lesson:
  • Hadith about graves being punished (reported in the Ṣaḥīḥayn) used to caution against leniency with urine.
  • Hadith about washing an object seven times (one with earth) invoked analogically for severe impurities (cf. hadith on dog-licked vessel).
  • Classical fiqh works referenced: al‑Majmūʿ, al‑Jawāhir, and post-classical Hanafi commentaries; also practical manuals (al‑Rawdah, Tuhfat al‑Muhtāj) were cited in commentary.

Differences of opinion — concise recap

  • Use of pig hair: permissive for craftsmen in necessity (some Hanafi reports) vs. general prohibition (many commentators and Imam Aḥmad); compromise permits only necessity or craftsman use, otherwise forbid.
  • ʿAfw (excusal) limits: Abu Hanifa’s broader report vs. common practical restriction (esp. for urine/feces); measurements vary across transmissions.
  • Istinjāʼ materials: stones/water accepted; dog-skin transmission exists but is not relied upon.
  • Eggs and goods from non‑Muslims: default presumption of purity vs. objections where proof of impurity exists.
