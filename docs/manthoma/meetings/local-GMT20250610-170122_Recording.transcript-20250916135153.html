1. Imagining or visual transformation of gender — legal effect

Definition / principle

  • Legal rulings follow the person’s real/biological legal sex, not mere imagination, pictorial depiction, or non‑diagnostic appearance.

Key rulings

  • Imagining someone as the opposite sex does not change rulings on wudu, awrah, marriage, or other ritual matters.
  • Surgical/medical changes do not automatically change the legal sex for ritual rulings in the presented opinion; cases of a biological male truly menstruating or becoming pregnant are treated skeptically unless fully proven.
  • If a person’s appearance (natural or altered) provokes sexual desire (fitna), looking at them may become impermissible to prevent temptation — this is a precaution based on preventing fitna, not a change in legal sex.

Differences of opinion

  • The excerpt does not elaborate formal divergent juristic positions; the instructor favors following biological/legal reality over imagination or appearance.

Practical actions

  • Apply rulings according to the person’s real/legal sex.
  • Avoid looking if the appearance provokes desire; instruct others likewise.
  • Do not rely on imagination or pictures to determine nullification of wudu or similar rulings.

2. Menstruation (hayd) after suspected pregnancy / miscarriage — specific case

Case summary

  • Woman had ~1.5 months without bleeding (suspected pregnancy), then 13 days of painful bleeding (suspected miscarriage) with no expelled products seen. Bleeding stopped, then returned before 15 days of purity completed.

Ruling given

  • Absent confirmed proof of childbirth/delivery, treat that bleeding episode as hayd (menstrual bleeding).

Practical actions

  • The woman should refrain from prayer and fasting during the bleeding treated as hayd.
  • If medical evidence (expelled fetal tissue, doctor confirmation) becomes available, revisit the ruling.

Notes

  • The ruling given is categorical in the excerpt; detailed Hanafi thresholds (mumayyiza distinctions, specific counting rules) should be applied if further details are provided.

3. Hayd, istihādah and habitual cycles (muʿtādah)

Definitions

  • Hayd: menstrual bleeding that forbids prayer and fasting.
  • Istihādah: non‑menstrual or prolonged bleeding treated as discharge that does not interrupt prayer/fast once classified.
  • Muʿtādah (habitual): woman with a regular, known monthly pattern.

Key principles & rulings

  • Presumption of habit: one follows the established pattern (habit) when in doubt; conjecture (shakk/ẓann) does not overturn established practice.
  • If bleeding exceeds the woman’s usual hayd and continues, the excess is commonly treated as istihādah; she resumes her habitual counting of hayd/tahāra.
  • Classical Hanafi benchmarks: hayd is generally limited to a maximum of 15 days; days beyond that are treated as istihādah for practical counting (application depends on specifics).
  • If a woman is mumayyiza (able to distinguish menstrual blood from other discharge), different rules apply and require case-by-case application.

Juristic discussion / evidence

  • References to classical fiqh works and commentators (Ibn Yahyā, Ibn al‑Qāsim, al‑Mubṣūt, al‑Majmūʿ) about whether to “return to the last habit” during istihādah; instructor favors returning to the last established habit.

Practical actions

  • Non‑mumayyiza habitual woman: follow usual hayd days (do not pray/fast) and usual tahūr days (pray/fast); treat continuous excess beyond habit as istihādah.
  • Mumayyiza: consult specific rules or a qualified scholar.
  • Keep medical evidence/documentation when pregnancy/miscarriage is suspected to determine correct classification.

4. Doubt during the prayer (purity or intention)

Key ruling

  • Brief, passing doubt that does not disturb certainty: continue the prayer.
  • Prolonged doubt or sustained hesitation about purity (wudu) or intention: stop, re‑establish certainty (repeat wudu or prayer) to avoid invalid prayer.

Evidence / authorities

  • Cited juristic statements that prolonged doubt in the condition of prayer invalidates it (e.g., al‑Bahjah citations).

Practical actions

  • If you briefly doubt but remain certain overall, continue.
  • If you cannot regain certainty, stop, renew the condition (wudu/intention) and repeat the prayer.

5. Exposure of another’s awrah during congregational prayer

Scenarios & key rulings

  • If a person’s awrah becomes exposed accidentally:
  • If they cover immediately, prayer remains valid.
  • If it remains uncovered, many jurists (and the instructor) consider the prayer invalid.

Differences of opinion

  • Some jurists excuse uncovering caused by wind or other external rare causes (lenient), while others treat all non‑covered exposure as invalidating; variability exists among authorities (al‑Halabī, al‑Barmawī, Ibn al‑Qāsim, al‑Ziyādī, al‑Hafnī). The instructor favors a stricter approach limiting excusing causes (wind mainly).

Practical actions

  • If your awrah is exposed while praying, cover it immediately to preserve the prayer.
  • If you see someone exposed, assist them to cover quickly.
  • When uncertain of the cause, err on the side of covering and, if necessary, repeating the prayer according to the stricter outcome.

6. Latecomer (masbūq) who joins imam in rukūʿ

Key ruling

  • A latecomer who arrives while the imam is in rukūʿ may be counted as having “caught” that rakʿah if he truly and confidently joins the imam and settles with him (yaqīn of joining).
  • If he is uncertain whether he properly joined, the rakʿah is not counted for him.

Evidence / discussion

  • Juristic debate exists about reliance on hearing al‑Fātiḥah vs. physical joining in rukūʿ; instructors reject misapplication of “no prayer without al‑Fātiḥah” to invalidate the masbūq’s joining in these cases.

Practical actions

  • If you confidently join the imam in rukūʿ, count that rakʿah.
  • If unsure, complete prayer cautiously (many recommend adding an extra rakʿah as a precaution).

7. Following imam’s prostration for a recited verse of prostration (sajdat al-tilāwah)

Key rulings

  • If you know for certain the imam did not recite a sajdah verse, do not follow his prostration (it would be a mistaken action).
  • If uncertain whether the imam recited the sajdah verse, follow the imam (to preserve unity and because uncertainty favors following).

Authorities

  • Shaykh ʿAbd al‑Raḥmān and other commentators cited the same practical distinction.

Practical actions

  • Hear or be reasonably sure of the imam’s recitation; if you did not hear clearly, follow the imam when he prostrates.

8. Minute impurities and the ordinary‑sight standard

Core principles

  • Legal assessments of impurity rely on what an average/moderate eye (al‑baṣar al‑muʿtadil) perceives.
  • What is imperceptible to ordinary sight is generally disregarded to avoid undue hardship; presumption of purity (al‑aṣl ṭahāra) applies.
  • Microscopic or technological detection beyond ordinary perception is not normally used to declare impurity, unless a reliable instrument makes the impurity perceptible in a practical sense.

Examples & rulings

  • Insects that carry minute, invisible impurity into oil/water: if no visible impurity results, the bulk remains usable.
  • Many minute particles that are not visible: the excusal generally continues (texts interpreted as covering even if they increase).
  • Bread baked in an oven fuelled by dung: the favored teaching here treats the loaf as permissible; if a visible contaminated crust adheres, wash that part (more cautious positions differ).

Smell vs. sight

  • Smell alone, when no impurity is visible to ordinary sight, does not automatically render an item impure according to the instructor’s practical leaning; opinions differ.

Use of instruments

  • If reliable instruments reveal impurity making it perceptible in a practical sense, the ruling may follow that perception; ordinarily one should not demand lab‑level detection to impose ritual burdens.

Practical actions

  • Use the ordinary‑sight standard; do not declare items impure for hairline or microscopic traces.
  • If you smell impurity but see nothing, cleaning for reassurance is recommended though the stricter legal consequence is not automatic.
  • If a reliable device demonstrates impurity practically, act accordingly (clean/wash).
  • For visibly contaminated food or bread crusts, wash the affected parts before use.

9. Jumuʿah obligation relative to hearing/seeing the adhān

Key rulings

  • Jumuʿah is not obligatory for those who do not receive/observe the call (adhān) with ordinary perception (hearing/seeing).
  • An isolated individual who hears the adhān does not by their hearing make Jumuʿah incumbent on others who did not perceive it.
  • Visual perception (seeing the congregation or caller with ordinary sight) can render Jumuʿah incumbent where hearing does not reach.

Differences of opinion

  • Some jurists differ on whether self‑caused failure to hear/see excuses one; practical advice is to avoid deliberately obstructing perception.

Practical actions

  • If you did not hear or see the adhān/congregation with ordinary senses, Jumuʿah is generally not incumbent.
  • If you can see or hear the adhān with ordinary perception, consider Jumuʿah incumbent.

10. Diyah (compensation) for diminished sight

Ruling

  • Jurists treat diyah compensation similarly across degrees of reduced sight discussed; smaller reductions (relative to an exceptional seer) do not create distinct diyah categories in the presented material.

Practical action

  • Follow relevant legal guidance in claims for diyah; differences in remaining acuity typically do not change the standard diyah applied.

11. Consistency in worship (example: witr)

Principle and ruling

  • Prefer a sustainable, consistent baseline of worship (dā’im) over irregular extremes.
  • For witr: maintain a regular minimum (e.g., commonly 3 rakʿahs); occasionally add extra, but avoid a pattern of sometimes omitting and sometimes overdoing.

Practical action

  • Adopt a realistic daily minimum you can sustain; increase occasionally but keep the baseline.

12. Greetings etiquette

Evidence

  • Qurʾān: respond to greeting (Q 4:86) — guidance supports returning salutations.

Rulings & practice

  • Respond to greetings; returning with at least equal wording is appropriate; better to respond with fuller formula (e.g., add mercy/blessings).

Practical action

  • When greeted (e.g., "حياكم الله" or "بأمان الله"), reply suitably (e.g., "حياك الله", "وفي أمان الله", or "السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته").

Sources, Authorities & Evidential Notes

  • Classical authorities and commentators referenced across sections: Imam al‑Ghazali (contextual praise), Abu Ḥanīfa, Ibn al‑Qāsim, Ibn Hajar, al‑Nawawī, and commentators on al‑Majmūʿ/al‑Mubṣūt among others.
  • Explicit Qurʾān/ḥadīth citations seldom appear in the excerpt; the lesson relies primarily on juristic reasoning, established fiqh principles (presumption of purity, avoiding hardship), and classical commentary.

Consolidated Practical Checklist

  • Gender/appearance: apply rulings by real/legal sex; avoid looking if appearance provokes fitna.
  • Menstruation/miscarriage: treat bleeding as hayd absent proof of childbirth; consult medical proof if available.
  • Habitual cycles: follow established habit; treat excess beyond habit as istihādah per school rules; mumayyiza cases require detail.
  • Doubt in prayer: brief doubt—continue; prolonged doubt—stop, re‑establish certainty and repeat.
  • Awrah exposure in prayer: cover immediately; uncovered exposure commonly invalidates prayer—assist and repeat if necessary.
  • Joining imam in rukūʿ: if you confidently join and settle, the rakʿah counts; if unsure, add precautionary rakʿah.
  • Imam’s sajdah: if certain imam did not recite the sajdah verse, do not follow; if uncertain, follow the imam.
  • Minute impurities: use ordinary‑sight standard; invisible traces excused; visible impurity must be removed.
  • Smell without sight: cleaning recommended for reassurance; legal declaration of impurity not automatic per the lesson’s leaning.
  • Instruments: reliable instruments making impurity practically perceptible may affect ruling; do not demand microscopic proof routinely.
  • Jumuʿah: obligation depends on ordinary perception of adhān/congregation.
  • Worship consistency: choose a sustainable baseline (e.g., witr 3) and adhere to it.
  • Greetings: respond; prefer fuller return of salam in line with Qurʾānic guidance (4:86).
