Fiqh of Missed Ramadan Fasts (Hayd) — Making Up Missed Days (Qadaʾ)

Core ruling (majority)

  • When a woman must make up (qadaʾ) two or more fasts missed due to menstruation and her cycle/timing is uncertain, the classical majority prescribe a precautionary scheduling method:
  • Double the number of missed days and add two extra days (double + 2).
  • Example: 2 days due → 2×2 = 4, +2 = 6 days scheduled.
  • 3 days due → 3×2 = 6, +2 = 8 days scheduled; larger numbers follow the same rule.
  • Distribute/stagger the chosen fast and non‑fast days across the 30‑day lunar month (first and second halves) so that, regardless of when bleeding begins on a given day, the woman will have achieved the required number of valid completed fasts.
  • The method secures legal certainty (yaqīn) despite uncertainty of onset of bleeding.

Definitions / juristic principles

  • "ضعفت" (they were doubled): juristic device of multiplying days to create a buffer against uncertainty.
  • "ضمّت إليه يومين" (add two): an extra two days beyond the doubled amount as additional precaution.
  • Month treated as 30 days; schedules use the two halves (first 15, second 15) to produce overlapping partitions that guarantee completion.

Differences of opinion

  • Majority: double + add two, then stagger across month (widely taught; examples provided above).
  • Sahib al‑Hawi (reported early view): simpler scheme—fast the number due at the start of each half (e.g., 2 days at start of each half for two owed). Lesson assessment: generally unreliable unless bleeding always begins at night; thus usually incorrect.
  • al‑Darimi’s method: praised as refined; gives detailed schedules and sometimes requires longer continuous fasting (e.g., to guarantee two consecutive qadaʾ days, might need up to 18 consecutive days in some formulations; for large totals, up to 30 consecutive days may be recommended).
  • Minor differences among commentators (al‑Mutawalli, al‑Baghawi, al‑Rafiʿi, author of al‑Hawi) on relaxations if schedule completes before day 15, and on exact partitions.

Practical action items

  • Adopt the conservative majority method (double the owed days + 2, stagger across the month) to ensure certainty.
  • If you want a precise daily fast/not‑fast pattern suited to your owed number, consult al‑Darimi schedules or a qualified jurist who can map the pattern.
  • Keep a written record: days owed, planned schedule, actual bleeding days to verify you achieved the required valid fasts.
  • When in doubt, follow the more precautionary schedule or ask a local scholar.

Evidence

  • Lesson discussion is juristic/technical; no explicit Qur'an or hadith citations were presented in the transcript.

Prayer When Property/Person Is Threatened (Pursuing a Thief) — Salat al‑Khawf and Related Rules

Issue

  • Can a person continue and complete the prayer if, during prayer, someone steals property (e.g., sandals, phone) or an animal/slave runs off? May one pursue while praying? What if others shout “thief!”?

Main rulings taught

  • Permissive view (followed by instructor; majority in lesson):
  • You may pursue a thief while remaining within the prayer framework (do salat al‑khawf / prayer-of-fear): permitted to turn from the qibla if necessary, to make abbreviated movements, and to step on impurity if required by necessity.
  • Pursuing a snatcher of a sandal is included in this permissibility.
  • For a runaway animal/slave, pursuing and performing the fear‑type prayer is allowed while the danger/loss continues; when safety is attained, perform the prayer at the place of safety (salat al‑amn).
  • Restrictive/dissenting view (Ibn Hajar):
  • One should break the prayer and then pursue. His reasoning: the pursuer becomes a seeker (ṭālib) rather than a fugitive (hārib), so the case is no longer analogous to salat al‑khawf.
  • Shouting from behind (صيَاح):
  • The lesson’s ruling: loud shouting (e.g., “Hold the thief!”) invalidates the prayer and it must be broken. Shouting removes the special clandestine circumstance that justified continuing the prayer.

Evidence/reasoning

  • Analogical application of rules of salat al‑khawf and the permissive allowances under necessity; rationale for invalidation by shouting is that shouting negates the emergency character that allowed continuation.

Practical action items

  • If your property is stolen while praying:
  • According to the permissive view: you may pursue and complete the prayer in the mode of salat al‑khawf; then, when safe, pray at the safe spot if required.
  • If you follow Ibn Hajar’s line or your local madhhab ruling, break the prayer first, then pursue.
  • If someone shouts “thief!” nearby, conclude that the prayer is invalid and stop praying.
  • If an animal/slave runs away: pursue while using the rules of fear; when safety is secured, pray at that place of safety.

Authorities mentioned

  • Permissive: al‑Ramli (and followers), al‑Khatib al‑Sharbini.
  • Dissenting: Ibn Hajar.

Running Because of Rain / Fleeing vs. Seeking — Which Prayer Forms Apply

Key distinction

  • Fleeing from harm (e.g., running to escape rain to save belongings) → treated like a fugitive (khāʾif) → prayer of intense fear (salat al‑shiddah) rules may apply.
  • Pursuing to recover property/animal (ṭālib) → treated as seeker → different ruling; after attaining safety, perform salat al‑amn.

Practical action items

  • If escaping rain to protect valuables and praying while in flight, use the rules for the prayer under fear.
  • If chasing to recover property, treat yourself as a seeker and follow the appropriate rules; when safe, pray at the safe location.

Severed Body Parts: Reattachment, Cutting, and Impurity

Definitions

  • Severed part: any limb or member wholly separated from the living body (ear, finger, tooth).
  • Part still attached: partially detached but connected by tissue.
  • Lisq/laṣq: reattaching or “sticking back” the severed piece.
  • Dam munfaṣil: separated (external) blood.

Main juristic positions

  • Partial detachment with minimal bleeding:
  • Many jurists permit reattaching (surgical repair) when bleeding is little and reattachment prevents harm.
  • Complete detachment:
  • Stricter opinion (cited: al‑Rāfiʿī and some commentaries) — a completely severed part, once returned/reattached, must be removed again; the detached part is treated as foreign and problematic for ritual purity.
  • Other Shāfiʿī transmissions/opinions: not obligatory to remove a fully severed and reattached part; it may be left (analogous to rulings on teeth in some early texts).
  • Medical necessity and harm:
  • If removal would cause greater harm (e.g., destroy function of remaining limb), leaving the reattached part is permitted.
  • Modern microsurgery that restores blood flow and function can change the legal assessment; saving life/function is a valid necessity.

Rationale

  • Those requiring removal base the ruling on the status of a part that left the body: its blood and tissue were “separated,” creating legal complications (najāsa or foreignness).
  • Opponents argue the living body and its parts are generally pure and medical repair may be allowed; differences stem from textual readings and juristic inference.

Practical action items

  • Immediately consult both a medical professional and a qualified scholar when a part is severed or surgical reattachment is proposed.
  • If reattachment is necessary to save life or prevent loss of function, it is permitted by necessity.
  • If reattachment is only cosmetic and not necessary, the safer juristic stance is to avoid non‑necessary reattachment.
  • If a part has been reattached surgically, consult a scholar about ritual‑purity consequences; removal may be advised only if it can be done without significant additional harm.

Authorities referenced

  • Al‑Rāfiʿī, commentaries (al‑Rawdah), Kitab al‑Umm (Imam al‑Shāfiʿī), later commentators such as Ibn Hajar, al‑Khatib al‑Sharbini.

Separated Blood, Transfusion, and Use of Grafts

Rulings summarized

  • Separated external blood is considered external impurity (najāsa) and must be removed for prayer.
  • Blood once transfused into the body becomes internal (baṭin); the external impurity of its original container does not persist in the recipient’s interior in the same way.
  • Using impure bone/tissue (dead bone) for grafting (jabr) is permitted only under necessity when no pure alternative exists and it is required to save the limb or prevent major harm.
  • Replanting/reattaching detached parts is permitted when necessary to preserve function; cosmetic-only purposes do not ordinarily justify use of impure materials.

Practical action items

  • Wash/remove visible/separated blood before prayer.
  • Transfusions: do not assume ritual impurity persists in the recipient; consult a scholar if concerned.
  • Grafts or use of impure material: permitted only when medically necessary — obtain medical confirmation and a juristic ruling.

Hiring a Third Party Service to Perform Aqiqah/Sacrifice

Key rulings

  • Validity and permissibility:
  • Paying a service to perform aqīqah or to distribute sacrificial meat is generally valid if your intention is clear (aqīqah vs sadaqah).
  • If your intent was sadaqah or to fulfill a vow (nadr), payment to a third party who distributes to the needy usually counts.
  • Caveat:
  • Do not hand over offerings to people who would reject them if they knew they were merely charity but accept them only as an aqīqah under false pretense.

Practical action items

  • Clarify your intention with the service (aqīqah vs charity) and confirm distribution channels reach legitimate beneficiaries.
  • Keep records/receipts. If the service would refuse charity, choose another channel or inform them truthfully.

Cosmetic Surgery, Removal of an Extra Finger, and Related Procedures

Core rulings

  • Unnecessary cosmetic alteration that permanently changes creation is generally prohibited.
  • Removal of an extra finger (polydactyly) is permissible when necessity exists:
  • Functional harm (pain, impaired use), risk to the rest of the hand, or severe psychological harm may justify removal.
  • Cosmetic procedures solely for beautification are not permitted; temporary/reversible procedures and genuine medical treatments are more lenient.

Nuances & differences

  • Some madhhabs and jurists (Hanafi and later Shāfiʿī treatments) allow removal in necessity. Contemporary jurists differ on marginal cases (psychological harm, social distress).
  • Botox vs fillers:
  • Fillers that permanently “change creation” were taught as impermissible.
  • Botox, when used medically (to treat wrinkles) was presented more leniently, but the teacher noted a prior written restrictive fatwā requiring a married woman to obtain her husband’s permission in that particular ruling—indicating scholarly variation.

Practical action items

  • Seek a medical opinion and a scholarly ruling before elective cosmetic surgery.
  • Proceed only if there is a clear medical/psychological necessity; do not undergo surgery purely for beautification.
  • Keep written fatwas or medical reports when relying on them.

Cosmetic Procedures, Self‑harm, Cupping, Hair Removal — Brief Rulings

  • Self‑inflicted wounding without medical purpose is blameworthy and often prohibited.
  • Cupping (hijama) and medically prescribed treatments are permitted.
  • Plucking hair: if done without valid reason and imitates the opposite gender, it can be disliked or forbidden; painful removal with no benefit is at least disliked.
  • Temporary/reversible cosmetic measures are more lenient than permanent alterations.

Practical action items

  • Avoid unnecessary self‑harm; seek medical help for treatments.
  • Consider the purpose (treatment vs beautification) and permanence before proceeding.

Fajr (Dawn) Angle: 15° vs 18° — Following Local Timetables

Key points

  • Local mosques use different twilight angles (15° or 18°) to set fajr; laypeople following a mosque timetable are generally excused if the timetable was prepared by qualified authorities.
  • Blindly following an unreliable timetable when one could reasonably verify/consult is blameworthy.

Practical action items

  • Prefer timetables from recognized, qualified institutions; when in doubt consult a local scholar or established astronomical/prayer‑time authority.

Consolidated Practical Checklist (Actionable Steps)

  • Missed Ramadan fasts (hayd, irregular cycle): use the majority double+2 staggered schedule; document owed days, planned schedule, and bleeding dates; consult a jurist for precise daily patterns if needed.
  • Thief during prayer:
  • If following permissive line: you may pursue and complete the prayer in the mode of salat al‑khawf; if shouting occurs, stop praying.
  • If following Ibn Hajar line: break the prayer first, then pursue.
  • Severed body part:
  • Seek immediate medical care and consult a local scholar.
  • Reattach if necessary to save life or function; avoid reattachment for pure cosmetic reasons without a clear necessity.
  • Address ritual‑purity implications with a scholar if reattachment occurs.
  • Blood/transfusion/grafts:
  • Clean external blood before prayer.
  • Transfused blood becoming internal does not create ongoing external impurity in usual terms; consult if unsure.
  • Use impure graft material only by necessity with medical confirmation.
  • Aqiqah via third parties: clarify intention and distribution; avoid deceptive handing to recipients who would refuse charity.
  • Cosmetic surgery / extra digits: obtain medical and scholarly opinion; proceed only for necessity.
  • Fajr timings: follow recognized timetables; verify with scholars/astronomers if uncertain.
  • For medical‑fiqh intersections: always consult both a competent doctor and a qualified jurist in your madhhab or locale.
