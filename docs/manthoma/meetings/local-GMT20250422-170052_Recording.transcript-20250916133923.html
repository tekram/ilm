I. Make‑up fasts (taḥṣīl al‑siyām)

Definitions / scope

  • Taḥṣīl: arranging chosen days inside a given interval to fulfill a required number of missed fasts (qada’).
  • ṭaraf (edge): an endpoint of the span (start or end).
  • The lesson develops combinatorial schemes to obtain 2, 3, 4, … fast days inside specified totals (examples mostly 22–30 days).

Key methodological rulings

  • It is permissible to schedule make‑up fasts in patterned, non‑consecutive ways provided the required days are fulfilled and juristic conditions are met.
  • Some constructive templates work only for specific total-length spans; schemes for small spans often do not extend validly to much larger totals.

Representative constructive patterns

  • Obtaining 2 days: examples using 5‑day spacing within 22–29 day spans (various symmetric/asymmetric placements; many admissible variants).
  • Obtaining 3 days:
  • Majority (jumhūr) technique: a doubling/constructive method (classical pattern producing consecutive days via block constructs; teacher described an 8‑day block method yielding three consecutive days in practice).
  • Al‑Dārimī and Dahamiyya: alternative numeric recipes and extremely detailed permutations; Dahamiyya yields a very large enumeration of cases.
  • Four days and higher were noted but treated briefly.

Counts and complexity

  • Numerous distinct admissible schedules: examples given (counts such as 105, 126, 210, 350, 600, 3,003 across ranges). Full tabulation referenced in the instructor’s Mukhtasar.

Differences of opinion

  • Majority (jumhūr) vs Dahamiyya vs al‑Dārimī: differ in construction methods, minimal totals required, and extent of enumerations.
  • Some schemes are invertible (can be mirrored from either end); others are asymmetric.

Practical action items

  • For ordinary cases use simple, transparent patterns (e.g., choose two spaced days within the permitted span) or the majority doubling method for three consecutive days.
  • Consult the instructor’s Mukhtasar or a jurist for complex spans and for applying schemes that only function for specific totals.
  • Do not apply small‑span constructions beyond their valid ranges (e.g., >29 days when teacher warned they fail).
  • When in doubt, consult a qualified jurist.

II. Bird droppings and birds in mosques — purity and excuse (ʿudhr / maʿfūn)

Main ruling

  • Classical authorities (notably al‑Nawawī, Ibn Daqīq al‑ʿĪd, Abu Isḥāq al‑Shīrāzī) permit an excuse for bird droppings in mosques: worshippers are not required to avoid every spot or continuously remove droppings when avoidance/cleaning is impracticable because birds repeatedly soil the same places. This is the preferred position cited by the instructor.

Conditions for the excuse (three practical conditions)

  • 1) Not deliberate: one must not intentionally step on the impurity.
  • 2) No wetness involved: if the droppings or the worshipper’s clothes/feet are wet, the excuse commonly does not apply (Ibn Ḥajar emphasizes wetness negates the excuse).
  • 3) Genuine difficulty/unavoidability: the impurity must be widespread or such that normal worship would be impracticable unless excused.

Special cases and clarifications

  • Tawaf: same conditions apply — stepping deliberately on droppings during tawaf invalidates the excuse.
  • Wet cleaning by others (e.g., wet‑wiping then re‑drying) may remove the state of excuse if it changes the impurity’s condition.
  • In private homes:
  • Birds in cages that soil the house: no excuse — owner must remove impurity.
  • Birds free in the house and soiling recurrently: mosque‑style concession may apply if avoidance/cleaning is impracticable.
  • Insects, blood, venom:
  • Small blood (from squeezing a pustule) or minor insect secretions may be excused in necessity or hardship.
  • Venom: treated as impure if known to be venomous; if uncertain, one may assume purity.

Differences of opinion

  • Majority (al‑Nawawī et al.) allow the concession; Ibn Ḥajar and others emphasize stricter treatment regarding wetness and identifiable impurities.
  • Some infer pigeons’ excretions are pure from permissibility of keeping pigeons; others reject that inference and see permissibility as concession, not proof of ritual purity.

Practical action items

  • Do not deliberately step on bird droppings; avoid known wet contamination.
  • If Mosque is widely soiled and avoidance is impractical, you may pray without obsessively avoiding each spot; prefer a clean side if the mosque clearly divides into clean and soiled areas and moving is easy.
  • Clean excused impurities when feasible; mosque caretakers should perform proper cleaning rather than merely concealing traces.
  • In private property, remove avoidable impurities; do not keep birds in cages that soil the home without cleaning.
  • If an issue (e.g., killing a bird in the Haram) arises, consult a local scholar for fidya/compensation specifics.

III. Birds in mosques (keeping, expelling, damage)

Key rulings

  • Leaving birds that nest or alight in a mosque is generally permitted; one need not expel them unless they cause real harm.
  • Deliberately bringing birds into a mosque to keep them there is impermissible.
  • If birds cause damage (soiling carpets, structural harm), removal may be permitted as choosing the lesser harm.
  • Killing birds in the Haram is strongly condemned; classical reports prescribe expiation (traditionally a sacrificial animal), consult a scholar for current application.
  • Consensus reported permitting pigeons to remain in mosques; this permissibility is treated as a concession, not necessarily proof their excrements are ritually pure.

Practical action items

  • Prefer the clean section of a mosque when available and easily accessible.
  • Avoid intentionally bringing birds into mosques.
  • Clean excused impurities when feasible; remove nesting birds only to prevent serious damage.
  • Seek local scholarly guidance on serious violations (harm or killing in Haram).

IV. Street mud (ṭīn al‑shawāriʿ) and minor impurities

Principle and definitions

  • General legal presumption: default is purity (al‑asl ʿadam al‑najāsa). Small, unavoidable traces of street mud (mixed with unknown impurities) are often excused.
  • Qalīl (small) vs kathīr (abundant): small amounts commonly excused; abundant/certain impurity is not excused.
  • Istihlāk (absorption/dilution): when impurity is so mixed or indistinguishable, many jurists excuse it.

Specific rulings

  • Small, unavoidable splashes of mud (including in busy public places) are generally pardoned for prayer/tawāf, provided:
  • The amount is small and not due to negligence.
  • It is not clearly dog/pig faeces or sewage.
  • Dog/pig traces: more problematic; some jurists excuse tiny unavoidable traces in public contexts, others do not — opinions vary.
  • Flowing water/drain run‑off: if it removes or dilutes impurity, the result may be treated as pure unless one knows it is sewage.
  • Private rooms and homes: no broad pardon — visible impurities must be removed.

Differences of opinion

  • Some jurists (incl. Maliki tendencies, al‑Ramli citations) allow broader pardon for widely spread impurity on roads and public places.
  • Others (e.g., cautious readings of Ibn Ḥajar and al‑Nawawī) do not excuse actual visible impurities even if widespread.

Practical action items

  • Shake off/dust clothes before entering a mosque; if contamination is small and unavoidable, you may rely on pardon.
  • Do not bring obviously impure garments into a mosque.
  • If you step on obvious faeces or dog/pig discharge, cleanse the area/garment before prayer.
  • Treat drain/runoff as pure unless impurity is certain.
  • In private homes, always clean visible impurities; use a small prayer mat in uncertain settings.

V. Washing new clothes and other tahāra principles

Key rulings

  • Garments are presumed pure by default. Washing a new garment is recommended (mustahabb) if there is a reasonable possibility of impurity; washing merely from unfounded suspicion is blameworthy (waswasa).
  • The exemption from removing small street impurities does not apply when contamination results from one’s negligence, a fall from above, or other culpable causes.
  • Seasonal clothing differences do not change the rule of exemption; hardship considerations apply regardless of season.

Wet mixtures and indistinguishable impurity

  • If a heavy impurity mixes into mud/water and becomes indistinguishable, many jurists excuse it; if the impurity remains identifiable, treat it as najāsa and remove it.

Practical action items

  • Wash new garments when there is reasonable ground to suspect contamination.
  • Avoid scrupulous, obsessive checking of every water source or every garment absent real reason.
  • When in doubt, rely on the presumption of purity unless certainty of impurity exists; consult your usual school or local scholar for concrete cases.

VI. Building/expanding a house while indebted

Key ruling

  • A debtor should not build or expand a house while creditors are entitled to immediate payment unless:
  • The creditors have consented to a postponement, or
  • A real necessity exists (e.g., family faces eviction or exposure to serious harm).

Definition of necessity

  • Necessity justifying delay: genuine risk of severe harm or being forced to sleep in the street. Mere need for more space or crowding is not sufficient.

Practical cautions

  • Prioritize repaying others’ money; avoid spending on non‑essentials when debtors are demanding payment.
  • If in true necessity, borrowing to secure shelter is permitted; otherwise, defer non‑urgent expansion.

VII. Widespread impurity on roads and in mosques (overlap)

Core dispute

  • Whether pervasive impurity in public spaces or mosques grants a general pardon for worshipers.
  • Two poles:
  • Lenient: widespread impurity may be pardoned due to hardship and impracticability (used for busy roads and some public spaces).
  • Strict: actual identifiable impurity (ʿayn al‑najāsa) is not pardoned even if widespread; removal is required when certain.

Practical synthesis

  • If impurity is certain and identifiable where you must touch or prostrate, do not pray there unless you can prevent contact.
  • If impurity is merely probable or the contamination is small/indistinguishable and unavoidable, many jurists allow proceeding with prayer.
  • Carrying a small prayer mat or cloth is recommended when praying in uncertain environments.

  • Authorities mentioned: al‑Nawawī, Ibn Ḥajar, Ibn Daqīq al‑ʿĪd, Abu Isḥāq al‑Shīrāzī (al‑Tadhkira), al‑Ramli, Dahamiyya, al‑Dārimī, the majority (jumhūr), Rushidi, Shubra Milsi (via ʿAbd al‑Hamid).

IX. Key practical checklists

  • Make‑up fasts:
  • Use simple patterns for small, common cases; consult Mukhtasar/instructor for complex spans.
  • Do not extend specialized small‑span templates beyond their validated ranges.
  • Mosques and bird droppings:
  • Do not intentionally step on droppings; avoid wet contact.
  • Prefer the clean side if distinct and accessible.
  • Clean excused impurities when feasible; mosque caretakers should perform proper cleaning.
  • Birds in mosques:
  • Do not intentionally introduce birds; leave nesting birds unless they cause damage.
  • Consult a scholar for serious violations (e.g., killing in Haram).
  • Street mud and minor impurities:
  • Shake off small, unavoidable mud before entering mosque.
  • Clean obvious faeces, dog/pig traces, or known sewage before prayer.
  • Use a prayer mat in uncertain places.
  • New garments and general purity:
  • Wash new clothes if reasonable suspicion of contamination exists.
  • Avoid obsessive questioning of water sources; rely on presumption of purity unless certain impurity.
  • Debt and housing:
  • Do not build/expand while creditors may demand payment; only when creditors agree or real necessity exists.

One-line practical takeaway

Classical jurists balance ritual purity requirements with practical hardship: small, unavoidable traces (street mud, recurrent bird droppings) can be pardoned under conditions (no deliberate contact, no wetness, genuine unavoidability), but identifiable or avoidable impurities (dog/pig excretions, definite faeces, known sewage) require cleansing and should be treated strictly; consult your regular madhhab authority for borderline cases.
