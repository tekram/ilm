Postpartum Bleeding Rules - May 13, 2025

Start: 2025-05-13T17:00:24

Postpartum bleeding (nifas) — definition and key terms

  • Definition (fiqh): Nifas (النِّفَاس) is the bleeding that occurs after childbirth; classical jurists generally fix its start at the child’s separation (complete delivery).
  • Linguistic note: Arabic lexicons show variant forms (nifās, nafās) and plurals, but the legal sense is the postpartum blood issuing after delivery.

Principal ruling (summary)

  • General parity with menstruation (hayd):
  • A woman in nifas is exempt from prayer and fasting, forbidden sexual intercourse, excused from tawāf and iʿtikāf; she performs ghusl when nifas ends and must make up missed prayers and fasts (qadā).
  • Classical authorities report broad consensus (ijmāʿ) on treating nifas like hayd in most legal respects.

Differences between nifas and menstruation (principal exceptions)

  • Scholars recognize four key distinctions (technical, affecting some legal counts and proofs):

1. Nifas is not a proof of puberty (bulūgh); pregnancy may precede bulūgh.

2. Technical treatment in certain discharge categories (istihāḍa) differs; pregnancy-related bleedings require case-by-case judgment.

3. Family-law counts (e.g., iddah, interruptions) may be handled differently when bleeding is nifas rather than hayd.

4. Rules on interruption/continuation of specific acts (e.g., kafāra fasts or other expiatory rulings) may vary if interrupted by nifas.

  • Practical note: these are technical exceptions; for everyday practice follow the concrete rulings below or consult a scholar.

Disputed scenarios: bleeding before, with, or after birth

  • Scenario A — Bleeding before complete delivery (during pregnancy or early labor):
  • Majority: not nifas; nifas begins only after the child is fully separated.
  • Practical effect: religious obligations continue until separation produces qualifying nifas.
  • Scenario B — Bleeding that issues together with the child:
  • Two views:
  • Majority view: nifas begins at the child’s separation, so blood seen earlier is not yet nifas.
  • Minority view: blood issuing with the child may be nifas from the moment it appears.
  • Practical effect: if treated as nifas, obligations cease; if not, obligations continue until separation.
  • Scenario C — Bleeding after separation:
  • Majority agree this is nifas; rules of nifas apply.
  • Special cases:
  • Twin deliveries or bleeding between twins are treated by analogy with minor variant rulings.
  • If birth completes after sunset while bleeding occurred earlier, most jurists count nifas from separation, affecting whether the prayer of that time was obligatory.

Induced fetal death / abortion — juristic notes

  • Some jurists held that if a woman intentionally caused fetal death and expelled the dead fetus she must make up the prayers/fasts of nifas days because of the sin; others (more commonly adopted) said no automatic qada arises solely from that act.
  • Practical: consult a qualified scholar; the instructor deferred fuller treatment to the prayer chapter.

Practical checklist for nifas (majority guidance)

  • If bleeding begins after full delivery/separation:
  • Treat as nifas: stop prayers and fasting; sexual intercourse forbidden.
  • When bleeding stops, perform ghusl; resume prayer/fasting and make up missed acts (qadā).
  • If bleeding occurs before separation (during pregnancy/labor): ordinarily not nifas — continue obligations unless a competent jurist rules otherwise.
  • If blood accompanies the child at birth (disputed): follow local madhhab or seek a mufti; the safer majority practice starts nifas at separation.
  • For intermittent/ambiguous bleeding during expected nifas: consult a scholar due to differing rules on interruption and counting.
  • In cases of induced abortion: seek specific scholarly guidance.

Case example: bleeding shortly before delivery

  • Two positions:
  • Treat the pre-delivery bleeding as hayd (menstrual).
  • Treat it as abnormal bleeding (dam fasād / istihādah).
  • Instructor’s practical note: in the discussed scenarios the legal consequences are similar; focus on the correct classification per established rules and ask a scholar if needed.

Medical devices, catheters, and ritual purity

General principles

  • Necessity and removal-of-harm rule: if removing a medical device (IV, feeding line, catheter) would cause real medical harm, severe pain, or excessive cost, the patient may rely on concessions (rukhsah).
  • Tayammum in necessity:
  • Permitted when removal or washing would cause harm; pray on tayammum and perform qada later.
  • Tayammum may cover both sound and wounded parts in certain positions.
  • If removal is safe and not harmful, the device should be removed so proper wudu/ghusl and cleansing can be performed.

Catheter and continuous urine discharge

  • Emission of urine from natural outlet (even via a tube) nullifies wudu; if continuous and removal causes harm, rely on tayammum and make up prayers later.
  • If the outlet was surgically altered (e.g., suprapubic), juristic treatment varies; tayammum may be permitted if washing is impossible without harm.
  • If catheter removal is safe, remove it and perform wudu; wash any confirmed impurity.

Obligation to wash physical impurity (Maliki notes)

  • Some Maliki authorities treat washing off najāsa as obligatory; others allow leniency in hardship, treating washing as recommended when removal causes damage.
  • Practical: where possible wash and perform obligations; where genuine hardship exists, a permissive position (tayammum and deferred qada) is allowed.

Purity with animals, infants, and vapours

Foundational rules

  • Default: original presumption is purity (al-asl fi’l-ashyā’ huwa al-ṭahāra).
  • Afw (excuse/leniency) applies where certainty of contamination is absent; rukhsah given in hardship.
  • Principle from Imām al-Shāfiʿī: in probable/uncertain cases apply the general rule; do not derive exceptional rulings from mere possibility.

Cats, poultry, and contamination

  • Cats:
  • If a cat ate impurity then later touched/eat from food or water, many jurists excuse the food/water — do not declare it impure without firm proof.
  • Poultry (chickens, geese):
  • Disagreement exists: some treat fowl more strictly (ghalib that they feed on filth); others (and the instructor’s adopted practical view) apply default purity and leniency to avoid hardship and waste.
  • Practical teaching: do not routinely declare food/water impure because poultry grazed nearby; require certainty of contamination before washing or discarding.

Infant’s mouth, regurgitation (kashṭ/ghurra), breastfeeding

  • General concessions:
  • Infant regurgitation and the infant’s mouth are excused in many rulings — they do not automatically render the breast or shared water/food impure.
  • It is permissible to drink from what an infant drank; default is purity.
  • Limits:
  • If a garment is clearly soiled/contaminated by the infant, it must be washed; afw does not excuse confirmed contamination.
  • Nursing mothers are often granted rukhsah to pray in a nursing garment when washing repeatedly would be excessively burdensome; Shāfiʿī jurists are stricter and require washing when impurity is certain.

Vapour/steam from excrement (bukhār al-rīth)

  • Rule:
  • If vapour contacts a cloth while moist/wet, the cloth becomes impure and must be washed.
  • If the vapour is dry (no moisture), it does not render the cloth impure.
  • Practical: clean and wash when there is wet contact; otherwise rely on the default purity.

Practical action items (animals/infants/vapour)

  • Presume purity unless you have direct evidence of contamination.
  • Do not waste food or declare it impure merely because an animal passed over it.
  • Nursing mothers: prefer a dedicated prayer garment if possible; if washing is impractical, follow the admissible rukhsah (according to your madhhab).
  • If a cloth shows wetness from vapour or obvious contamination, perform istiǧāʼ/ washing.

Flatulence (passing wind) — ruling and practice

  • Preferred/stronger view presented:
  • Wind from the anus is treated as pure; dry flatulence does not invalidate wudu nor render clothing/vessels impure.
  • Rationale: smell or perceived impurity via odor does not equal transmission of najāsa; exposing people to hardship is avoided.
  • Evidence cited: Qur’anic principle “and He has not placed upon you in the religion any hardship” (phrase referenced) and Prophetic practice reported in traditions where the Prophet ﷺ did not order washing for mere passing wind.
  • Alternative views:
  • Some jurists hold that if emission is wet (dampness on clothing/seat) then impurity is established and washing is required.
  • Others discourage or view istinjāʼ for mere wind as unnecessary or disliked.
  • Practical rule:
  • If only dry wind passed and no wet impurity is visible, no washing or istinjāʼ required; prayer remains valid.
  • If there is visible moisture/soiling, wash the affected area.

Zakat and state levies (maks/taxes) — when does state collection discharge zakat?

Definitions

  • Maks: state levies, taxes, customs (non-zakat takings).
  • Ghasb: forcible/unlawful seizure.
  • Qabd: actual taking/possession.
  • Niyyah: intention of the payer (or taker).

Key juridical points and differences

  • Divergent positions:
  • Some jurists (e.g., al-Ramli’s articulation) treat the ruler’s collection as sufficient to discharge zakat when the ruler takes the money.
  • Others (Ibn al-Qasim, al-‘Abadi, al-Shurunwani) limit this: if the ruler’s taking is lawful and intended as zakat (or taken by valid ijtihad), it discharges zakat; if it is ghasb (unlawful), it does not — unless the owner, at the moment of seizure, intended the taken amount to be his zakat.
  • Intention matters:
  • Owner’s niyyah at the time of seizure can make a forced taking count as zakat.
  • Intention after the fact does not normally cure a prior ghasb.
  • Ruler’s niyyah/correctness:
  • If the ruler lawfully takes believing it to be zakat (or by legitimate ruling), the payer is absolved even if the state later misuses funds.
  • Unjust/forcible takings aimed at private/unlawful ends are generally not treated as zakat.
  • Modern application:
  • Contemporary general taxes not designated as zakat typically do not discharge zakat; forming the intention at payment may help in some views but consult a scholar for local contexts.

Practical action items

  • Conservative (safest) practice: do not count ordinary state taxes as zakat unless you or the collector explicitly intend/announce them as zakat at the time of payment and the taking is lawful.
  • If forced to pay an unlawful levy, consider (after scholarly consultation) whether a contemporaneous niyyah can be made to count it as zakat.
  • Scholars should advise taxpayers to intend zakat when handing money to a ruler/collector if they wish it to count, and examine the legality of the levy.

Practical guidance for patients, caregivers, and families (summary)

  • Postpartum women:
  • Follow the majority rule: nifas begins at separation of the child; stop prayer/fasting; resume after bleeding stops and ghusl is performed; make up missed acts.
  • In ambiguous cases (bleeding with labor, before separation), consult local madhhab guidance.
  • Medical devices and catheters:
  • Do not remove devices if removal causes real medical harm; rely on tayammum and make up prayers later.
  • When safe, remove devices to perform wudu/ghusl and cleanse impurity.
  • Infant/animal contamination:
  • Presume purity; wash only when contamination is certain or there is wetness.
  • Nursing mothers may rely on permitted concessions when washing continually is burdensome (follow your madhhab).
  • Flatulence and vapour:
  • Dry wind does not invalidate wudu nor make things najis; clean when wetness is present.
  • Zakat and taxes:
  • When possible make clear niyyah; treat modern taxes as generally not substituting zakat unless legal/intention conditions are met.
