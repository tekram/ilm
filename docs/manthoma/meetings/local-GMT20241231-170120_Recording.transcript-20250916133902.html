Menstrual Cycle Determination - December 31, 2024

📅 2024-12-31 🕐 17:01

Topic and context

  • Fiqh lesson (Shāfiʿī context, part 1/5) covering two related subjects:
  • The mutahayyirah: a woman confused about whether bleeding is menstrual (haydh) and how to determine her nūbah (cycle), with implications for ʿiddah (waiting period).
  • The mustahadhah: a woman with irregular/continuous non‑menstrual bleeding — rules for ghusl and wudu for prayer.
  • Also covered: juristic treatment of small blood, wound/exudate fluids, cupping/phlebotomy blood, contaminated liquids, and insect‑related residues.
  • Mostly based on classical scholars (al‑Nawawī, al‑Rāfiʿī, Ibn Hajar, Ramli, etc.); one hadith quoted on moderation (Bukhari). No direct Qurʾānic/ḥadīth texts used for the menstrual rulings in the transcript.

Key definitions

  • Mutahayyirah: a woman uncertain whether a bleed is menstrual or non‑menstrual, or whether she is a beginner (mubtadiʾah) or habitual (mutaʿawwidah).
  • Nūbah (noubah): the menstrual cycle/interval used to identify a pattern (period from a bleed to the next or the cycle interval reported by the woman).
  • Mustahadhah / Istihāḍah: continuous or irregular non‑menstrual bleeding that requires special rules for purification and prayer.
  • Maʿfū (excused): a small blood/exudate customarily overlooked and not treated as ritual impurity.
  • Dam al‑ajnabī (foreign blood): blood outside the menstrual/postpartum seat (wounds, cupping, boils, insect secretions).
  • ʿUrf: customary measure determining "little" vs. "much."

Rulings on the mutahayyirah (woman confused about her cycle)

Fundamental approach

  • Act with precaution when there is uncertainty — adopt the stricter position to avoid premature permissibility.
  • Determine the nūbah from what the woman reports:
  • If she gives a bound (e.g., “never exceeds X days/months”), take that bound as her nūbah.
  • If she gives no bound, treat her as most confused and follow the stricter rulings.

Classification rule (beginner vs. habitual)

  • If the candidate nūbah is less than 30 days → treat as beginner (mubtadiʾah).
  • If it exceeds 30 days → treat as habitual (mutaʿawwidah).
  • When comparing intervals, the larger relevant measure tends to determine classification.

On ʿiddah (waiting period)

  • Instructor emphasized applying the more stringent opinions so ʿiddah is not shortened erroneously.
  • Practical working rule (lesson conclusion): follow the majority practical norm (e.g., count three months where applicable) unless the woman knows a different habitual length.
  • If menopause is reached or expected age reached, treat per rules for menopausal cessation; if bleeding stops during ʿiddah apply the appropriate cessation rules.

Practical steps when advising

  • Ask the woman for any known maximum or pattern; if given, use it for nūbah.
  • If the time between a bleed and a divorce/event equals or exceeds her nūbah, follow what passed; if shorter, complete the nūbah and add the prescribed small intervals per the stricter positions.
  • In cases affecting marriage/divorce, prefer stricter scholarly positions; consult a qualified jurist with full dates/details.

Rulings for the mustahadhah (purification for prayer)

Basic principles

  • If a mustahadhah knows the usual time her bleeding stops (e.g., stops at sunset), then:
  • She performs ghusl after that known cessation and that ghusl suffices for the prayer that begins at that time.
  • For other prayers earlier that day, wudu is sufficient.
  • If she does not know the cessation time:
  • Safer majority view: perform ghusl at each obligatory prayer time (precaution).

Timing and validity of ghusl relative to prayer time — four positions

1. Ghusl must occur during the prayer time (and suffices).

2. Ghusl must occur during the time and the woman must hasten immediately to pray.

3. It suffices that ghusl finishes exactly at the beginning of the prayer time.

4. Ghusl must be completed somewhat before the prayer time ends (a margin equal to the prayer's duration).

  • Practical guidance:
  • Best practice: pray promptly after the ghusl if intending to use it for that prayer.
  • If delay occurs, many scholars require renewing wudu before praying because bleeding might have resumed; renewing wudu is a safe precaution.

Rulings on blood, wound fluids, cupping, contaminated liquids

General principles

  • The distinction between "little" (qalīl) and "much" (kathīr) is governed primarily by ʿurf (local custom).
  • Small unmixed foreign blood (dam al‑ajnabī), commonly unavoidable stains, is often excused (maʿfū) and does not invalidate prayer.
  • If foreign blood mixes with other substances or visibly soils clothes/skin (tadammukh), stricter opinions require removal and treat it as impurity.

Blood of boils, cupping, wounds (dam al‑dammāmīl)

  • Two authoritative positions:
  • al‑Rāfiʿī: when such bleeding tends to be continuous, treat like istihāḍah — apply containment/binding/packing and precautionary measures.
  • al‑Nawawī (madhhab‑al‑muʿtamad in lesson): treat these exudates as excused — leniency applies.
  • Practical outcome: the lesson favors al‑Nawawī’s leniency but recommends istihādhah measures if bleeding is continuous and hard to stop.

Wound/blister fluids (judrā) & pus

  • al‑Nawawī: wound/blister fluids are pure (ṭāhir) unless they change color, smell, or taste — then they are impure.
  • al‑Rāfiʿī: leans to treating such fluids like pus (impure) though allows leniency for minute amounts.
  • Practical adoption: follow al‑Nawawī’s criterion — treat unchanged wound exudates like sweat (pure); altered fluids are impure and should be removed.

Contaminated liquids (e.g., urine in wine)

  • General rule taught: if an impure substance falls into a liquid, that liquid becomes impure; impurity persists despite later transformation (e.g., wine → vinegar) in most positions.
  • Notable textual nuance: a transmitted textual statement accepted in class states that when juice is poured and then that mixture ferments and becomes vinegar, the resulting vinegar is treated as pure in that specific chain of events. This is a narrow exception found in the textual sources and affects practical outcomes if the specific sequence is established.
  • Practical implication: details matter — ascertain which substance was poured when and whether fermentation actually occurred; consult the texts or a scholar for complex chains.

Insect‑related residues (lice/fleas: blood, skins, eggs)

Main rulings

  • Tiny secretions/blood of lice and fleas on clothes/body:
  • Generally excused (maʿfū) and do not invalidate prayer when small and customary.
  • If secretion results from one’s action (crushing an insect), small amounts remain excused; large amounts require cleaning.
  • Insect skins/bodies (جلدة): treated as impurities — must be removed; praying with a visible insect body invalidates the prayer (majority); some earlier authors allow pardon if one was unaware.
  • Eggs:
  • Unanimated egg: pure.
  • Egg with living insect/viable stage: treated as pure.
  • Egg with a dead insect inside: impure technically but often pardoned; remove when possible.

Differences about repetition of prayer

  • Dispute whether a prayer performed unknowingly with an insect skin present must be repeated:
  • Some (e.g., al‑Zayyadi) require repetition even if unaware.
  • Others (e.g., al‑Rashidi, Shibr Malsi following Ibn al‑ʿImād) pardon the unaware person.

Differences of opinion — concise recap

  • Mutahayyirah: classification and computation of ʿiddah vary by school; instructor recommends stricter positions when uncertainty affects permissibility.
  • Mustahadhah ghusl timing: four positions range from ghusl strictly within the prayer time and immediate prayer to allowance for finishing exactly at the start; majority trend toward requiring ghusl at each prayer time if cessation time unknown.
  • Blood/exudates:
  • al‑Nawawī (Shāfiʿī standard in lesson): lenient on wound/cupping exudates and certain fluids unless changed; use ʿurf for little vs. much.
  • al‑Rāfiʿī and some other authorities: stricter — treat many exudates like istihādhah/pus, require containment or removal.
  • Contaminated liquids: majority rule impurity persists; narrow textual exception accepted in class for a specific fermentation scenario.

Practical action items (clear steps)

  • For a woman uncertain about her cycle:
  • Ask for any habitual bound; if given, adopt it as nūbah. If none, adopt the cautious rule.
  • Classify as beginner if nūbah < 30 days; habitual if > 30 days.
  • For ʿiddah disputes, prefer the stricter approach when consequences are marriage/divorce related; otherwise follow the madhhabal‑muʿtamad you adhere to.
  • For a mustahadhah:
  • If you know cessation time (e.g., at sunset): do ghusl after that time for that prayer; do wudu for other prayers.
  • If you do not know cessation time: perform ghusl at each obligatory prayer time (safer majority).
  • After ghusl, pray without delay if intending to use that ghusl; if delayed, renew wudu as a precaution.
  • For blood and wound fluids:
  • Use ʿurf to decide little vs. much. Small, unmixed, customary stains → may be excused.
  • Visible soiling or mixing with other substances → wash/remove and treat as impurity (stricter route).
  • For continuous bleeding from boils/cupping/wounds: contain/bind/pack if bleeding likely to reach clothes; follow madhhabal‑muʿtamad leniency unless bleeding is continuous, then apply istihādhah measures.
  • If wound/blister fluid shows change in color/smell/taste → remove and treat as impure.
  • For contaminated liquids:
  • Treat liquids contaminated by any impurity (even a drop) as impure in typical cases; however, verify specific chains (poured juice → fermented to vinegar) against authoritative texts when relevant.
  • For insect residues:
  • Ignore tiny unavoidable secretions; remove insect skins/bodies; clean large stains produced by crushing insects.
  • If uncertain and the impurity is minor/unintended, rely on leniency; for visible urine or large stains, clean and repeat prayer if performed in known impurity.

Evidence and sources

  • Lesson relies on classical juristic authorities: al‑Nawawī, al‑Rāfiʿī, Ibn Hajar, Ramli, al‑Bijarmi, al‑Zayyadi, al‑Rashidi, Shibr Malsi, Ibn al‑ʿImād, and passages of Al‑Mughni and its marginalia.
  • Hadith cited in the class: “Religion is easy... be moderate and near to perfection...” (Bukhari) — used to stress moderation in voluntary worship.
  • No explicit Qurʾānic verses or Prophetic texts were quoted for the menstrual/wound‑blood rulings in the transcript; rulings are juristic.

Practical checklist (quick)

  • Mutahayyirah: obtain any reported nūbah; if none, be precautious; classify by 30‑day benchmark.
  • Mustahadhah:
  • Known stop time → ghusl after stop for that prayer; wudu for other prayers.
  • Unknown stop time → ghusl at each obligatory prayer time (safer).
  • After ghusl, pray promptly or renew wudu if delayed.
  • Small unmixed blood/exudate: likely excused — continue worship.
  • Visible soiling / mixed blood: wash/remove and treat as impurity.
  • Continuous oozing from wounds/boils: bind/pack or follow istihādhah measures if bleeding persists.
  • Insect skins/bodies: remove; eggs/secretions — apply leniency unless large/obvious.
  • Dog/pig blood or clear urine contamination: always treat as impure and cleanse.

Recommended follow‑up

  • For concrete cases (dates, durations, exact quantities, fermentation chains), consult the classical texts cited (Al‑Mughni and its commentaries) or a qualified local scholar to apply the specific madhhab rulings.
  • If you follow a particular madhhab (Hanafi/Shāfiʿī/Malic), ask for a tailored flowchart or ruling per that school.

Allah knows best.
