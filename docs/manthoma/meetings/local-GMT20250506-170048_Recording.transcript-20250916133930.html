Menstruation and Purity Rules - May 06, 2025

📅 2025-05-06 🕐 17:00

Overview

A fiqh lesson (based on Imam al‑Nawawi’s principle) on dealing with menstrual uncertainty and related purity issues. Topics: how a woman should allocate days as certainly menstrual / certainly pure / doubtful; effects on prayer, wudu, ghusl, intercourse, and fasting (qada’). Also covered: ritual purity regarding flame, smoke, soot (habab), animal hair, saliva/lapping (esp. cats), and practical family‑finance rulings (children’s money), with juristic differences and actionable rules.

Foundational rule (Imam al‑Nawawi)

  • Principle: act on certainty (yaqīn). Certainty is not removed except by certainty.
  • If a woman is certain a day is haydh (menstrual), all haydh rulings apply.
  • If certain a day is taharah (pure), rulings of a pure woman (and istiḥādhah where applicable) apply.
  • If a day is doubtful (may be menstrual or pure), apply precaution:
  • Pray (perform wudu for each prayer) as if pure.
  • Treat the time as haydh regarding intercourse (i.e., intercourse prohibited).
  • Do not perform ghusl until there is certainty of cessation; if later a definite stoppage time is known, perform one ghusl at that time.
  • If the doubtful time cannot possibly be a break (i.e., cannot be a non‑bleeding interval), ghusl is not required but wudu per prayer is required.

Definitions / technical terms

  • Certain menstruation (haydh bi‑yaqīn): days definitely menstrual — no prayer, no intercourse, must make up missed fasts.
  • Certain purity (ṭahārah bi‑yaqīn): days definitely pure — normal rulings apply.
  • Doubtful time (zaman mashkūk / mayyaḥtamil al‑inqiṭā‘): days that could be either — follow precautionary rules above.
  • Mansī (المنسي): the forgotten/uncertain slot in the month where the remembered block may fall.
  • Nāsiyah (forgetful woman): woman who remembers counts or partial information but not precise placement.

Practical method to determine certain vs doubtful days

  • Place the remembered block (e.g., “6 days”) into the claimed interval of the month.
  • Inspect overlap with the mansī (forgotten portion):
  • The central portion that must fall inside the remembered block by logical placement is haydh by certainty.
  • Edge days that could or could not be within the remembered block remain doubtful.
  • Rule of thumb: as the remembered block grows or shifts centrally, the central (necessary) haydh days increase; edge days remain mashkūk.

Representative numeric examples (concise)

  • “6 days of the first 10”:
  • Days 5–6 (central) are certainly haydh; days 1–4 and 7–10 are doubtful (pray with wudu; no intercourse; ghusl only after known stoppage).
  • “7 of the first 10”: central 4 days become certainly haydh; edges doubtful.
  • “8, 9 of the first 10”: central block widens accordingly; edges remain doubtful.
  • “5 days of every 30 but start unknown”: memory of count alone is often insufficient to fix certain days — treat many days as doubtful; be conservative when counting qada’.
  • “10 days somewhere in the month” without placement: neither certain haydh nor certain taharah — perform wudu and pray in doubtful days; if bleeding stops at a known time, perform ghusl then.

Rulings affecting acts of worship (clear actions)

  • Prayer:
  • If day is certainly haydh: do not pray; make up missed prayers if applicable (haydh suspends prayer).
  • If day is certainly pure or doubtful: perform prayer; ensure wudu each prayer during doubt.
  • Wudu and ghusl:
  • Doubtful days: perform wudu for each prayer; ghusl only when bleeding cessation is known with certainty.
  • If doubt could not contain a break, ghusl not required.
  • Intercourse:
  • Prohibited during certain haydh and during doubtful days (treat doubtful as haydh for intercourse).
  • Fasting / qada’:
  • When uncertain whether a Ramadan day was missed due to haydh, count conservatively; once days of haydh become certain, complete qada’ accordingly.
  • If uncertainty concerns start time (night vs day), adjust qada’ count to cover worst‑case scenario until clarified.

Evidence and authorities

  • Method and maxims are classical juristic principles (Imam al‑Nawawi’s approach). Specific textual hadith mention: linguistic/hadith usage cited earlier for “al‑dakhan” (Sahih Muslim) when discussing smoke; otherwise rulings are drawn from madhhab commentaries (Ibn al‑‘Imad, Ibn Hajar, al‑Ramli, al‑Rifa‘i, al‑Zayn al‑‘Iraqi, etc.).

Purity rulings on flame, smoke, soot, ovens, and animal hair

Key rulings

  • Flame (naked flame): treated as pure — flame alone does not transmit ritual impurity to food cooked over it (view favored by Ibn al‑‘Imad and the instructor).
  • Smoke (dukhan):
  • Light/small smoke is generally excused (does not render touched items ritually impure).
  • Thick/abundant smoke is not excused and can render food/objects impure.
  • Conditions debated: whether the smoke must be unintentional (not produced “by his doing”) and whether moisture affects the concession.
  • Soot/particles (habab):
  • Not excused. Visible solid soot or black flecks that settle on food or clothing render them impure and must be removed/washed.
  • Contact with impure fuel/oven:
  • Direct contact with impure fuel or oven surfaces can render food impure; leniency for light smoke may allow eating but not carrying into prayer.
  • For use in prayer (e.g., carrying into or performing prayer upon), be stricter — do not rely on smoke‑based excusal.
  • Animal hair:
  • Small amounts of permissible animal hair (cats, livestock) are often excused; dog and pig hair are not excused.
  • Hair transferred by riding (marqūb) is generally excused even if seemingly abundant.
  • No uniform quantitative measure of “little” was established in the lesson.

Evidential note

  • The smoke example used the hadith linguistic usage (“al‑dakhan”) from Sahih Muslim as supporting classical usage; otherwise positions derived from juristic reasoning and madhhab transmissions.

Practical actions for purity issues

  • If flame is smoke‑free: treat food as pure.
  • If slight smoke touched food: usually permissible to eat; wash if doubts persist or if used in prayer context.
  • If soot/habab landed: remove/wash the affected area.
  • If food contacted impure fuel/oven: avoid using in prayer; wash if contact was direct.
  • For animal hair: remove visible/abundant hair (esp. dogs/pigs); minor cat hair may be excused; if you rode the animal, leniency applies.

Rules on animals (cats), saliva/lapping, doubt and certainty

  • Core maxims:
  • Certainty remains until negated by certainty.
  • Default is purity (al‑asl fi al‑ashyā’ al‑ṭahāra), but established impurity is not removed by mere possibility.
  • Cats and licking/drinking:
  • If cat certainly ate impurity, that impurity remains in the mouth unless there is certainty it was washed away.
  • If the cat possibly ate impurity and later plausibly drank from running/abundant water during its absence, many jurists permit presuming purification; stricter jurists require stronger proof.
  • For dog saliva, classic rules are stricter (e.g., multiple washings) — different treatment from cats.
  • Human/child/insane person contact:
  • If impurity was certain on a person or child and they later reappear in prayer area, their presence invalidates the prayer unless you have certainty the impurity was removed.
  • If a person may have washed while away and circumstances make washing likely, some jurists permit presuming purification; use local madhhab guidance when uncertain.

Practical action items (animals & doubt)

  • If a cat that certainly ate impurity touched water/utensils but you cannot be sure it washed, do not assume purification — wash the utensil or avoid using it in prayer.
  • If there was a real/lively possibility of drinking abundant/running water, you may treat later contact as pure according to permissive opinions.
  • When in doubt about whether an impure mouth was purified, inspect circumstances (was there running water, how long was the animal/ person away, typical habits) and prefer the stricter view in sensitive contexts (prayer).

Financial rulings: parental obligation, children’s money, intention, commingling

Key rulings

  • A father’s obligation (nafaqa) exists when the child lacks means. If the child has own funds, the father need not spend on that child until those funds are exhausted.
  • A father may prevent access to funds if he reasonably fears waste or forbidden use, but may not withhold obligatory maintenance.
  • Money given/gifted to a child belongs to the child. If a father spends it for the child, he should intend expenditure is from the child’s property for the child’s benefit.
  • Taking a child’s money while simultaneously claiming voluntary support from one’s own funds is impermissible; must return or account for taken funds.
  • Commingling funds: if child’s money is mixed with the father’s and becomes a joint pool, spending from it for the child is normally acceptable; still keep records where possible.

Intention (niyya) and agency

  • Intention matters: classify transfers correctly (gift vs safekeeping vs spending for child).
  • As agent, be explicit if using someone else’s funds; for selling a specific thing of the principal, repeated explicit niyya is not needed per transaction, but clarity prevents disputes.

Practical action items (family/finance)

  • Keep records of children’s funds; avoid mixing when possible.
  • If you take a child’s money to spend for them, document intention that it is for the child’s benefit.
  • Do not withhold obligatory maintenance; prevent gifts only when clear risk of sin/waste.
  • When in doubt about rights or mixed funds, restore the child’s funds or consult a qualified local scholar.

Summary of juristic differences (brief)

  • Menstrual allocation: differences in interpreting author’s phrasing about 5–9 of ten; but all agree on the primary rule: act on certainty; omit unmentioned days as doubtful.
  • Smoke: disagreement about whether excusal requires unintentionality, absence of moisture, or is absolute for small smoke.
  • Cats/saliva: differences on whether mere possibility of drinking suffices to remove certainty of impurity; stricter scholars demand stronger certainty.
  • Animal hair: quantitative threshold for “little” varies; riding‑origin hair generally excused.
  • Parental finance: nuance about when taking child’s funds is permissible; general consensus to preserve child’s property and clarify intention.

Practical consolidated checklist (what to do)

  • Menstruation:
  • Record start dates and lengths of periods; when uncertain, apply the mansī method to allocate central certain haydh days and edge doubtful days.
  • Doubtful day: perform wudu and pray; do not have intercourse; delay ghusl until known stoppage.
  • For fasting/qada’: count conservatively; make qada’ once haydh days are certain.
  • Purity (smoke/soot/flame/animal hair):
  • Flame without smoke → treat food as pure.
  • Little smoke → usually excused for eating; heavy smoke or visible soot → wash.
  • Remove visible soot/habab; be stricter for items intended for prayer.
  • Remove dog/pig hair; small permissible animal hair often excused; riding‑transferred hair excused.
  • Cats/saliva:
  • If certain impurity on mouth and no clear washing occurred → treat contacted items as impure and clean.
  • If clear opportunity for abundant/running water occurred → many jurists permit presuming purification.
  • Family finances:
  • Keep records; clarify niyya when taking/using child’s funds; do not claim voluntary support while using child’s money.
  • When in doubt on any of the above, consult a knowledgeable local scholar and prefer local madhhab practice.
