Overview and session plan

  • Purpose: Study fatwas in the book "Beneficial Fatwas on Issues of Personal Status that Occur" (al-Fatawa al-Nafi‘a fi Masa’il al-Ahwal al-Waqi‘a) by Imam Abu Bakr ibn Ahmad al-Khatib al-Ansari al-Tamimi al-Hadrami, using the compiler/editor Salem bin Hafidh al-Alawi al-Hadrami’s introduction as the roadmap.
  • Immediate plan: Begin each session by reading the book’s estiftah/introduction, then proceed through the fatwas sequentially; invite senior scholars (e.g., Sheikh Omar, Sheikh Dawood) for guidance.

The book, author, and compiler

  • Title (English): Beneficial Fatwas on Issues of Personal Status that Occur.
  • Author: Imam Abu Bakr ibn Ahmad ibn Abdullah al-Khatib — a noted Shafi‘i jurist from Hadramawt (Tarim).
  • Compiler/Editor: Salem bin Hafidh al-Alawi al-Hadrami (reliably transmitted and teacherly role; obtained ijāzah from the author).
  • Scope: Starts with nullifiers of wudu, covers major fiqh chapters, ends with miscellaneous Q&A; only answers verified by the compiler were included — some responses remain missing.

Biographical & historical notes

  • Al-Khatib family: historically prominent in Tarim for fiqh and teaching; many family members formed continuous scholarly chains (isnad). They combined juristic mastery with humility and Sufi tendencies in some branches.
  • Local history: Spread and consolidation of the Shafi‘i school in Hadramawt attributed partly to migrating imams (e.g., Imam al‑Muhajir); uncertainty remains about exact chronology and prior local adherence.
  • Recommended historical reading: Habib Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Shatri’s Adwar al-Tarikh al-Hadramiyya.

Transmission, ijāzah, and sanad — definitions and practice

  • Key definitions:
  • Ijāzah (إجازة): permission to transmit or teach a work, often with a sanad.
  • Riwāyah: authorization to narrate/transmit texts or litanies.
  • Dirāyah: authorization indicating mastery/competence in meanings/sciences.
  • Ifādah (إفادة): certificate/transcript recording attendance or study (analogous to a transcript).
  • Ilbās: symbolic dressing (e.g., imāmah or khirqa) as a sign of authorization in Sufi contexts.
  • Types/levels of relationship with a teacher:

1. Attended public circles (no private reading, no ijāzah).

2. Private reading(s) but no ijāzah.

3. Private reading(s) + ijāzah.

4. Full authorization: reading, ijāzah, ilbās, taklīm (oral instruction), mushāfah (handshake).

  • Forms of ijāzah:
  • Oral and written ijāzāt both valid; written preferred for preservation/institutional purposes.
  • Ijāzah may be limited (permission to teach a beginner text) or advanced (permission to issue fatwā); fatwa‑giving ijāzah is more stringent.
  • Sufi ijāzāt commonly grant permission for awrad, maqāmāt, and may mix riwāyah and dirāyah (a hybrid authorization).
  • Regional variation:
  • Levant/Egypt: more institutionalized written ijāzāt; formal certificates issued at graduation.
  • Hadramawt/Yemen: greater reliance on continued presence and oral transmission; some formal ijāzāt lost due to historical disruptions.
  • Sufi chains: local meanings for ilbās and symbolic signs differ by region — clarify local usage.

Notable jurisprudential issues & differences of opinion

  • Injection during Ramadan:
  • Reported local disagreement: Sheikh Salem ruled an injection invalidates the fast; others (e.g., Sheikh Fadl) expressed differing views or surprise. Students instructed to consult the precise reasoning of each scholar before applying rulings.
  • Writing Qur’anic/religious texts on historically manufactured paper:
  • Issue: older paper produced on clay/moulds that may have had impurities during manufacture.
  • Ruling in lesson: permissible to copy sharʿī works (including Qur’anic text) on such traditionally manufactured paper as a concession in that historical/material context, but preference remains for writing on clearly pure materials; using obviously najis ink or a manifestly impure surface for a Mushaf remains prohibited.
  • Difference of opinion: general classical prohibition vs historical concession due to customary manufacture; treat concession as exception, not norm.
  • Istinjā’ / istijmar (cleaning with a solid) and remaining traces:
  • Core rulings:
  • Using a clean solid (stone) to clean the private parts is valid; wipe three times until clean. If a stubborn trace remains that only water (or very fine pottery shards) removes, the person who performed the istijmar is excused (ma‘fu ‘anhu) — their ritual state is valid.
  • If the cleaner used an impure stone, most transmitted positions require subsequent washing with water; relying on an impure stone alone is not acceptable.
  • Excusal applies only to the cleaner; it does not legalize others touching or carrying the object/person bearing the remaining trace. Carrying an actually najis item (e.g., corpse, bloodied vessel, carcass) invalidates the carrier’s prayer unless purified.
  • Sweat that flows after a valid istijmar with a pure stone does not negate the excusal — the site remains treated as pure.
  • Sexual relations after istijmar: if substantive impurity remains, intercourse transmits impurity to the spouse — avoid intercourse unless you are certain impurity was removed (prefer water).
  • Differences and textual issues:
  • Some weak reports attribute permissiveness to using impure materials to Imam Abu Hanifa or al‑Rāfi‘ī; these readings are disputed or weak — the practical school position in the lessons is cautious and favors washing after impure contact.
  • Practical methodological note: scholars may vary and sometimes revise positions when re-questioned; always seek the textual reasoning.

Practical action items (summary)

  • For study and transmission:
  • Begin sessions by reading the book’s estiftah/introduction; follow fatwas in order and invite senior scholars for periodic guidance.
  • When seeking authorization, clarify the intended scope: riwāyah (narration), dirāyah (teaching/mastery), or specific Sufi awrād/maqāmāt.
  • Prefer sustained presence and private reading before requesting ijāzah; request written specification when needed (especially for institutional or public teaching).
  • Preserve ijāzah documents, chains (sanad), and family genealogies; attach any missing mufti answers to proper chapters and report to custodians.
  • Avoid “certificate‑chasing”; prioritize real mastery.
  • For handling manuscripts/Qur’anic copies:
  • Prefer pure paper and clean ink for copying a Mushaf; if using historically manufactured paper of the described kind, accept it as a permissive concession but avoid manifestly impure materials.
  • For ritual purity (istinjā’/istijmar):
  • Prefer using water for istinjā’ whenever feasible.
  • If using a solid: wipe thoroughly (three times); if only a stubborn trace remains that only water or fine pottery removes, the cleaner is excused — but wash if you used an impure stone or suspect impurity transferred.
  • Do not assume the excusal covers others (do not carry or bring potentially najis items into prayer without purification).
  • Avoid intercourse until certain purity is achieved; when in doubt, perform washing.
  • For resolving local juridical differences (e.g., injections in Ramadan):
  • Obtain the detailed reasoning and evidences (texts quoted) from each scholar before adopting a local ruling; prefer the stronger textual basis for community application.
  • Administrative course items:
  • Locate and read referenced works (Adwar al-Tarikh al-Hadramiyya, Tuhfat al-Muhtaj) and the compiler’s ijāzah texts.
  • Schedule next lessons per announced dates; instructor to share photographs/pages when appropriate.

Where Qur’anic phrasing or Qur’an 4:86 guidance on responding to greetings was alluded to, specific verse citations were not always provided.
  • When applying rulings, especially on modern issues (injections, manufactured materials), seek the original texts and reasoning cited by the muftā for authoritative implementation.

Closing etiquette

  • Common closing invocation heard: "Hafazakum Allah wa ra‘akum — fi amān Allāh" (May Allah preserve and protect you). Appropriate short responses: "Wa iyyākum" or "Wa iyyākum fī amān Allāh."
  • Social-religious etiquette: responding to blessings and greetings follows Qur’anic guidance (e.g., Quran 4:86).
