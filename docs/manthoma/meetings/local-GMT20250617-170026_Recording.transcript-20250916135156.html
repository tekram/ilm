Jumuʿah in a small village vs. nearby town

Scenario

  • Small village with fewer than 40 adult males; nearby town has ≥40.
  • Question: May the village hold Jumuʿah (per the Shafiʿi position taught) or must villagers attend the town’s Jumuʿah?

Key ruling (Shafiʿi position presented)

  • A valid Jumuʿah is not established in a place where the resident adult male population is fewer than 40.
  • Villagers thus may not lawfully hold their own Friday prayer under this ruling.
  • If the call (adhān) for Jumuʿah from the nearby town is heard under normal conditions (calm voice, no strong wind, clear hearing), villagers are obliged to attend that Jumuʿah unless they have a valid excuse.
  • If they do not hear the adhan and have no realistic means to attend, they are not obliged to go.

Definitions / conditions emphasized

  • Numerical condition: 40 adult males is treated as the traditional threshold (presented as juristic/historical practice; no explicit Qurʾān/hadīth quoted in the lesson).
  • Settlement (istiwṭān): those who form the congregation must be settled residents of the place, not merely temporary inhabitants (e.g., students who return home seasonally usually do not qualify as "settled").
  • Location: Jumuʿah must be held within an inhabited/settled built-up area; uninhabited ruins, temporary camps, offshore platforms, or purely seasonal encampments are not valid locations in this view.

Differences of opinion / uncertainty

  • The lecturer applied the Shafiʿi (and described as mainstream) criteria. He noted that the Hanafī and Maliki schools may treat settlement or other technicalities differently but did not fully elaborate those variants.
  • For borderline cases consult the specific madhhab or a local scholar.

Practical action items

  • Do not organize independent Jumuʿah in a small village with <40 adult males if you follow the Shafiʿi ruling presented.
  • Attend the nearest valid Jumuʿah if you hear its adhan and have no excuse.
  • Students/temporary residents: determine whether your presence counts as settlement in your madhhab; when unsure, join a nearest recognized Jumuʿah.
  • Do not hold Jumuʿah in uninhabited or only-seasonally populated sites according to this position; check local rulings before establishing one.

Preaching/teaching in a town without official permission; fabricated stories; insulting others

Main questions

  • May a visitor preach/teach or lead prayers in a town without the ruler’s permission?
  • Are fabricated or fictional stories permissible in preaching?
  • Is insulting someone by calling them an animal (e.g., “you donkey”) permitted?

Core rulings

  • A qualified, sincere visitor may preach or teach in small/private settings without the ruler’s permission provided the activity does not cause fitna and no fabricated (false) reports are used.
  • An unqualified person (no adequate knowledge of fiqh, tafsīr, ḥadīth) must not preach even if the ruler permits.
  • Preaching from a reliable book (e.g., well-known trustworthy works) is permissible provided the preacher does not invent or misattribute ḥadīth.
  • Known-forged ḥadīth and fabricated stories presented as authentic are forbidden; authorities should prevent their spread.
  • If local custom requires ruler’s permission for the main/central mosque, one must follow that custom. Otherwise, permission is not required for small informal teaching.
  • If the ruler forbids preaching to prevent fitna, people should generally comply to avoid disorder; the ruler is blameworthy if he unjustly bars legitimate benefit.

Evidence / authorities

  • Ruling relies on Ibn Hajar’s answer cited in the lesson: permission not required for qualified preachers in small settings; unqualified people must not preach even with permission.
  • General juristic principle: obedience to legitimate authority in matters of public order, but unjust prevention is blameworthy on the ruler.

Fictional speech vs. lying

  • Telling fictional/parabolic stories is permissible if you clearly indicate they are fictional/hypothetical.
  • Presenting fabricated stories as real (without disclosure) is impermissible (lying) and forbidden in preaching.

Insults (calling someone an animal)

  • Insulting others with animal names is impermissible when false or abusive; it causes harm and is morally and legally blameworthy.
  • Retaliatory insults remain sinful and generally should be avoided.

Practical action items

  • Ensure your qualification before preaching (knowledge of fiqh, tafsīr, ḥadīth) or preach only from reliable sources.
  • Avoid narrating unauthenticated or forged ḥadīth; use parables only when clearly presented as such.
  • Check local custom: obtain permission for the main mosque if customary; for small private gatherings permission is usually not required.
  • If a ruler forbids you to prevent fitna, comply and seek redress through proper channels rather than open defiance.
  • Report and seek correction from authorities if someone spreads fabricated religious reports.

Who may preach (ahliyya) and mosque permissions

Qualifications to preach

  • Two acceptable bases to preach publicly:
  • The person has requisite scholarly competence (fiqh, tafsīr, ḥadīth).
  • The person preaches from a trustworthy, established book.
  • Without either, the person should not preach.

Permission and place

  • Small mosques/private settings: qualified preachers may generally teach without ruler permission.
  • Major/central mosques: custom usually requires permission from the ruler or the person responsible (to prevent fitna); abide by that custom.
  • Community members may lawfully prevent clearly unqualified persons from preaching.

Obedience and preventing fitna

  • If prohibition aims to prevent fitna, obedience is required even of qualified preachers; if prohibition is arbitrary, the ruler is blameworthy for unjust prevention, but public compliance is advised to avoid greater harm.

Practical action items

  • Seek permission for public preaching in major mosques when custom dictates.
  • Ensure competence or rely on reputable sources; discourage unqualified persons from the pulpit.
  • When in doubt about a ruler’s motives, comply and consult scholars.

Qunūt in Witr and related practice

Positions reported

  • Dominant Shafiʿi position in the lesson: qunūt is not generally prescribed in Witr except in specific cases — it is prescribed in certain situations (noted examples: qunūt in Fajr; qunūt in Witr during the latter half of Ramadan; qunūt during calamities/naazila).
  • A secondary (weak) opinion recommends qunūt in Witr year‑round; this view is considered weak. Al‑Nawawī was mentioned in connection with inclination toward some broader practice.
  • Local practices (e.g., Tarim) vary; some communities recite qunūt more frequently by local custom.

Practical action items

  • Follow your madhhab and the local imam’s practice.
  • If you follow the Shafiʿi mainstream described, do not perform qunūt in Witr except in the specified times (last half of Ramadan, calamity); treat year‑round qunūt as a weak opinion.
  • Ask the local imam when unsure.

Purity (najāsa) rulings: ash, food cooked in impure liquid, eggs

General note

  • Discussion relies on juristic reasoning, local practice, and empirical observations; no explicit Qurʾān or Prophet’s ḥadīth were quoted in the transcript excerpts.

1) Ash of impurity adhering to underside of bread or under roasted meat

  • Textual/initial position: such ash is impurity and would require purification.
  • Authoritative commentary presented: ash of impurity in these cases is treated as pure (no mandatory washing) — this is the established practical ruling adopted in the lesson.

Practical action:

  • Treat such ash as excused; no mandatory washing required by the established practical position in the lesson. If local scholars differ, follow local guidance.

2) Food cooked in impure liquid (e.g., impure water, urine, etc.)

  • Preferred ruling presented: washing the visible outer surface of the cooked food suffices to purify the whole; re-cooking in pure water or squeezing (ʿaṣr) is not required.
  • Minority/weaker opinions:
  • Re-cook the food in pure water.
  • Wash and squeeze to ensure removal.
  • Reasoning: legal concern follows the manifest (outward) condition; purification focuses on external appearance and effect.

Practical action:

  • If food was cooked in impure liquid unknowingly, wash the outside thoroughly — this is generally sufficient in the presented school. Extra measures may be taken for reassurance but are not legally required by this view.

3) Eggs cooked in impure liquid; ash/contact on shell

  • Majority/preferred position in the lesson: egg interior (white/yolk) remains pure; washing the shell suffices. No dislike (makrūh) in eating such eggs once shell cleaned.
  • Maliki/minority position: more cautious — because shells have pores, impurity may penetrate and the egg should be treated like meat (potentially impure).
  • Evidence debated:
  • Maliki evidence: seepage in roasting, penetration of color/flavor, embryological breathing through pores.
  • Rebuttal: observed seepage is generally inner→outer (not necessarily inward contamination); color/flavor penetration does not prove impurity ingress; direction of flow and legal presumption of purity rebut the inference.

Practical action:

  • Per the lesson’s favored position: wash the shell; the egg’s interior is permissible unless there is clear visible ingress or cracking showing contamination.
  • If you follow Maliki rulings or prefer caution, avoid/effect stricter measures.

Local custom (ʿurf): Tarim example

  • Tarim’s local practice is often treated as practical evidence in many matters; visiting scholars consult Tarim’s practices (e.g., funeral rites, qunūt practice).
  • Local customs may determine mosque permissions, prayer variations, and accepted ritual practice — follow established local ʿurf when applicable.

Practical action:

  • Take local custom into account and consult local scholars when cases are borderline.

Overarching evidential note & consultation advice

  • The lessons cited juristic authorities (Ibn Hajar, al‑Nawawī) and local customary practice (e.g., Tarim) but did not recite explicit Qurʾān verses or specific prophetic ḥadīth in the provided excerpts.
  • For borderline or community-level cases (Jumuʿah thresholds, mosque permissions, public food served, disputed impurity), consult a qualified local scholar who can apply the relevant evidences and the applicable madhhab position.

---

Practical checklist (quick)

  • Jumuʿah: If your place <40 settled adult males → attend nearest valid Jumuʿah when you hear its adhan.
  • Preaching: Only preach if qualified or from a trustworthy book; seek permission for the main mosque if custom requires it; avoid fabricated reports.
  • Fictional stories: Label them fiction; do not present them as real.
  • Insults: Avoid animal-name insults and abusive speech.
  • Food/impurity: Wash visible surfaces; eggs interior presumed pure unless visibly contaminated; follow local madhhab for stricter positions.
