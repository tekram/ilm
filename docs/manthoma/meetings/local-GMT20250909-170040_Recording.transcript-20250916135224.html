Sale of Inherited Shares - September 09, 2025

Start: 2025-09-09T17:00:40

Case: sale and post‑sale vows (nadhar) in a joint inheritance

Facts

  • Three brothers inherit mixed estate (gold, silver, land, livestock, furniture, cash) held jointly.
  • One brother (seller) sold his inherited share to a co‑heir (buyer) and received payment.
  • After the sale both parties made explicit vows: the seller vowed the sold items to the buyer and the buyer vowed the price to the seller. Both vows were expressed as binding obligations. Parties were adult, sane, competent.
  • The seller later died; his heirs claim the sold property as part of the estate. The buyer relies on the sale and the vows.
  • Additional questions: effect of a pre‑death confession by the seller; effect and reward for a reconciler appointed by the parties.

Key rulings (concise)

  • The sale, as described, is likely defective (void/baatil or fasid) in many typical joint‑share cases; such defective sales do not transfer ownership in the usual way.
  • A vow (nadhar) made after a sale that is void is nevertheless a valid, independent religious obligation; the vow remains binding even if the antecedent sale is null.
  • Consequence in this case (given the facts presented): if the deceased seller vowed to give what he had purported to sell, heirs generally have no claim against the buyer on that object — the post‑sale vow binds the seller and thus his estate to the extent intended by the vow.
  • If instead the sale was in fact valid and became binding, or if the vow was made during a khiyār (option) period, different rulings apply: a vow during khiyār may annul the sale in some transmissions; a vow after a firm sale is an independent obligation. Outcomes depend on timing and proof.
  • A reconciler (accepted by disputants) who effects reconciliation is rewarded spiritually; an accepted settlement reached with clear terms and witnesses is legally effective.

Definitions and jurisprudential points used

  • Void/defective sale (baatil/fasid): a sale affected by legal defects (e.g., prohibited riba‑type barter of like metals without equality/hand‑to‑hand, ambiguity of subject or price, selling undivided joint shares without specification).
  • Nadhar (vow): a unilateral religious declaration creating a binding obligation; valid whether it concerns existent or nonexistent, known or unknown items (classical position cited).
  • Khiyār (option): period in which a contracting party may rescind the sale; whether a vow made in khiyār annuls the sale is a disputed technical matter.
  • Transfer (taslim / qabḍ): physical/constructive delivery and receipt; decisive for whether ownership passed.

Evidences and authorities

  • No Qurʾān or Prophetic ḥadīth were quoted in the transcript; the ruling relies on madhhab literature and fatāwā.
  • Classical/Shāfiʿī authorities cited/transmitted:
  • Shaykh ʿAbdullāh ibn ʿUmar ibn Yaḥyā — position: vow stands even if preceding sale is invalid.
  • Imam Muḥammad ibn Sulaymān al‑Kurdi (as transmitted by Muḥammad ibn ʿAbdullāh al‑Basūdān) — warning that vows made before a sale becomes binding can invalidate both sale and vow in some circumstances.
  • Abu Bakr al‑Khaṭīb — nuanced reading limiting generic vows to what actually belonged at the time of the vow (post‑inheritance increases excluded unless specified).
  • General reliance on Shāfiʿī texts (al‑Baghiyah, al‑Tuhfa, Nawawī transmissions).

Differences of opinion / technical distinctions

  • Timing is decisive:
  • Sale valid and final → vow functions as a separate obligation; buyer’s ownership generally protected.
  • Sale valid but within khiyār → some transmitters (Ibn ʿUmar) treat a vow during khiyār as annulling the sale.
  • Sale void from the outset (e.g., illegal silver‑for‑silver) → the sale is treated as non‑existent and a subsequent vow is an independent valid gift/obligation.
  • Scope of a generic vow phrased “all that pertains to me from my father’s inheritance”:
  • Some jurists interpret it to include only what the vower actually had at the moment of the vow; increases after inheritance are not included unless expressly stated.
  • Others read broader coverage but commentators advise precise wording to avoid dispossessing heirs.

Practical action items (sale & vow disputes)

  • Immediately collect and preserve evidence:
  • Written sale contract, receipts, date/time stamps, witnesses, proof of payment, proof of delivery/possession.
  • Record text and time of the vows and witnesses to those declarations.
  • Verify factual sequence and legal defects:
  • Was delivery (qabḍ) effected? Was the sale of any like metals subject to riba rules? Was the subject and price specified? Was the sale within or after khiyār?
  • If sale is shown void but a clear post‑sale vow exists: treat the vow as binding; consult a qualified shariʿa judge for enforcement against the deceased’s estate.
  • If facts are uncertain or conflicting: bring the case to a shariʿa court or competent mujtahid with all documents and witnesses; do not rely on assumptions.
  • Reconciliation: encourage mediation; an accepted settlement with clear terms and witnesses is effective and meritorious.

Purification (najāsa): stains, odors, and required procedures

Classifications and definitions

  • By intensity: mughaladha (severe, e.g., dog/pig), mutawassiṭa (intermediate), mukhafafa (light, e.g., infant pre‑weaning urine).
  • By detectability:
  • ʿAyniyya (sensory/visible): impurity perceptible by color, smell, or taste — removal of substance and its qualities required.
  • Ḥukmiyya (imperceptible/judicial): impurity not perceptible — running water over the area suffices.
  • Technical steps:
  • al‑hath (scraping/rubbing) and al‑qars (pinching/rubbing with fingers) — methods to bring out embedded residue.

Key rulings and procedures

  • Dog/pig contamination: seven washes required; one wash should use pure earth mixed into water to murk it (classical majority).
  • Infant urine (pre‑weaning): rinsing with water until clean is sufficient; scrubbing not required.
  • Dried or imperceptible impurity: running water over the spot suffices if you are certain of its prior existence but sense no trace.
  • If color, smell, or taste remains:
  • Majority position: attempt removal (al‑hath/al‑qars, repeated washings); if removal is difficult or impossible after reasonable effort, many jurists treat the spot as purified or at least excused.
  • Some jurists require further removal (even using implements/“teeth”) when feasible; there is a minority stricter view.
  • Obligation of al‑hath and al‑qars: the lesson adopts the position that these are required steps (a condition) before declaring purity, not mere recommendation.
  • Threshold practical rule: if impurity persists after three washings with al‑hath/al‑qars each time, and further removal is impracticable, one may — according to an authoritative strand — consider the item pure; but consult local authority because there is a dissenting opinion.

Differences of opinion

  • Whether al‑hath/al‑qars are obligatory or merely recommended.
  • Whether persistence of smell/color after washing excuses the person (majority allow excusal in hardship; some insist on complete removal using forceful means if feasible).

Practical action items (cleaning and ritual practice)

  • For sensory impurities: perform al‑hath and al‑qars and wash up to three times; use appropriate cleaning agents and attempt to remove smell/color.
  • For dog/pig: perform seven washes; include one wash with earth.
  • If residue persists despite sincere, reasonable effort (three washings plus rubbing/scraping), either:
  • Attempt implement removal if safe and feasible; or
  • If removal is truly impossible, rely on the lenient strand but consult a local scholar when necessary (especially before prayer).
  • Document cleaning attempts where institutional rulings are required (e.g., bank/organization guidance).

Partnership withdrawals: rabb al‑māl and mudārib

Scenario

  • Capital owner (rabb al‑māl) and working partner (mudārib) run a joint business; both had permission to “take what they need” or withdrawals occurred at separation.

Rulings applied

  • Mudārib withdrawals:
  • With permission: treated as an advance/loan (debt) to be settled in final accounting; mudārib must return equivalent or settle at partition.
  • Without permission: treated as unauthorized taking — mudārib liable to return goods or pay compensation at market value.
  • Rabb al‑māl withdrawals:
  • Capital owner’s withdrawals are treated as withdrawals from his capital/entitlement, not as theft from the mudārib (assuming they fall within agreed rights); they reduce his capital/share accordingly.
  • General principle: mudārib does not own profit or entitlement until accounting and division; unauthorized consumption by mudārib creates obligation to repay.

Practical action items (for partnerships)

  • Document any agreement on withdrawals and permitted uses (written clause preferable).
  • At separation prepare inventory and record all authorized/unauthorized withdrawals; treat unauthorized mudārib withdrawals as debt/liability.
  • If permission was given informally, document it during reconciliation to avoid later disputes.
  • Use clear accounting in partition: apply withdrawals against capital or record as advances per agreement.

  • Key reading recommended: Shaykh Muḥammad Taqi al‑Uthmānī, Fiqh al‑Buyūʿ (two volumes) for contemporary sales jurisprudence.

Practical action items (research & institutional practice)

  • Use AAOIFI and Islamic Economic Forum outputs as primary practical references for institutional rulings; cite Majmaʿ and bank boards as corroboration.
  • Maintain communication lines with forum/board scholars for case‑specific clarifications.
  • Adopt a studied group opinion process for institutional fatāwā; keep model contract forms and clear drafting clauses (e.g., conditioning vows on sale becoming binding).

Final summary — bottom line and recommended steps

  • Verify facts first: sequence (sale → vows), proof of payment, delivery/possession, precise wording of vows, and whether sale fell within khiyār or was null from the start.
  • Legal conclusion based on the lesson:
  • If the sale was void: a subsequent vow by the seller is still valid and typically bars heirs from reclaiming the vowed items.
  • If the sale was valid: timing matters — vows during khiyār may annul the sale in some transmissions; vows after a firm sale create independent obligations.
  • For disputes: gather documents and witnesses, and refer the case to a competent shariʿa authority or court for adjudication.
  • For purification and ritual practice: follow the stepwise cleaning procedure (al‑hath/al‑qars, repeated washings), apply seven washes with earth for dog/pig, and consult a jurist in borderline cases where removal is impracticable.
  • For partnerships and institutional practice: document withdrawal permissions, account at partition, and treat unauthorized mudārib withdrawals as liabilities.
  • Encourage reconciliation: a reconciler accepted by the parties who settles the matter is both legally effective (if properly witnessed/recorded) and spiritually meritorious.
