Topic 1 — Traveling on Friday (Jumuʿah)

Key rulings

  • Two basic scenarios when one leaves his town:
  • If the destination is within the same locality such that the call to Jumuʿah from either place is heard in the other (treated as one locality): travel does not excuse one from attending Jumuʿah in his origin town; one must return and attend unless a valid excuse exists.
  • If the destination is not within that locality, timing matters:
  • If one departed before Fajr of that Friday: one is not required to return to attend Jumuʿah.
  • If one departed after Fajr: one should either join a Jumuʿah congregation en route or return to attend Jumuʿah in his town if reasonably possible; leaving after Fajr while thereby missing Jumuʿah is blameworthy except for a genuine excuse.
  • If an unexpected obstacle makes crossing impossible:
  • If one can still return and catch Jumuʿah, one must return and attend.
  • If, before departure, one reasonably believed the crossing would be possible and only later it became impossible, one is excused and bears no sin.
  • Waiting for an expected ease of crossing:
  • Permitted when the prevailing reasonable expectation is that crossing will become easy and one still expects to catch Jumuʿah (Ibn Hajar cited permitting waiting when ease is dominant).
  • Not permitted when waiting would unreasonably risk missing Jumuʿah.
  • Performing Dhuhr in place of Jumuʿah:
  • Do not perform Dhuhr where Jumuʿah is obligatory while still hoping to catch Jumuʿah.
  • One may perform Dhuhr only after one despaired of catching any Jumuʿah — practically: when one will arrive only after the imam has already said the salām.

Evidences / authorities

  • No explicit Qurʾan/hadith quoted in this segment. Rulings drawn from classical juristic works and commentaries: Al-Ramli, Shibrani, Al-Bujairimi, Ibn Hajar, and marginalia/commentaries.

Differences of opinion / notes

  • Jurists differ on exact duties when travel begins after dawn and whether returning is required in all cases; some allow travel if one reasonably expected Jumuʿah en route, others insist on returning unless excused.
  • Ibn Hajar’s view allows waiting when ease is the dominant expectation.

Practical action items

  • Before traveling on Friday, check departure time relative to Fajr.
  • If leaving after Fajr, plan to attend Jumuʿah (en route or by return) unless you have a valid excuse.
  • If blocked by an unexpected obstacle, return if you can catch Jumuʿah; if the obstacle was unforeseeable, you are excused.
  • Do not perform Dhuhr in place of Jumuʿah until you are sure you cannot reach any Jumuʿah (i.e., after the imam’s salām).

Topic 2 — Funeral practice: orienting the deceased (facing the qibla)

Key rulings

  • It is recommended (sunnah) to place the deceased facing the qibla both before and after ghusl (washing) and kafān (shrouding) whenever possible.
  • According to the lecturer and his transmitted practice, positioning the body to face the qibla inside the grave is treated as obligatory (wajib) in their locality/madhhab practice.

Evidences / authorities

  • No explicit Qurʾan/hadith quoted in this segment; rulings presented as transmitted juristic positions and practice of the lecturer’s teachers.

Differences of opinion / usul note

  • When texts say simply “face the qibla,” the lecturer read that as a general (itlaq) command rather than a statement limited by later specifications (takhsis); treat unqualified commands as generally applicable unless a clear limitation appears.

Practical action items

  • When preparing a deceased person: if possible, orient the body toward the qibla both before and after washing and shrouding.
  • In the grave: take care to place the body so it faces the qibla (considered obligatory in the lecturer’s practice).

Related funeral/mosque conduct: tahiyyat al‑masjid and standing for the janāzah

Key rulings

  • On entering the mosque with a funeral present: perform tahiyyat al‑masjid (the two‑rakʿah greeting) upon entry.
  • If one begins the tahiyyah and then the coffin is placed before one sits, remaining standing until the coffin is set does not negate the sunnah of standing for the janāzah; performing tahiyyah and staying standing is acceptable.
  • If intending to join the janāzah prayer, some authorities (preferred by the lecturer citing al‑Nawawī) advise not to sit before the coffin is placed.

Evidences / authorities

  • Classical madhhab discussions cited; a hadith about the Prophet and funerals was alluded to but not fully quoted.

Practical action items

  • Enter mosque → perform tahiyyat al‑masjid; if a funeral is taking place, remain standing until the coffin is placed if you intend to join janāzah.

Mixing sunnah acts with obligatory rukun (brief rule)

  • Performing additional recitation or dhikr (e.g., Mu'awwidhat) in a sunnah prayer adjacent to an obligatory prayer does not invalidate the obligatory prayer nor materially mix intentions to its detriment; keep intentions clear but small overlaps are permitted.

Dog bite and dog saliva (purification)

Key rulings

  • Dog bite: follow the prophetic prescription — wash the bitten place seven times, one of which should include earth (tatrīb). Many scholars require excising the actual bitten portion of meat as a precaution; others accept washing alone.
  • Dog saliva without a bite (licking): consensus that the vessel/area must be washed.
  • Dog scratch by claws: does not equate to the bite‑scenario that requires excision.

Evidence

  • Hadith: “If a dog licks your vessel, wash it seven times, one of them with earth” — reported in the hadith collections (e.g., Bukhari/Muslim). This is the basis for the seven‑wash rule.

Practical action items

  • If a dog bites meat or a person: wash the affected place seven times with one wash involving earth; consider excising the bitten portion for meat if following the stricter precaution.
  • If a dog licks meat or a vessel: wash thoroughly (seven times with one using earth per the prophetic instruction is the adopted practice).
  • When in doubt, follow the majority/traditional hadith‑based practice (seven washes, one with earth).

Vaginal and penile discharges: definitions, rulings, differences

Definitions

  • Madhy: pre‑ejaculatory fluid — considered impure (najis).
  • Mani: semen — impure (with details depending on sequence and contamination).
  • Sweat (ʿaraq): generally pure.
  • Rutooba al‑farj: vaginal moisture/secretions (white-ish or clear) — debated status.
  • Al‑qissa al‑bayḍā’ (post‑menstrual white discharge): whitish discharge that may follow menstruation and indicate return to purity.

Key rulings

  • Stronger/primary view presented: vaginal moisture that resembles sweat and the post‑menstrual white discharge are to be treated as pure (analogous to sweat); the white discharge after menses is a sign of tahārah (Imam al‑Shāfiʿī reported to Aḥmad).
  • Sexual fluids (madhy and mani) are impure and require purification; if impure fluids preceded later fluids, rulings may change (stricter).
  • If impurity is not firmly established, presume purity (default: tahārah).

Differences of opinion

  • Inner‑sourced vaginal moisture: Ibn Hajar — pure; Ibn al‑Raml — impure. Practically, both agree newborns or eggs need not be washed after contact even if the discharge is considered impure.
  • Post‑menstrual white discharge: al‑Shāfiʿī treats it as a tail of the menses (marker of purity); other jurists vary.

Practical action items

  • Distinguish discharges by color, timing, consistency, smell, and relation to intercourse or menses.
  • Treat clear/white, sweat‑like moisture and the typical post‑menses white discharge as pure by the stronger view; act stricter if signs indicate sexual fluids (madhy/mani) or internal origin of impurity.
  • If semen/madhy is emitted, apply the corresponding purification (washing, ghusl if required).
  • When uncertain, give priority to the default of purity but consult a local scholar if the situation affects ritual obligations.

Spousal duties, tamkīn, nunūz (disobedience), and nafqa (maintenance)

Key definitions

  • Tamkīn: enabling/being available to the husband for conjugal relations.
  • Nunūz: disobedience — serious refusal of marital duties (e.g., persistent denial of tamkīn without valid excuse).
  • Nafqa: financial maintenance.

Key rulings and differences

  • Tamkīn and remaining in the home are distinct duties; absence or refusal may be assessed independently.
  • Exceptions where a wife may leave without husband’s permission: fleeing danger, seeking medical treatment, attending court to seek rights, obtaining essential religious counsel, and routine customary public activities.
  • Differences on maintenance (nafqa) when a wife leaves:
  • Ibn Hajar: in some cases of extended absence for her benefit, the husband may suspend maintenance.
  • Al‑Raml: if the husband gave permission, nafqa continues.
  • The wife’s domestic service is considered a religious obligation in the Hanafi presentation cited, but not enforceable by court (sinful if refused, yet not judicially compelled).

Practical action items

  • A wife may leave for safety, medical treatment, court, or to seek a religious ruling without husband’s permission.
  • If permission to leave was given, keep clear communication/documentation to avoid later disputes over nafqa.
  • For prolonged absence or contested nafqa, consult a local scholar or judge with full facts.
  • Husbands may prohibit actions that create legitimate suspicion damaging the marriage; mediation is recommended for conflicts.

Short consolidated checklist (practical steps)

  • Traveling on Friday:
  • Left before Fajr → generally exempt from returning.
  • Left after Fajr → try to attend Jumuʿah en route or return; wait only if reasonable expectation of making Jumuʿah exists; perform Dhuhr only after despairing of any Jumuʿah.
  • At funerals:
  • Place the deceased facing qibla after ghusl/kafan; orient in grave (obligatory in lecturer’s practice).
  • Entering mosque with funeral → perform tahiyyat and remain standing until coffin placed if intending janāzah.
  • Dog incidents:
  • Dog bite → wash affected place seven times, one wash with earth; excise bitten meat if following stricter view.
  • Dog licks vessel/food → wash (seven times/one with earth recommended).
  • Sexual/vaginal fluids:
  • Treat madhy/mani as impure; treat typical sweat‑like vaginal moisture and post‑menstrual white discharge as pure per stronger view; distinguish by color/timing.
  • Spousal matters:
  • Leave for safety/medical/court/fatwa → permitted without husband’s permission.
  • For contested extended absence and nafqa → consult local scholar/judge.

Authorities cited (selected)
