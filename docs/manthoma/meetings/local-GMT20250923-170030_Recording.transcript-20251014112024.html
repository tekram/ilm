Cheese and Carrion Rulings - September 23, 2025

Start: 2025-09-23T17:00:30

Definitions

  • Anfahah (الانفحة): the substance taken from an animal’s stomach (rennet) used to coagulate milk and make cheese.
  • Maytah (الميتة): an animal that died without lawful ritual slaughter.
  • “Magus / المجوس”: a label used here for people whose slaughter is not valid for Muslims (generic for disallowed slaughterers).
  • Najis / ṭāhir (نجس / طاهر): ritually impure / pure.
  • Ghalabat al‑ṭahārah (غلبه الطهارة): presumption of purity when purity predominates.
  • Al‑asl (الأصل): the original legal presumption (e.g., things are pure unless known otherwise).
  • Al‑ghālib (الغالب): the prevailing probability or dominant circumstance that may point away from the original presumption.

Main ruling on rennet (anfahah) and cheese

  • Two primary positions:
  • Abu Hanifa (reported): rennet is ritually pure; cheese made with rennet taken from a maytah or from the slaughter of a disallowed slaughterer (e.g., Magians) remains pure and may be eaten.
  • Opposing position (commentators / the speakers’ school): rennet taken from a maytah or disallowed slaughter is impure; cheese made from such rennet is not permissible if you know its source is impure.
  • Exception agreed by many jurists: if the rennet came from a very young animal that had only fed on milk (suckling), its stomach contents are treated as milk — using it is permissible because the substance was pure milk.
  • Practical rule: knowledge of source controls the ruling — known impurity forbids consumption; unknown origin invokes the presumption of purity unless context makes impurity more likely.

Evidences and juristic reasoning

  • Presumption of purity (ghalabat al‑ṭahārah): do not declare impurity without clear evidence or trustworthy report. This principle underlies permissive rulings for market foods.
  • Prophetic practice cited:
  • Report that the Prophet ﷺ ate Syrian cheese without inquiring about its source — used by Ibn Hajar (Al‑Tuhfa) as practical evidence to permit common market foods without interrogation.
  • Report that the Prophet ﷺ wore a robe woven by Magians — cited to support permissibility of wearing garments associated with non‑Muslim producers in ordinary cases.
  • Methodological principle: al‑asl (original presumption) generally prevails over al‑ghālib, but some jurists accept al‑ghālib in specific technical cases; al‑Qarāfī gives precedence to al‑asl except where earlier jurists explicitly followed the ghālib.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • Rennet/cheese:
  • Abu Hanifa: pure regardless of source (maytah or disallowed slaughter).
  • Other scholars/commentators: impure if source is known impure; exception for suckling animals’ stomachs.
  • Market items and exposed food:
  • Some jurists: presume purity of market goods (yusr) and accept items sold openly by Muslims/Ahl al‑Kitāb.
  • Others: if an item is found lying exposed (unwrapped) in a public place, treat it as suspicious/impure because ordinarily edible food would not be left exposed.
  • Skins/leather:
  • Basri position: skin is like meat — impure if derived from impure animal.
  • Alternate view: skin is like hair — inherently pure; different rules apply.
  • Use of al‑ghālib vs al‑asl:
  • Majority tendency in the lesson: prefer al‑asl; exceptions exist where jurists acted on al‑ghālib (e.g., the juice→vinegar→wine divorce example).

Illustrative jurisprudential examples (methodology: al‑asl vs al‑ghālib)

  • Carrion claim: do not accept a seller’s bare assurance that meat is lawful if the original prohibition (carrion) applies; require proof (bayyina).
  • Juice → vinegar → wine (divorce scenario): some jurists applied al‑ghālib (accepted probable fermentation stage) and ruled a divorce occurred; others held to al‑asl (no divorce without certainty). This shows authorized exceptions to the general preference for al‑asl.
  • Creature touching vessel (uncertain drinking): rely on al‑asl (purity) when drinking is not observed and impurity is not certain.
  • Seminal emission after ghusl: act on the prevailing likelihood that emission relates to prior intercourse — ghusl is required.
  • Khuff wiping, qasr, and similar concessions: in doubt, act to preserve worship validity (perform required action) and apply rukhsa when conditions are met.
  • Witness testimony: judges may act on credible testimony even when it appears to conflict with original presumption; judicial context permits reliance on apparent facts.

Related rulings summarized

  • Market goods:
  • If sold openly by Muslims or People of the Book in the market (in containers/hands), presume permissibility.
  • If sold in a place where non‑permissible slaughter predominates, verify provenance before consuming.
  • Exposed items found in streets/markets:
  • Exposed flesh or cheese: avoid unless origin is known; suspicious because edible food is rarely left exposed. Bare bones are treated as pure.
  • Garments and treated materials:
  • If manufacturing likely involved impure animal fat, treat garment as suspect; but prophetic reports (robe by Magians, use of grease) support permissibility of ordinary garments absent clear impurity.
  • Dog contact:
  • If only the dog’s head touched a cloth and no licking occurred (and wetness is minor), many jurists permit continued use; if licking/contamination occurred, follow purification rulings.
  • Stolen/illicit goods and transformation:
  • Goods taken unjustly (ghasb) remain prohibited to buy/eat as long as owner’s right remains; if transformed and mixed beyond identification or sold by the imam’s agent, many jurists permit purchase.
  • For processed products made from stolen materials, rulings vary by school — check local madhhab or authority.
  • Gambling proceeds (sahth):
  • Items known to be proceeds of gambling (e.g., cracked eggs used in a game) are forbidden; eggs merely used temporarily and returned without loss are not necessarily prohibited.

Practical action items (clear, actionable)

  • Buying cheese/market food:
  • Prefer vendors who are Muslim or Ahl al‑Kitāb and sell openly — assume permissibility.
  • In areas dominated by non‑permissible slaughterers, verify provenance or avoid if origin unknown.
  • Finding exposed meat/cheese:
  • Do not eat exposed flesh/cheese unless provenance is confirmed; bare bones are permissible.
  • Suspected impurity without evidence (waswasa):
  • Do not act on vague doubts; rely on presumption of purity unless there is concrete evidence or trustworthy report.
  • If informed by a reliable person that a product comes from maytah/disallowed slaughter:
  • Avoid consuming it.
  • Garments and animal‑fat treatment:
  • Do not discard or over‑wash new clothes out of mere suspicion; investigate only when there is reasonable cause (e.g., known manufacturer practice).
  • Dog contact:
  • If licking is not established, many jurists allow continued use; if licking occurred, follow cleaning rules (wash affected area).
  • Judges and adjudicators:
  • Default to al‑asl unless strong, specific precedent or convincing probability (al‑ghālib) applies; require bayyina for claims contradicting the original rule (e.g., lawful slaughter vs carrion).
  • Cite precedents when accepting al‑ghālib in technical cases.

Key cautions and general principles

  • The decisive factor is knowledge/evidence about the origin or contamination. Known impurity forbids; unknown origin favors permissibility.
  • Avoid excessive scrupulosity (waswasa, tanattuʿ): do not invent extra acts of worship or purification without valid reason; scholars caution that extreme rigor beyond textual warrant is blameworthy.
  • Apply the majority/prevailing reality (ghalaba) sensibly: if Muslims predominate in a market, presume purity; if non‑Muslims predominate, be cautious.
  • When in equipoise (istiwāʾ) or when the presumption of purity prevails, do not adopt innovative prohibitions.

Evidences and authorities referenced

  • Prophetic practice: report that the Prophet ﷺ ate Syrian cheese without inquiry (cited via Ibn Hajar, Al‑Tuhfa). Report that the Prophet ﷺ wore garments woven by Magians — used as practical evidence.
  • Jurists cited: Ibn ʿAbbās (reports), Ibn Hajar, al‑Nawawi, al‑Qarāfī, al‑ʿAbbādī, al‑Zubayrī, al‑Dārimī — referenced for principles and specific rulings.
  • Methodological principles invoked: presumption of purity (ghalabat al‑ṭahārah), al‑asl vs al‑ghālib, rukhsa (concession), avoiding bidʿa (innovation) and tanattuʿ (excessive stringency).

Short consolidated takeaway

  • Do not label cheese made with rennet impure unless you know the rennet came from a maytah or disallowed slaughterer; Abu Hanifa permits it, others forbid it — rely on your madhhab or trustworthy evidence.
  • For market foods and doubtful contamination, rely on presumption of purity unless clear evidence or trustworthy testimony indicates impurity; avoid needless suspicion, but verify when contextual signs make impurity likely.
  • Judges should generally adhere to the original presumption (al‑asl) but may accept the prevailing probability (al‑ghālib) in narrowly defined technical cases — always require clear evidence to overturn the original rule.
