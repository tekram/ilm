Agency and Payment Rights - August 12, 2025

📅 2025-08-12 🕐 17:02

Case summary

  • A woman authorized an agent to hire a pilgrim to perform Hajj on behalf of her daughter. The agent demanded his fee afterwards; the principal refused, claiming the authorized act was not performed (or that the written record differed). The agent insists he fulfilled the contract. Central dispute: did performance occur or is the issue only a defect in writing/documentation?

Key legal questions

  • When principal and agent disagree about performance, whose claim is accepted absent evidence?
  • Does a mistake in the written record (wrong name/wording) invalidate the agency act (e.g., Hajj on behalf of another)?
  • If the hired pilgrim intended (made niyyah) for the intended person despite name confusion, is the Hajj valid?
  • In related disputes (e.g., allegations of sale at gross loss—ghubn fahish), whose allegation is presumed?

Definitions

  • Muwakkil: principal (who appoints/hires).
  • Wakil: agent/contractor.
  • Iqrār: admission/confession by a party.
  • Muʾathir vs. ghayr muʾathir: whether an error/admission affects the legal ruling (material vs. non‑material).

Core legal principles and rulings

  • Presumption (al‑asl): The default rule is non‑occurrence — actions are presumed not to have happened unless proven. Thus when a principal denies performance and the agent merely claims it occurred, the principal’s denial generally prevails absent proof.
  • Burden of proof: The claimant who asserts that the contractual duty was performed must prove it (witnesses, receipts, corroboration).
  • Written record alone is not decisive: A written document without corroboration (witnesses, testimony of the pilgrim, other evidence) may be insufficient to settle the dispute.
  • Mistake in naming/writing: A clerical or naming mistake does not necessarily invalidate acts of worship. If the pilgrim actually intended for the correct person (niyyah), and there is contextual indication (pointing, description), the act can be valid despite recording errors.
  • Credibility for exculpatory claims: If the agent claims a fact that would absolve him from liability (loss, return, damage), courts may be prepared to accept his claim to avoid imposing liability without evidence. This is applied more readily where acceptance of the claim removes liability rather than imposes rights.
  • Confession (iqrār): An agent’s admission that he failed or erred is significant. One must then assess whether the admission establishes a material breach (requires remedy) or only a non‑affecting error.

Evidences & authorities cited

  • Classical fiqh authorities and commentaries referenced: Ali al‑Shibramalsi (gloss), Hashiyat al‑Rawd, Sheikh Abd al‑Rahman al‑Mashlul (citing Al‑Kurdi).
  • No explicit Qur’anic verses or Prophetic hadith were cited in this portion of the lesson; reliance was on fiqh evidentiary maxims (presumption, value of admission).

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • View A (majority position applied here): In absence of proof, favor the denier (principal) — presume non‑performance (al‑asl).
  • View B (alternative): In some contexts one may accept the claimant who asserts correctness (belief in the claimant of validity), which can support the agent’s assertion that the task was performed; applicability is contested and context‑dependent.
  • Nuance: Courts are likelier to accept the agent’s statement when it serves to exonerate him from liability (e.g., claiming return or loss) than when it creates entitlement without corroboration.
  • Specific disputes (e.g., ghubn fahish) may require different treatments depending on particulars and stronger proof rules explained in the glosses.

Practical action items

  • For principals (muwakkil):
  • Obtain witnesses and documentary evidence at appointment and completion (written authorization, receipts, confirmations).
  • If refusing payment, state the factual basis and request the agent’s proof (witnesses, pilgrim’s declaration).
  • For agents (wakil):
  • Document all steps: contract, receipts, confirmations from the hired pilgrim, witness statements.
  • Secure an explicit declaration of niyyah from the pilgrim (an oral confirmation or witnesses) that the pilgrimage was performed on behalf of the named principal — this preserves validity despite errors in writing.
  • For judges/adjudicators:
  • Do not accept a written record as conclusive by itself; seek corroboration (witnesses, testimony of the pilgrim, contemporaneous evidence).
  • Apply the default rule (al‑asl) where appropriate, but consider accepting agent testimony when it reasonably serves to exonerate him and is not contradicted by stronger evidence.
  • When an agent admits failure, determine whether the admission is legally material and apply remedies accordingly (repayment, replacement, compensation).

Practical examples and applications

  • Hiring someone to recite Qur’an at a grave or hiring a pilgrim for Hajj: If principal denies the act and agent claims performance, require proof (witnesses, pilgrim’s declaration). If the pilgrim declares he performed the rite for the intended person, the worship is valid even if a name was written incorrectly.
  • Name/writing mistakes: If context or pointing identifies the intended person, or the actual intent (niyyah) by the performer is established, the act is upheld.

Conclusion

  • The dispute is fundamentally evidentiary: without corroboration the principal’s denial generally stands (presumption of non‑occurrence). However, convincing evidence by the agent (witnesses, pilgrim’s declaration of niyyah, receipts) will validate the agent’s claim and discharge his obligation.
  • Mistakes in writing or naming do not automatically invalidate worship when intention and context show who was meant.
  • Best practice to avoid such disputes: document transactions, obtain witnesses, and secure explicit confirmation of niyyah at the time of performance.
