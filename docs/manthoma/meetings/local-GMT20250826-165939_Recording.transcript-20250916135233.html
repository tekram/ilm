Bequests and Timing Rules - August 26, 2025

📅 2025-08-26 🕐 16:59

Bequests and Hajj/Umrah (time‑specific bequests) — definitions and core principles

  • Istita‘ah (ability): Whether Hajj/Umrah owed after death depends on whether the deceased was mustaṭīʿ (able) — financially and physically — prior to death. If he was able and died before performing obligatory Hajj, the Hajj is a debt on his estate and must be fulfilled by heirs.
  • Ajrat al‑mithl (customary wage): Payment to a hired pilgrim acting as proxy should be the prevailing/market wage for performing Hajj/Umrah on another’s behalf.
  • Time‑specific stipulation: If a bequest/waqf explicitly requires the act at a particular time (e.g., “in Ramadan” or “on the nights of Ramadan”), that timing is part of the condition and must be observed; if missed, the make‑up may need to occur at the same time in the next occurrence unless the stipulation was only a preference.

Key rulings (merged and non‑redundant)

  • Unfulfilled annual Hajj/Umrah specified in a will:
  • If some annual payments/performances were missed (e.g., two years), heirs may hire separate proxies to perform each missed pilgrimage in a later year; hiring two different people to make up two missed years is permissible and, according to the cited opinion, obligatory when necessary to discharge the estate’s liability.
  • One pilgrim cannot validly perform two separate obligatory Hajj obligations (for different years) in a single pilgrimage as if it counts for multiple years.
  • Insufficient yield from waqf/estate:
  • If at inception the bequest was feasible (estate/waqf could cover it) but later temporarily lacks yield, it is permissible to wait until funds accumulate to perform the stipulated acts.
  • If the bequest demanded an impossible/implausible amount from the start (estate could never have provided it), the stipulation is invalid: it cannot bind the estate in that impossible form, though voluntary performance remains meritorious.
  • Hajj for a deceased who was mustaṭīʿ:
  • Heirs must expend estate funds to hire a qualified proxy and pay ajrat al‑mithl; the Hajj is treated like an estate debt.
  • Prefer performing a muwaṣṣal (complete, valid) Hajj entered from the correct miqāt for the deceased (majority/safer view).
  • If heirs disagree about using shares, obtain consent of capable heirs or follow the local madhhab/court ruling; a safe practical route is to fund the Hajj from consenting heirs’ shares.
  • Timing when a time‑limited bequest was missed:
  • If the bequest explicitly required the act in a particular time, make‑up should be in that same occasion (e.g., next Ramadan) per Ibn Hajar/al‑Nawawi’s positions.
  • If the timing was merely preferred, the act may be performed at another suitable time.

Evidences and authorities cited

  • Classical jurists: Ibn Hajar and al‑Nawawi cited regarding time‑limited bequests and make‑ups.
  • Contemporary fatwas referenced (e.g., Shaykh Ibn Baz) on practical issues (miqāt, hiring pilgrims).
  • No direct Qur’anic verse or Prophetic hadith was quoted in the lesson transcript for these specific bequest rulings; rulings presented as established juristic practice. For estate/amanah principles and general evidence consult qualified mufti for case application.

Differences of opinion / practical nuances

  • Whether an estate must remain entirely blocked (mahjūr) until the deceased’s Hajj is actually performed, or whether only the cost need be set aside — juristic divide; practice varies by madhhab/local court.
  • Using estate funds to perform Hajj for a person who was not actually able: majority disallow absent a prior valid will or unanimous heirs’ consent; some allow in practice if heirs agree.
  • Ihrām/miqāt for proxy Hajj: majority require entering ihrām from the proper miqāt for the person; some allowances exist in hardship to enter from the nearer boundary (adnā al‑ḥil), but the safer position is proper miqāt.

Practical checklist for heirs/executors (action items)

  • Establish mustaṭīʿ status at death. If deceased was able and did not perform Hajj, treat unpaid Hajj as an estate debt.
  • Examine wording of the will/waqf: identify time‑specific conditions; if explicit, plan to perform at that time (next occurrence if missed).
  • If years were missed: hire separate pilgrims for each missed year; pay ajrat al‑mithl for each proxy.
  • Fund the Hajj from estate funds (or from consenting heirs’ shares if dispute). Document expenditures.
  • If estate temporarily lacks funds but was initially adequate: postpone until sufficient yield accrues. If never adequate: the bequest clause is invalid.
  • Prefer muwaṣṣal Hajj and ihrām entered from the correct miqāt for the deceased; consult local scholar if hardship justifies adjustment.
  • Keep records and seek a qualified mufti for disputed or complex cases (e.g., cross‑madhhab situations, modern travel obstacles).

Hajj of a deceased person — concise principles

  • Core rule: unpaid obligatory Hajj of a deceased who was mustaṭīʿ is due from his estate.
  • Who may perform: any capable Muslim (heir or outsider) may perform Hajj on the deceased’s behalf; using estate funds requires observing estate‑law safeguards.
  • Remedy for doubt about ability in modern circumstances: where ability is doubtful due to contemporary obstacles, perform a connected Hajj funded by consenting heirs to remove doubt.

Food and purity rulings (brief practical summaries)

Honey contaminated with dung/urine mixed with ash

  • Ruling: If impurity is attached and purification is impractical, many scholars permit relying on leniency (excuse) and consuming the honey.
  • Basis: juristic maxim of relief in hardship (الأمر إذا ضاق اتسع).
  • Action: try to purify if feasible; if not, consumption is permitted under hardship leniency.

Milk contaminated during milking (bahr/ba‘r scenarios)

  • Ruling: If impurity is observed to have fallen into the milk during the act of milking, the milk is excused and may be used.
  • If timing is uncertain (you do not know whether contamination occurred before/during/after milking), do NOT rely on the excuse — treat milk as impure.
  • Action: if you saw contamination occur during milking, milk may be used; if uncertain, avoid.

Vessels/pottery made with dung mixed in clay

  • Two positions:
  • Strict (e.g., al‑Muzani): such vessels/water are impure; cooking/fire does not purify embedded impurity.
  • Lenient (adopted in many locales): washing/exterior treatment or customary use and necessity permit considering them pure/excused.
  • Action: prefer glazed/metal utensils; where custom and necessity make use common and vessel treated/washed, many jurists permit use; consult local scholar for sensitive cases.

Juristic principles repeatedly applied

  • Presumption of purity (طهارة الأصل): things are pure unless clear impurity proven.
  • Excuse in hardship (al‑‘afu): habitual or unavoidable contaminations may be excused when conditions for the excuse are satisfied.
  • “When necessity tightens, the law widens” (الأمر إذا ضاق اتسع): invoked to permit leniency under real hardship.

Medical use of alcohol (khamr) — summary rulings

  • Ingestion of pure intoxicating alcohol is prohibited except in true life‑saving necessity (darūra) with no lawful alternative; only the minimal required amount is permitted in that case.
  • Medicines containing alcohol as part of a mixed preparation (maʿjūn) are generally permissible if no halal alternative exists and the alcohol is not the effective/intoxicating component.
  • Topical/external use is more lenient and commonly permitted.
  • Evidence cited: Qur’an on wine’s prohibition/limited benefit (2:219; 5:90 paraphrase) and hadith disallowing reliance on khamr as medicine (reported in Muslim).
  • Action: always seek non‑alcoholic alternatives; document attempts to find alternatives when using alcohol in necessity.

Forgiveness of a dying wrongdoer — rights between people

  • Ruling: A victim’s free, uncoerced forgiveness of a dying wrongdoer is valid and extinguishes the interpersonal right (financial or moral).
  • Document forgiveness if possible (witnesses) to avoid later disputes.
  • Forgiveness does not automatically absolve sin before God — encourage repentance.

Family size and planning — principles and guidance

  • Normative encouragement: marriage and increasing offspring is recommended (hadith: “Marry and multiply…”), but not absolute.
  • Balance maslahah and mafsadah: limiting children is permissible when genuine harms (health, inability to provide, severe hardship) outweigh benefits.
  • Action: assess capacity, plan responsibly (spacing, provision, education), prefer lawful alternatives to overpopulation that leads to neglect; accommodate wife’s legitimate health/education concerns.

Deposits (wadāʿ) — concise ruling

  • Deposited items are trusts (amanah); custodian must preserve and return them; they are not transferable without owner’s explicit consent.
  • Evidence: Qur’an 4:58 (returning trusts).
  • Action: issue receipts, document terms, do not use deposits; obtain written permission if usage is allowed.

Procedural and evidentiary notes

  • Authorities cited in lessons: Ibn Hajar, al‑Nawawi, Abu Zayd, al‑Muzani, Shaykh Ibn Baz; hadith references include reports on wine and on encouraging multiplication of the ummah. For case‑specific rulings and school‑specific application consult a qualified mufti.
  • When textual proof is required (Qur’an/hadith), seek a jurist to apply conditions of istita‘ah and local madhhab rules.
