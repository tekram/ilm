Zakat al-Fitr Transfer Rules - July 01, 2025

📅 2025-07-01 🕐 17:00

Zakat: Transfer between localities (including zakat al‑fitr)

Key ruling

  • Predominant Shafi‘i position: generally impermissible to transfer zakah/zakat al‑fitr out of the originating town.
  • Well‑attested opposing view (Hanafi and many later Shafi‘i authorities): permitted to send zakah to another place, especially to relatives/friends or known deserving recipients.
  • Maliki school: more restrictive — often forbids transfer.
  • Consensus exception: if the imam or his authorized collector distributes, he may send zakah to another locality.

Definitions / controlling criterion

  • “Same town” is determined by local custom (ʿurf), not strictly by walls, administrative borders, or built continuity.
  • The source locality can depend on where the money is held and who distributes it: owner/payer versus imam/collector.

Evidences / authorities

  • Juristic reasoning and classical authorities cited (Abu Hanifa, Ibn ‘Ajlan, Ibn al‑Salah, local jurists). No explicit Qur’an or full hadith text quoted in the lessons for this issue.

Applied example

  • Damun and Tarim: treated as one locality if customary unity exists → transfer permitted.

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • Shafi‘i majority: prohibit transfer.
  • Hanafi and many later Shafi‘i: permit transfer in many cases.
  • Maliki: stricter, generally disallow.
  • Consensus: imam/authorized collector may transfer.

Practical action items

  • Before transferring zakah/zakat al‑fitr, ascertain local ʿurf: are the places regarded as one town? If yes, transfer is permissible.
  • If sending to relatives/trusted recipients elsewhere, rely on permissive opinions but consult a local scholar if uncertain.
  • If an imam/authorized collector handles distribution, sending to other localities is acceptable.
  • Keep records: where funds were held and who distributed them.

Case: Farmer compelled by ruler to hand food (intention and discharge of zakah)

Scenario

  • A farmer gave food to the ruler intending it to be his zakah; the ruler took it as a tax/security levy and placed it with the sultan.

Ruling

  • Generally: the farmer’s intention does not discharge his zakah obligation.
  • Reason: the ruler took it as tax/security, not explicitly as zakah; a unilateral intention by the payer does not transform a tax into zakah.
  • Exception (rare): if the ruler legitimately and effectively collects as zakah (explicitly taken/recorded as zakah, ruler has tawliyah/shawka), the payment may count as zakah.

Key technical terms

  • Tawliyah: legitimate appointment/recognition of a ruler.
  • Shawka: effective authority/control.
  • Taghallub: authority established by conquest/force.
  • Qabd: possession/receipt — relevant where payer must have taken possession before giving (important in some madhhabs).

Practical action items

  • Do not rely on intending a forced tax to be zakah unless the collector explicitly takes and records it as zakah and is a legitimate zakah administrator.
  • If government deducts zakah from salary, verify whether it is explicitly recorded as zakah and reaches eligible recipients; otherwise give zakah personally.
  • Seek a local fatwa when in doubt; retain receipts when giving through official channels.

Paying zakah to rulers/state/officials — general principles

Key rulings

  • Payment to a ruler or state discharges zakah if:
  • The collector explicitly takes it as zakah (niyyah/name), and
  • The collector has legitimate/ effective authority (wilāyah/shawka).
  • If taken under other names (tax, customs, protection fee) or by coercion, it does not discharge zakah.
  • Forced exactions (robbery/unjust seizure) do not absolve the payer unless the funds were taken as zakah by a legitimate collector.
  • If a ruler takes zakah and later spends it wrongly, the payer is still absolved if it was accepted as zakah.

Differences and contested cases

  • Validity disputed when rulers lack real authority, are under non‑Muslim protectorates, or are non‑Muslim agents — rulings depend on classification of authority and local circumstance.
  • Juristic difference on whether non‑Muslim/female rulers placed by force have binding administrative/judicial authority.

Practical action items

  • Prefer giving zakah to recognized local collectors who explicitly take it as zakah.
  • Do not treat ordinary state taxes as zakah; in non‑Muslim countries do not intend taxes as zakah.
  • When paying through state channels, obtain clear confirmation that collection is as zakah and that funds reach rightful recipients.

Zakat al‑Fitr (sadaqat al‑fitr): measure and quality when paid in dates

Scriptural basis

  • Classical hadith requirement: one sa‘ of a staple food per person (hadith reported in Bukhari & Muslim).

Key rulings on measure

  • Classical measure: one sa‘ (volume). Local conversions vary.
  • Hadrami teaching: often cited as six Hadrami ratl/abṭāl; in some local calibrations ~7–7.5 ratl recommended as a precaution.
  • Always follow local scholarly conversion of the sa‘ to current weights/measures.

Processed dates (pitted, mashed, peeled — marzum / sīn)

  • Majority view: removing pits (pitted dates) or heavy processing that reduces the staple character is a defect → such forms do not count for fitrah.
  • Minority/conditional view (Abu Zurʿa and others): permitted if that processed form is commonly the staple in the locality and its taste/colour/odor remain essentially unchanged.
  • Mashed/peeled dates that spoil quickly are generally discouraged for fitrah by stricter texts (e.g., al‑Tuhfa).

Practical action items

  • Prefer to give fitrah in the staple form commonly consumed by recipients.
  • If using processed dates, ensure they remain the ordinary staple (not spoiled or materially altered); otherwise increase the quantity or give whole staple dates.
  • When unsure about the local sa‘ conversion, give the larger amount (precaution e.g., 7–7.5 ratl in some Hadrami contexts) or consult local mufti.

Purification (taharah) of foods, utensils, vessels, and leather contaminated by najasah

General principle

  • Purification is judged by what appears: washing/removing the visible impurity suffices where inner penetration is not established.
  • Scholars differ in cases of porous vessels, wine‑saturated containers, and leather; majority positions summarized below.

Food soaked in impurity (e.g., olives in urine)

  • Preferred ruling: wash the exterior (commonly two washes) — this suffices to purify similar interior substance.
  • Caveat: if impurity penetrated by heat or other means, more intensive treatment may be required.

Knives, swords, and metal tools

  • Washing the apparent surface (two washes) generally suffices.
  • If washing would damage a blade’s cutting edge (ṣaqālah), wiping off visible impurity is permitted (Imam Malik’s practice).
  • Cutting dry foods with a contaminated blade: majority say dry items do not become impure by cutting.

Wine and vessels, porous jars (rashḥah)

  • Vinegar: many scholars accept vinegar as pure; others are stricter.
  • Ordinary porous vessels: pouring water into/over them is generally sufficient for purification.
  • Minority (Imam Ahmad): in saturated cases, breaking the vessel was prescribed.
  • External seepage need not be waited for; thorough rinsing or pouring suffices in the preferred view.

Leather and tanning (dabbgh)

  • Tanning normally purifies hides that became impure by death (not properly slaughtered); such tanned leather is considered pure.
  • Exception: dog and pig skins remain impure even after tanning — they are not purified by the tanning process.

Practical action items for purification

  • Wash visible impurity; two washings are commonly cited for food/tools.
  • If washing would damage a valuable blade, wipe until the impurity is removed.
  • For porous vessels: pour water thoroughly; follow stricter opinion (breaking) only if you choose extra caution.
  • For leather goods from non‑Muslim countries or uncertain processes: if doubt exists about tanning/washing, clean the surface when feasible; avoid dog/pig leather.

Summary of principal differences of opinion (condensed)

  • Transfer of zakah between towns: Shafi‘i majority forbids; Hanafi and many later Shafi‘i permit; Maliki stricter; imam/collector exception agreed.
  • State taxes vs. zakah: disagreement over whether paying taxes under compulsion can discharge zakah without explicit zakah collection and legitimate authority.
  • Fitrah in processed dates: majority view rejects pitted/mashed dates as sufficient; minority allows if they remain the staple form.
  • Porous vessels and wine: majority permit purification by rinsing; Imam Ahmad’s stricter view may require breaking saturated porous vessels.
  • Leather: tanning purifies most hides; dog and pig skins remain impure — majority position.

Practical consolidated checklist

  • Before transferring zakah/zakat al‑fitr between localities: check local ʿurf; if unsure, consult local scholars.
  • When paying zakah via state or officials:
  • Ensure collector takes it explicitly as zakah and has legitimate authority; get documentation/receipt.
  • Do not treat ordinary taxes or coerced exactions as zakah unless conditions above are met.
  • For zakat al‑fitr:
  • Use the staple food; for dates follow local conversion of the sa‘; when unsure, give the larger measure.
  • Avoid heavily processed/pitted dates unless local scholars permit; otherwise increase amount.
  • Purification of items:
  • Remove visible impurity (wash/wipe); two washes commonly sufficient.
  • For porous wine‑saturated vessels, pour water thoroughly; follow stricter measures only if you choose extra caution.
  • Tanned leather from non‑dog/pig animals is generally pure; avoid dog/pig leather or treat as impure.
  • If in doubt on rulings tied to local practice or political legitimacy (rulers, collectors, measures), consult a qualified local mufti and retain receipts when using official channels.

Sources and authorities referenced (as taught)

  • Madhhabs: Shafi‘i, Hanafi, Maliki; jurists: Ibn Hajar, Ibn ‘Ajlan, Ibn al‑Salah, Abu Zurʿa, Imam Ahmad, Imam Malik, al‑Nawawi; texts: al‑Tuhfa and other classical fiqh works. Hadith basis for fitrah: sa‘ of staple food (reported in Bukhari & Muslim).
