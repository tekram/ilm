Prayer During Menstruation - January 07, 2025

Start: 2025-01-07T17:00:39

Topic — Overview

Rules on menstrual/irregular bleeding and the five daily prayers (whether to perform qada), plus related fiqh issues: excused bloods (insects, cupping, wounds), impurities from animals/insects, bleeding during prayer, jumuʿah/dhuhr overlap, and prayer times for astronauts.

Definitions / Context

  • Haidh (menstruation): normal menstrual bleeding that exempts a woman from prayer and fasting while it continues.
  • Irregular/intermittent bleeding: bleeding that may stop or start within a prayer time, creating uncertainty whether the woman was pure at the prayer moment.
  • Excused bloods (maʿfū ʿanhā): types of blood or excretions jurists treat as not nullifying wudu or as not requiring full purification treatment.

A. Women with irregular/intermittent bleeding — Prayers and qada

Key rulings

  • Majority Shafiʿi position (and many transmitters): when status is uncertain one must assume obligation to pray — i.e., treat each prayer time as possibly a time of purity and perform the five prayers (precaution). If clearly menstruating at the time → do not pray; if clearly pure → pray.
  • On qada (making up missed prayers after menstruation):
  • Majority classical Shafiʿis: no qada is required for prayers missed because of menstruation.
  • Opposing view (Abu Zayd al-Marwazi and followers): qada is required when bleeding may have ceased during the prayer time (so the woman could legitimately have prayed); this leads to repeating some prayers.
  • Practical extremes: Abu Zayd’s logic can require praying some prayers twice (early-time with wudu; again at end-of-time with ghusl if purity is later established), producing repeated prayers and extra wudu/ghusl acts.

Evidence & authorities

  • No explicit Qurʾan/Prophetic texts quoted in the lesson; discussion rests on juristic reasoning and precedent.
  • Authorities for “no qada”: Imam al-Shafiʿi, Abu Hamid, Qadi Abu Tayyib, Ibn al‑Sabbagh, majority Iraqi Shafiʿis, al‑Ghazali (al‑Wajiz), others.
  • Authorities for “qada required / repeat”: Abu Zayd al‑Marwazi, followers recorded in al‑Rafiʿi, Qadi Husayn, al‑Baghawi; Imam al‑Haramayn discussed arguments.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • Core split: no qada (majority Shafiʿi) vs. qada/repeat where bleeding may have ceased (Abu Zayd).
  • Practical consequence: majority → simpler (no make-up); Abu Zayd → potentially many repetitions and added ritual washing.
  • Moderating compromise (teacher’s recommendation): adopt a limited twofold approach (pray early with wudu, repeat at end with ghusl only where realistically necessary) to reduce hardship.

Practical action items

  • First determine status: clearly menstruating → do not pray; clearly pure → pray normally.
  • If intermittent/uncertain:
  • Option A (majority/lenient): accept exemption — do not make up missed prayers after the period.
  • Option B (precaution/Abu Zayd): pray at start of time with wudu; if bleeding later stops, perform ghusl and repeat the prayer at the end of time.
  • Option C (moderate/teacher’s compromise): adopt the twofold method only where realistically needed; avoid multiplying repetitions.
  • Consult a qualified local scholar for individual cases (medical history, timing patterns, madhhab application).

B. Note on delayed qadaʾ prayers (brief)

  • Reported view (Imam al‑Haramayn as quoted): if extra prayers were not performed at earliest times, within a month the person may only be required to qada the prayers of two days. This is an advanced juristic point and requires detailed context; follow madhhab/local authority.

C. Bloods that are "excused" (maʿfū ʿanhā) — insects, pustules, cupping, wounds

Types discussed

  • Insect blood (bedbugs, mosquitoes, lice, fleas), small pustule discharge, blood from cupping/phlebotomy/fasd, wound discharge, and incidental blood on clothing or bedding.

General ruling (majority)

  • These are generally excused — they do not nullify wudu or require the strict purification rules — when incidental and not self‑caused. Leniency rests on difficulty of avoidance and the juristic principle of attaching rare cases to the common (ألحِق نادرها بالغالب).

Exceptions / qualifications

  • If the blood resulted from the person’s deliberate action (e.g., deliberately crushing insects and smearing blood) → stricter: only a very small stain may be excused; large deliberate stains require cleaning.
  • If blood has “moved from its place” (becomes foreign on clothes) → treated more strictly; only small amounts excused by many jurists.
  • If blood spreads by water of wudu/ghusl or by sweat and runs like water → generally excused.

Minority views

  • Some transmitters (e.g., Ibn al‑Sabbagh, Awn ibn Nasr, Abu al‑Futuh al‑ʿAjali) held stricter positions: insect blood spreading on garments should not be excused.

Practical action items

  • Incidental insect blood, pustule discharge, or cupping blood on body/clothes: usually treat as excused; no need to invalidate prayer.
  • If you intentionally caused the stain or it transferred widely → remove and wash the affected spot per your madhhab.
  • When in doubt, follow local madhhab practice and consult a scholar.

D. Animals/insects and their products (impurity and jallālah)

General rulings

  • Small flying insects and their excrement/urine (flies, mosquitoes, wasps, ants, moths, geckos etc.): generally excused — their contact or minor excretions usually do not render person or vessel ritually impure.
  • Bees and honey: honey and beeswax are pure even if bees ate impure matter.
  • Cats: if a cat ate impure food, its excretions or milk do not acquire the heavier impurity; cleaning the soiled spot with one wash suffices (no seven-wash rule).
  • Rodents (mice, similar): their excrement/urine are treated as ordinary impurity and require stricter cleansing.

Jallālah (animal fed on impurity) — products (meat, milk, eggs, hides)

  • If an otherwise lawful animal ate impurity but its product shows no perceptible change in taste/odor/color → many schools permit using the product (some call it makrūh but valid).
  • If the product shows a change → it becomes disliked or disallowed until the change is removed. Remedy: feed the live animal wholesome feed (taʾlīf) until the change disappears before slaughter/use.
  • Newborns nursed by impure animals: differences exist — some allow but regard it disliked; others forbid.

Practical action items

  • Inspect animal products for perceptible change. If none → permitted (some advise avoidance if cautious).
  • If change exists → stop using; apply taʾlīf to the live animal and wait until the change disappears pre‑slaughter.
  • Honey/beeswax: permitted.
  • Cat contamination: wash the soiled spot once.
  • Mouse contamination: treat as normal impurity; remove and wash.

E. Bleeding during prayer & insects provoking movement

Evidence

  • Hadith: Report of Jābir — two Companions wounded by arrows during prayer removed arrows and completed the prayer while blood flowed; Prophet did not object (Sunan Abū Dāwūd; graded hasan by commentators such as al‑Nawawī).

Rulings

  • Bleeding that falls to the ground or only slightly soils the person: prayer may be completed (hadith evidence).
  • Blood caused by one’s own act (cupping/bloodletting) that bursts and spreads widely: many scholars do not excuse it — purification required.
  • Blood mixing with a foreign substance (ajnabi) or heavily soiling clothes: many scholars require purification; thresholds differ.
  • Frequent involuntary movements (e.g., to repel many mosquitoes or due to severe itching/scabies): permitted; such necessary movements do not necessarily invalidate prayer.

Practical action items

  • If wounded and bleeding in prayer: continue the prayer if possible; if blood soaks clothes significantly and you can change, do so.
  • For cupping/bloodletting bleeding that spreads widely: follow your madhhab; many require stopping to purify.
  • For nosebleeds during Jumuʿah: some jurists allow completing the prayer without repeating it if one fears missing the khutbah; otherwise follow madhhab practice.
  • For insect disturbance: repel or scratch as needed; try to minimize disruption.

F. Jumuʿah vs. Dhuhr overlap

  • If dhuhr time begins during the jumuʿah sermon:
  • Some jurists: leave jumuʿah and pray dhuhr if staying risks missing the time.
  • Others: remain in jumuʿah; if jumuʿah was missed, pray dhuhr and you are excused.
  • Practical action item: if leaving risks losing the prayer time, prioritize performing dhuhr; consult local practice/madhhab for routine cases.

G. Prayer times for astronauts (practical contemporary issue)

  • Principles:
  • Prayer times are tied to visible solar signs where the person is located.
  • If an astronaut experiences distinct local sunrise/sunset aboard (frequent cycles), the straightforward approach is to use those local apparent day/night markers.
  • If no local day/night (continuous light/dark), use the nearest reasonable land/time‑zone on Earth.
  • Critique of fixed Mecca time: adopting Mecca time for all orbiting cases lacks general juristic basis; nearest land or local solar signs are more defensible.
  • Practical action items:
  • Follow local apparent day/night onboard if clear.
  • If continuous day/night, adopt times of nearest land/time zone or seek fatwa from competent space‑era jurists.
  • Due to hardship and novelty, rely on authoritative councils or appointed religious advisors for definitive rulings.

Authorities cited (selection)

  • Classical jurists: Imam al‑Shafiʿi, Abu Zayd al‑Marwazi, Imam al‑Haramayn, al‑Ghazali, Ibn al‑Sabbāgh, al‑Baghawi, Ibn Hajar, and later commentators (Ramli, al‑Sharbini).
  • Hadith reference: Jābir’s wounded‑in‑prayer narration — Sunan Abū Dāwūd (supporting permissibility to complete prayer while bleeding).

Practical consolidated checklist (for daily practice)

  • Determine status: clear haidh → do not pray; clear purity → pray normally.
  • If bleeding is intermittent/uncertain:
  • Prefer majority madhhab position (no qada) for ease, or
  • Use the twofold compromise: pray at start of time with wudu; if purity later is certain at end of time, perform ghusl and repeat only when realistically necessary.
  • For incidental insect blood/pustules/cupping:
  • Treat as excused unless self‑caused or widely transferred; clean removable traces.
  • For cat contamination: wash the spot once.
  • For mouse/rodent contamination: treat as regular impurity and wash accordingly.
  • If bleeding occurs during prayer: stop it if feasible; otherwise continue the prayer (hadith evidence) unless bleeding is self‑caused and widespread per your madhhab.
  • If disturbed by insects during prayer: repel/minimize movement; necessary movements do not necessarily invalidate the prayer.
  • For novel circumstances (astronauts, complicated bleeding histories): consult qualified contemporary scholars and follow local/madhhab guidance.

Final note

  • The lesson emphasized mercy and avoiding excessive hardship: favor practical mitigations (the teacher preferred the twofold easing) rather than imposing all theoretical repetitions. For individual cases—especially medical, repetitive or novel situations—seek rulings from a knowledgeable local scholar or an authorized juristic body.
