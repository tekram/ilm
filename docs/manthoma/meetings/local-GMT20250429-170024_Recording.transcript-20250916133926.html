Uncertain Menstruation Rules - April 29, 2025

Woman who is mutaḥayyirah (uncertain about menstruation/purity)

Definitions and governing principles

  • Mutaḥayyirah: a woman who is unsure whether she is currently menstruating (hayd) or pure (taharah).
  • Core rules:
  • If a time is certainly menstruation → she must observe prohibitions of hayd (no prayer, no fasting, no intercourse); perform ghusl at its end.
  • If a time is certainly purity → she may perform all acts of worship and intercourse.
  • If a time is doubtful → intercourse is forbidden; for prayer adopt precautions of a pure woman (routine wudu), and perform ghusl at any time when there is a possible cessation (ihtimal inqitāʿ) if she wishes to pray.

Key specific rulings (from Imam al‑Nawāwī and the teacher)

  • Prayer behind a mutaḥayyirah:
  • Prayer led by a mutaḥayyirah is invalid for followers (majority/authoritative view stated): one cannot rely on an imam whose purity is doubtful.
  • If both imam and follower are mutaḥayyir, the follower’s prayer is invalid.
  • Two mutaḥayyirāt praying together:
  • Preferred position given: such prayer is not valid when the leader’s purity is doubtful.
  • Sexual intercourse during mutaḥayyirah (e.g., in Ramadan):
  • Instructor favors: no kaffāra (expiation) becomes incumbent on the woman by reason of uncertainty; presumption of innocence applies. Two earlier views exist; instructor prefers leniency.
  • Breaking fast for nursing:
  • If a mutaḥayyirah breaks a fast to nurse, fidya (ransom) is required for the broken fast; the special leniency for intercourse in doubt is not applied here.
  • Qada’ (making up missed fasts) and intention doubts:
  • If doubt about whether a completed qada’ day was intended arises after the fact, the instructor prefers leaving the completed day as valid (no repeat required); an alternative view would annul it.
  • Combining prayers in travel:
  • Combining prayers based on a doubtful prior state is not valid unless the earlier prayer was certainly performed.
  • Marital maintenance:
  • Husband must continue maintenance (nafaqa) of a mutaḥayyirah wife; uncertainty does not annul the marriage or give unilateral option to dissolve it (ref. al‑Ghazālī).
  • Known previous patterns:
  • If she remembers definite menses days or definite pure days, treat those days accordingly; areas of doubt are handled by the general precautionary rules above.

Method for periodic-length reports (practical decision method)

  • For women who know the length of bleeding (e.g., 3, 5, 10 days) but not when it falls monthly:
  • Partition the month into ranges where those bleeding-days could fall; mark days that are certainly menses, certainly pure, and doubtful.
  • Action:
  • Certainly menses → refrain from prayer/fast/intercourse; do ghusl after its end.
  • Certainly pure → perform wudu and all acts.
  • Doubtful → forbid intercourse; perform wudu for each prayer; perform ghusl on any day where cessation might have occurred before praying thereafter.
  • Examples (illustrative only):
  • 10-day cycle unknown start: no day is certainly menses/pure; ordinarily pray and fast as if pure, but perform ghusl at any possible cessation point to resume prayers when bleeding could have stopped.
  • 3-day cycle: partition the month into possible 3‑day spans; pray with wudu in days that may be pure; perform ghusl only when a possible cessation time arrives.

Evidences and authorities

  • Primary sources cited: Imam al‑Nawāwī’s chapter and teacher’s commentary; references to al‑Ghazālī’s summary.
  • No specific Qur’anic verse or Prophetic hadith was quoted in this transcript portion; rulings rely on classical fiqh method and juristic precedent.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • Validity of praying behind a mutaḥayyirah — majority: invalid.
  • Kaffāra for intercourse with a mutaḥayyirah during Ramadan — two views; instructor favors no kaffāra.
  • Effect of post‑completion doubt about qada’ intention — two views; instructor favors maintaining the completed day.
  • Other fine points of counting and fixing patterns across months show variation among jurists.

Practical action items

  • Establish: (1) how many days her menses usually last, (2) any definitely known days of menses or purity this month.
  • Partition the month and mark days as certainly menses / certainly pure / doubtful.
  • For doubtful days: forbid intercourse; pray with wudu and perform ghusl at any moment when cessation is reasonably possible before praying.
  • If you prayed behind a woman whose purity was doubtful: consider that prayer invalid and advise repeating it.
  • If intercourse took place during Ramadan with uncertainty: follow the instructor’s favored view (no kaffāra), but seek clarity for future cycles.
  • Husbands must continue maintenance; encourage women to track bleeding to reduce future doubt.

Menstruation (hayd) — determining purity and practical conduct (summary)

  • Treat each prayer-time according to certainty:
  • Tahara yaqīnīya (certain purity): pray normally.
  • Hayd yaqīnī (certain menstruation): abstain and perform ghusl after cessation.
  • Doubtful (mashkūk): act with precaution (wudu each time; ghusl when possible cessation occurs).
  • Conservative approach: when in doubt about a specific day, prefer measures that protect the validity of worship (wudu, delay intercourse, ghusl when cessation is possible).

Footwear (khuff/na‘l) and street dirt — wiping vs washing

Definitions

  • Khuff/na‘l: any footwear (shoe, sandal, slipper).
  • Najāsa: juridical impurity (e.g., feces, urine).
  • Qadhar/adhā (dirt/harm): ordinary street dirt or non‑juridical residues.

Key rulings

  • Superficial non‑impure dirt (dust, mud, benign secretions that wipe off) — wiping (masḥ) on earth is permitted; praying in shoes is valid.
  • Clear, attached najāsa (feces, wet urine, adhered filth) — washing the sole/shoe is required; wiping is insufficient.
  • If impurity has soaked into or beneath the khuff, or into the interior, washing is required.
  • Deliberate stepping into filth generally removes the leniency; accidental contamination may allow wiping if conditions below are met.

Evidences

  • Hadith of Abu Sa‘id al‑Khudrī (Sunan Abū Dāwūd): permitting wiping shoes when dirt/harm is seen — used as support for leniency.
  • A hadith attributed to Abū Hurayrah (“the earth is purifying”) discussed but had weak chains; not relied upon heavily.
  • Scholarly commentaries (al‑Nawāwī, Ibn al‑Rif‘ah, al‑Rafi‘i) distinguish surface dirt from true najāsa.

Conditions that limit leniency (three practical conditions)

1. The impurity must be removable by rubbing (not a clinging mass).

2. The impurity should be dry when wiping; wet impurities risk spreading.

3. Contamination must be unintentional (not deliberate).

Practical action items

  • Inspect the sole: dust/wipe for ordinary dirt; wash for clear najāsa.
  • If shoe fell into impure liquid or is soaked — wash it.
  • Avoid intentionally walking through filth; if you did, expect to wash.
  • When in doubt, prefer washing if feasible; wipe only under conditions of minor/dry accidental contamination.

Walking in mosques with soiled shoes & bat excreta

Mosque entry with soiled shoes

  • It is impermissible to enter or walk inside a mosque with shoes that carry known najāsa; adding impurity deliberately is sinful.
  • Exception: necessity (ḍarūra), e.g., extreme heat that would harm the feet — prohibition may be lifted by necessity, not by mere dislike.
  • If mosque is already dirty, do not worsen it; clean when possible.

Bat excreta (urine/droppings)

  • Many jurists offer leniency (iʿfāʼ, “excuse”) for bat droppings/urine in places where bats commonly mix with people — due to prevalence and hardship of absolute avoidance.
  • Conditions commonly attached to excusing:
  • Unintentional occurrence.
  • Not deliberately placed by a person.
  • It did not spread through deliberate action beyond where it fell.
  • Differences: some texts excuse wet and dry excreta equally; others restrict to certain cases (e.g., during flight or dry deposits). Schools diverge in details.

Practical action items

  • Do not enter or pray in a mosque with visibly impure shoes; clean or remove them.
  • Clean isolated bat droppings if feasible. If excreta are widespread and unavoidable, many juristic opinions permit treating them as excused under the three conditions above — but follow local scholarly guidance.
  • Avoid intentionally spreading or leaving impurities in places of worship.

Small animal impurities (fleas, flies, mice, rodents) — clothes, food, and water

General principles

  • Many small animal residues (insect blood, small droppings) may be treated as excused in Hanafi discussions, subject to conditions (type of animal, dry vs wet state, whether mixed with moisture).
  • Dogs, pigs, and human excreta are generally not excused — require purification.
  • Work clothes (clothes worn in one’s trade) sometimes have a classical leniency for minor contamination; stored clothes are treated more strictly.

Specific rulings

  • Mouse droppings:
  • On work clothes: some leniency (excused) in certain Hanafi statements.
  • In bread/food: if droppings are removable and have not mixed with moisture, many jurists permit removal and using the food; if moisture mixed, treat as impure.
  • Water:
  • If impurity falls into a large body of water, exemption or dilution principles may apply; for small quantities the outlet/portion may be affected — Shafi‘i rule restricts exculpation to the outlet portion.
  • Bats and flying animals: discussed above; often excused by prevalence.
  • Dog and pig impurities: always require removal/washing; no excuse.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • Hanafi: relatively broader excusal for small non‑dog/pig animal impurities, with conditional allowances for work clothes and for liquids after removing solids.
  • Maliki (per some later authorities): sometimes more expansive leniency for small contamination in liquids/wealth if the thing is not materially altered.
  • Shafi‘i: narrower — often only the affected outlet portion of water is excused; human/dog/pig impurities not excused.

Practical action items

  • Remove visible solids first; if no moisture contact, use/consume after removal per many jurists.
  • For contaminated clothes: wash stored/clean garments; work clothes may be treated more leniently per classical positions.
  • For contaminated water vessels or small quantities: prefer to clean/wash when feasible; follow the madhhab’s specific rule if you follow one.
  • In mosques/communal areas: clean and avoid relying on leniency where worship is affected.

Key differences of opinion — quick summary

  • Prayer validity behind a mutaḥayyirah — majority: invalid.
  • Kaffāra for intercourse with a mutaḥayyirah during Ramadan — disputed; instructor favors no kaffāra.
  • Wiping shoes vs washing for true najāsa — majority stricter (wash); a lenient minority relies on Abu Saʿid’s report permitting wiping in minor/dry accidental dirt.
  • Excusing bat/mouse/insect impurities — scope and conditions differ by school and commentator; dog/pig/human impurities are generally not excused.

Consolidated practical checklist (for students and affected women)

  • For mutaḥayyirah cases:
  • Record average bleeding length and any definite pure/menstrual days.
  • Partition the month and apply certainly menses / certainly pure / doubtful rules.
  • Prohibit intercourse on doubtful days; pray with wudu; perform ghusl when cessation is possible.
  • For footwear:
  • Wipe off ordinary dust; wash if clear/attached najāsa or wet contamination or contamination under the sole.
  • Do not enter mosque with known impurity on shoes.
  • For animal/rodent contamination:
  • Remove solid impurity; if moisture mixed, wash/avoid use.
  • Treat dog/pig/human impurity as non‑excused; clean without relying on leniency.
  • When in doubt, follow the stricter practical step if it is easy; if hardship exists and classical leniency conditions are met, the lenient positions may be relied upon; consult a local scholar when uncertain.
  • Encourage women to track cycles to minimize recurring doubt.

Etiquette note (closing)

  • Greeting/parting prayer phrases: respond to salutations; Qur’an 4:86 commands returning a greeting with equal or better phrase. Respond with “Wa ʿalaykum as‑salām” and a brief dua.
