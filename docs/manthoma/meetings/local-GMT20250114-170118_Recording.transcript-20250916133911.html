Fasting During Menstrual Doubt - January 14, 2025

Start: 2025-01-14T17:01:18

Definition: mutahayyira

  • Mutahayyira: a woman whose menstrual cycle is irregular/uncertain so she cannot reliably distinguish menstrual (hayd) days from pure (ṭaḥr) days.

Fasting rules for a mutahayyira — Shafi‘i classical summary

Key rulings

  • Majority early Shafi‘is treat the mutahayyira stringently: she may not rely on uncertain days when counting valid Ramadan fasts/prayers.
  • Numerical positions for how many days to impute as menstrual when she fasts with the people in Ramadan:
  • Majority classical reading: impute 15 days (based on the juristic premise that 15 is the usual maximum menstruation).
  • Abu Zayd al‑Marwazi and some Khurasanis: impute 14 days (accounting for partial‑day beginnings/ends).
  • For a short (29‑day) Ramadan many later Shafi‘is hold only 13 days can be imputed (possibility of bleeding starting/stopping within half‑days).
  • If she did not fast with the people but fasted two full other months, the classical compilers (e.g., al‑Musannif as read in the lesson) are best understood to say she still owes one missed day.
  • Technical rule often cited: “two intervening fasts suffice” in certain counting contexts (used in assessing continuity/gaps).

Juristic basis and evidences

  • Based on Shafi‘i textual readings and juristic maxims (not explicit Qur’an/hadith in the recorded segment). Authorities in the chain: Imam al‑Shafi‘i (textual attributions), Imam al‑Haramayn, al‑Juwayni, al‑Ghazali, al‑Muzani, Abu Zayd, Darimi, al‑Baghawi, al‑Rafi‘i, etc.
  • Disagreements arise from how to read the texts and whether to account for partial days/strong suspicions (dhann) as decisive.

Differences of opinion (concise)

  • 15 days imputed (majority early Shafi‘is).
  • 14 days (Abu Zayd and some Khurasanis).
  • 13 days for 29‑day Ramadan (later authorities who factor half‑day possibilities).
  • Dispute over whether fasting two months elsewhere removes all owed days or leaves one day outstanding — the lesson favors the reading that one day remains owing in many scenarios.

Practical action items (mutahayyira)

  • Follow your madhhab or a reliable local Shafi‘i scholar; tell them whether Ramadan was 29 or 30 days.
  • If following the majority: treat up to 15 days as menstrual in a 30‑day Ramadan.
  • If Ramadan was 29 days: many authorities allow only 13 imputed days — consult local scholar.
  • If unable to consult and you need at least one unquestionably valid fast (for qada, vow, kaffāra, fidya, or voluntary fasts), use the precautionary fasting patterns taught classically:
  • Fast a short, strategically spaced set so at least one chosen day must be pure — e.g., 3 well‑placed days (examples in fiqh texts show spacing such that at least one falls on ṭaḥr) or 4 days arranged (two early and two late) to guarantee at least one pure day.
  • Keep records of days fasted and consult a jurist to determine remaining qada.

Counting fasts around bleeding (general principle)

  • Core point: counting depends on precise factual pattern (bleeding start/stop, gaps); “two days between them suffice” is cited in the Shafi‘i transmissions for certain cases.
  • Practical takeaway: the matter is technical — follow madhhab guidance or a qualified scholar in contested numeric scenarios (7, 10, 14, 15 etc. arise in different conditional rules).

Watery discharge from mouth after sleep (saliva vs gastric fluid)

Legal question

  • Is the thin watery fluid found at the mouth after sleep najis (impure) or tahir (pure), and how to determine its origin (saliva vs stomach reflux)?

Positions and authorities

  • Dominant practical position (Shafi‘i transmissions): ordinary saliva/phlegm is pure; fluid that is yellow, foul, or unmistakably gastric is impure.
  • Ibn Hajar: insist on certainty (yaqīn) of stomach origin before declaring impurity; presumption of purity remains where doubt persists.
  • Al‑Khawarizmi, al‑Juwayni, al‑Muzani and others: accept color/odor, continuity, and sleeping position as signs; some accept strong suspicion (dhann) as sufficient in practice.
  • Medical testimony cited in the lesson: physicians deny stomach contents normally reach the mouth without vomiting — supports presumption of saliva/purity.

Signs to infer origin (practical markers)

  • Color/odor: yellow or fetid smell → points to gastric origin → many jurists deem impure.
  • Continuity: discharge that flows continuously during long sleep → suggests stomach origin.
  • Head position: head low/flat → reflux more likely; head elevated → saliva more likely.
  • Drying on lip, appearance and persistence also informative.

Legal consequences and concessions

  • If judged impure but continuous/habitual (affliction): scholars grant concession (ma‘fū) — the afflicted person is excused from repeated ritual purifications for practical hardship, though this may not absolve responsibility if others are soiled.
  • Shared food/drink: many jurists permit leaving small food/drink touched after mouth contact in hardship contexts; transfer or discard if contamination likely.

Practical action items (mouth discharge)

  • Inspect fluid: clear/ordinary saliva → treat as pure (no washing needed).
  • Yellow/fetid/continuous → treat as possibly impure; wash affected area/clothes when feasible.
  • If habitual and continuous, consult scholar about the concession for repeated washing.
  • When in doubt and no clear sign of impurity → apply presumption of purity.

Residual blood on slaughtered meat (ma‘fū ʿanhu — excused)

Definition and ruling

  • Residual blood: blood remaining in flesh, vessels, or bones after lawful slaughter (not the freely flowing/spilled blood, al‑dam al‑masfūḥ).
  • Principal position in lesson: residual blood is technically impure but legally excused — customary/simple washing suffices and cooking without full removal is generally permitted.

Authorities and evidence

  • Ibn Hajar, Imam al‑Nawawī, al‑Subkī: support the excused‑impurity position; report of ʿĀ’ishah cooking and eating from a pot with yellowish discoloration used as practical precedent.
  • Abu Isḥaq al‑Shīrāzī (al‑Tadhkira): stricter — requires purification/washing before cooking (minority/stricter strand).

Limits and conditions

  • Flowing/spilled blood (masfūḥ) or blood made to flow by pouring water must be washed off — excuse does not apply.
  • When removal is difficult (neck area, tight places), the usual washing is accepted and what remains is excused.
  • If in doubt or preparing for others, follow the stricter view (wash) to avoid dispute.

Practical action items (meat)

  • Usual washing then cook; remaining traces excused in most Shafi‘i rulings.
  • If blood flows after pouring water → wash thoroughly.
  • Prefer washing when serving others or where controversy may arise.

Offal, paunch and viscera (karsh, am‘a) — cleaning rules

Principal rulings

  • Paunch/intestines: normally must be emptied and washed; washing is the preferred method to remove impurities.
  • In hardship (no water or remote conditions), some regional authorities allow beating/scraping/striking to remove contents as a concession; Ibn Hajar and others insist beating alone is insufficient and require washing when possible.
  • Small stubborn filth that cannot be removed is excused if only smell/color remains and removal is difficult — customary practice supports permissibility.

Practical action items

  • Prefer to wash paunch and viscera thoroughly after emptying.
  • In actual hardship, local customary concessions (scraping/striking) may be relied upon; consult local scholar.
  • If offal looks visibly clean under local custom, no need to demand proof of washing.

Liver and similar organs

  • Liver, spleen and similar organs are treated as pure and lawful to eat; juices appearing during/after cooking are regarded as pure. If red blood appears before cooking, treat as impurity and wash.

Fish blood

  • Some jurists (e.g., al‑Rīmī as cited) considered fish blood pure (noting it dries and whitens in sun); exceptions noted for whales and similar aquatic beasts.
  • This is a minority/particular opinion — follow local scholar/madhhab.

Sword/weapon stained with blood during combat — prayer rulings

Core rulings

  • If a combatant must keep a blood‑stained weapon out of necessity (fear of death/loss), he may carry it while praying; the prevailing view requires making up the missed/affected prayer(s) (qada) afterwards.
  • If it is safe to sheathe or secure the weapon without risk, he should do so before praying.
  • If discarding the weapon would cause loss/danger, securing it (sheathing/placing in container) is allowed rather than abandoning it.

Differences and reasoning

  • Some argued that the weapon must be put away or prayer is invalid; majority responses emphasize practical risk and the juristic excuse for necessity.
  • Distinction with turban/headgear: turban can usually be removed without loss; weapon removal may risk loss — this practical difference affects permissibility and timing.

Practical action items (combatant)

  • If necessary for safety to hold the weapon: you may pray with it; perform qada later.
  • If you can safely secure/sheath it, do so before praying.
  • Avoid abandoning a weapon if doing so risks loss or danger.

Choosing mosque prayer vs praying at home with family

General rule

  • Prayer in the mosque is generally preferable and takes precedence over praying at home with family.

Important exceptions and qualifications

  • Praying at home with one’s wife may be better if:
  • The mosque imam is known to be deviant/innovative, or
  • Leaving would deprive the wife/family of having a congregational prayer leader at home.
  • If feasible: best practice is to attend the mosque and then return to pray with family.
  • If one choice must be made and leaving will cause significant hardship or prevent household congregation, prioritize praying at home.
  • Minor disturbance (waking family) does not automatically forbid leaving; substantial hardship does.

Practical action items

  • Default: attend mosque when reasonably possible.
  • If leaving causes real harm or prevents family congregation, pray at home.
  • When in doubt, try to combine both (attend mosque then pray again at home) if feasible.

Practical quick checklists

  • Mutahayyira fasting:
  • Tell your local Shafi‘i scholar whether Ramadan was 29/30 days.
  • If no access to a scholar, use conservative fasting patterns (3–4 strategically spaced days) to secure at least one unquestionably pure fast for qada/vows.
  • Mouth discharge after sleep:
  • Clear/ordinary saliva → treat as pure.
  • Yellow/foul/continuous or with head‑down sleep → treat as possibly impure; wash affected area.
  • Habitual continuous discharge → consult for concession regarding repeated washing.
  • Residual blood in meat:
  • Usual washing then cook → permitted; remaining traces excused.
  • Flowing blood or blood made to flow by added water → wash until clean.
  • Serving others or avoiding dispute → wash thoroughly.
  • Paunch/viscera:
  • Prefer washing; in hardship, accepted concessions may apply — consult local scholar.
  • Sword/weapon in combat:
  • If necessary to keep it: allowed in prayer; perform qada later.
  • If safe to stow/sheath: do so before praying.
  • Mosque vs home prayer:
  • Mosque preferred except where leaving causes real harm or prevents household congregation; when possible, do both.
