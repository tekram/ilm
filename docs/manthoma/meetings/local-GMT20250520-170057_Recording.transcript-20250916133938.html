Postpartum Bleeding Rules - May 20, 2025

Start: 2025-05-20T17:00:57

Postpartum bleeding (nifas) — definitions and main rule

  • Definition: Nifas = blood discharged after childbirth; its legal effects determine suspension of prayer, fasting, and the obligation of ghusl.
  • Starting point for counting: from the moment of delivery of the child, even if blood did not accompany the child.
  • Main evidential hadith: Umm Salama — “The postpartum women used to sit for forty days” (reported by Abu Dawud, al‑Tirmidhi; graded hasan) — principal textual basis for the 40‑day measure.

Key rulings

  • No unanimous minimum: some early authorities held no fixed minimum (even a single emission or spot may be nifas); others reported minima (1 hour, 3, 4, 11, 25 days) in some transmissions.
  • Customary maximums reported:
  • 40 days: widely transmitted and relied upon by many companions, tabi‘un, and later scholars (common position).
  • 60 days: reported by other early authorities and appears in some Shafi‘i transmissions.
  • Other maxima reported rarely (30, 50, 70 days).
  • Ghusl after delivery: perform ghusl after childbirth; do not delay waiting for nifas to appear. The view requiring delay (e.g., one hour) is weak in this lesson.

Differences of opinion (summary)

  • Maximum duration: majority favor 40 days; some accept up to 60; rare reports of other lengths.
  • Minimum and what counts as nifas: some say any post‑partum bleeding (even a drop or mingled spot) is nifas; some narrations specify minima (see above).
  • Reconciliation: some read the 40‑day report as descriptive (common case) rather than absolute; others accept longer durations when clearly evidenced.

Practical action items

  • Start counting nifas from delivery.
  • Follow your madhhab/scholar on using 40 vs 60 days as a working maximum.
  • If bleeding occurs after childbirth—even a small mingled spot—many scholars treat it as nifas: stop prayer/fasting until nifas ends and ghusl is performed.
  • If no bleeding is seen after delivery: perform ghusl and then consult your madhhab about whether to count any nifas period or consider the woman pure.
  • If bleeding persists beyond the customary period, seek local scholarly guidance.

Purity and animals entering water; fish; grain contamination

Definitions

  • Small/limited water vs flowing water: quantities where contact may affect ritual purity.
  • Manfaḏ (outlet): the animal’s excretory opening where najāsa could be present.
  • Musāmaḥa (excusal): juristic permissiveness that minor contact does not render the water impure.

Animals (non‑dog, non‑pig, non‑human) entering water

  • Ruling presented (majority in lesson): when a tahir non‑human animal (e.g., mouse, bird, other beasts except dog/pig) enters small or flowing water, water is generally excused (remains usable) unless verified contamination is seen.
  • Rejected rationale: the lesson rejects the claim that birds avoid contaminating water by “contracting”; permissiveness is juristic, not mechanical.
  • Dissent (Qadi Husayn): if a larger beast with impurity on its outlet enters small water, the water is rendered impure (no excusal). He limits permissiveness to mice/birds.

Fish urine and aquatic contamination

  • Ruling: fish urine in water is regarded as pure/forgiven and does not render the water impure.
  • Cautionary note: if fish contents visibly leak into food/oil, some jurists consider the food affected — inspect and act accordingly.

Grain heaps and minor contamination

  • Ruling: when impurity falls on large grain heaps and removing it is unduly burdensome, leniency may apply (excusal) in certain classical formulations (e.g., dīsah contexts).
  • Practical caution: if urine directly contaminates grain outside the specific hardship context, washing or discarding is required.

Practical action items

  • If a mouse/bird/other non‑dog/pig animal falls into small household water without apparent contamination, you may rely on the permissive view and continue using it (especially for irrigation/garden use).
  • If you can verify visible contamination (smell, color, particles), err on the side of purification or discard for use in purification.
  • Do not assume pond/bathwater is impure because of fish urine alone.
  • For grain: if contamination is minor and removal is impractical, consult a local scholar; otherwise wash/discard visibly contaminated portions.

Istinjā’ (cleansing), prepuce (al‑qalf), retained fluids, and ghusl

Key definitions and principles

  • Istinjā’: cleansing with water after excretion to remove physical impurity (najasah).
  • Qalf (prepuce): foreskin or similar covering where impurity can be retained under folds.
  • Principle: inner parts/folds that correspond to the external organ must be washed in major purification (ghusl); stones (tayammum) alone are insufficient when impurity has reached inner folds.

Main rulings

  • When urine or semen is trapped under the prepuce, the inner surface must be washed during ghusl (major ritual bath).
  • If semen was trapped and later released after ghusl was performed when it was still trapped, one does not need to repeat ghusl for that later release.
  • A packing (al‑ḥashwah) used to stop blood becomes impure if it contains blood/urine and is a carrier of impurity.
  • Cow urine on grain in the specific case of dīsah (cattle threshing/trampling) is excused by many scholars due to hardship; outside dīsah, washing is required.

Differences of opinion

  • Validity of prayer while impurity remains under an uncut prepuce:
  • Qāḍī Shurayḥ: prayer may be valid (lenient).
  • al‑Qaffāl and the preferred view in the lesson: prayer is invalid until the impurity is removed and washed.
  • Circumcision (khitan): classical positions vary—some make it recommended or obligatory; practices about who may perform it differ; consult local scholarly and medical advice.

Practical action items

  • In janāba or after seminal/urinary emission, wash the glans and inner preputial fold thoroughly with water during ghusl.
  • Do not rely on stones for cleansing when impurity has entered inner folds—water is required.
  • If a packing contains blood/urine, remove and wash/replace it before prayer if possible.
  • If semen/urine trapped under the prepuce releases after ghusl, no repeat ghusl is required per the lesson’s ruling.
  • For circumcision questions or ambiguous genital cases (khunthā), seek medical and qualified juristic guidance.

Istihadha / chronic bleeding and urinary incontinence (practical worship rules)

Definitions

  • Istihadha: irregular/pathological uterine bleeding outside the menstrual cycle.
  • Sals / urinary incontinence: continuous or intermittent urine leakage.

Key rulings

  • If blood/urine issues after purification in chronic conditions, jurists disagree whether every discharge nullifies wudū’/ghusl.
  • Two main approaches:

1. Restricted concession: exemption applies only to small quantities; abundant discharge requires renewal of purification before prayer (view attributed to Ibn Hajar, al‑Ramli and others).

2. Broader concession: exemption applies even to abundant discharge in chronic cases (position attributed to Ibn Rifa‘ah and some transmitters).

  • For fasting: preservation of the fast can justify leniency in treatment of dressings or packing when changing/washing would invalidate the fast or cause harm.

Practical action items

  • Ensure water reaches the skin in wudū’/ghusl even if covered (hair, grafts).
  • With chronic bleeding/incontinence:
  • Use appropriate packing/dressings and change as required.
  • For small intermittent flows many jurists permit continuing prayer without repeating purification for each drop—follow your madhhab.
  • For abundant flow, the safer practice is to renew purification and change dressings before prayer unless a specific concession applies.
  • When in doubt, follow local madhhab guidance or adopt the cautious practice for acts immediately affecting validity of worship.

Zakat on receivables (debts owed to you) and business assets

Definitions

  • Receivables: debts owed by customers (accounts receivable).
  • Hawl: completion of one lunar year in possession (relevant for zakat timing).
  • Nisab: minimum threshold for zakat liability.

Main rulings

  • Zakat is due on wealth in possession at the hawl; collectible receivables may be included if reasonably certain/collectible.
  • If debtors acknowledge the debt and it is collectible (not dilatory), include receivables in your zakatable assets at hawl.
  • If receivables are doubtful/delayed/no evidence, you may exclude them from zakat until actually collected; pay zakat on what you later receive.
  • Debts you owe: if you pay them before your hawl they reduce your zakatable wealth; unpaid debts at hawl are generally not deducted.

Trade goods vs tools

  • Inventory (raw materials, goods for sale, consumables used in trade) is zakatable.
  • Fixed business tools and machinery used in production (e.g., welding machines) are generally excluded from zakat as tools of trade.

Practical action items

  • At your zakat hawl date:
  • Count cash, inventory, and collectible receivables (if evidence and collectible) toward nisab.
  • Subtract debts actually paid before hawl; unpaid debts remain part of wealth for zakat purposes.
  • Keep clear records of receivables and evidence of debt acknowledgments.
  • If you want unpaid debts to reduce zakat, pay them before hawl.
  • For business owners: separate tools/equipment (non‑zakatable) from inventory/raw materials/consumables (zakatable) and value inventory at market price.
  • When collecting previously deferred receivables, pay zakat on amounts received according to the applicable hawl rules.

Closing phrase: "أستودعكم الله" (I entrust you to God)

  • Meaning: a supplication asking Allah to protect and safeguard those addressed.
  • Legal status: permissible and commendable as general dua; no objection in fiqh.
  • Practical responses: brief replies like “to you as well,” “in God’s protection,” or “May God protect you.”
  • Evidence: general Qur’anic encouragement to supplicate (e.g., “Call upon Me; I will respond” — Qur’an 40:60).

Authorities, evidential note, and general guidance

  • Named jurists referenced: Malik, Abu Hanifa, Ahmad, al‑Awza‘i, al‑Hasan al‑Basri, Qadi Husayn, al‑Qaffāl, Ibn Rifa‘ah, Ibn Hajar, al‑Nawawi, and others — differences reflect transmitted reports and madhhabic positions.
  • Many rulings above are juristic and based on classical transmission and ijtihad; some parts lacked direct Qur’an/hadith citation in the recording (except the hadith for nifas).
  • Practical rule of thumb: follow the authoritative position of the madhhab or local qualified scholar you adhere to; when disputes affect obligatory acts (prayer/fast/ghusl), prefer the cautious ruling or seek local guidance.
