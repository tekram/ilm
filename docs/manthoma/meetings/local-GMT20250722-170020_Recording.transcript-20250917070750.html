Eid Moon Sighting Rules - July 22, 2025

📅 2025-07-22 🕐 17:00

Case: Moon-sighting dispute (Shawwal, 30th of Ramadan)

  • Facts
  • Two men received a written report that the new moon was sighted in another town (about a stage away) on the night of the 30th of Ramadan.
  • They informed the local judge, who refused to accept the report and maintained the month had not ended.
  • Despite the judge’s rejection, the two men publicly proclaimed takbīr in streets and mosques, announced Eid for the next day, and coerced some people to break their fast.
  • Many others later claimed to have seen the crescent around one-third of an hour before sunrise on the 29th morning; however, skilled observers in the area did not see it.

Legal questions

  • May the two men lawfully break their fast based on that report?
  • May they publicly proclaim Eid (takbīr, Eid prayer) and compel others?
  • May the ruler punish or restrain them if their action is impermissible?

Main rulings on moon-sighting and public proclamation

  • Public proclamation of Eid (public takbīr, Eid prayer, public announcement) based on a disputed or unconfirmed sighting is impermissible.
  • The ruler (walī al-amr) must prevent and restrain such public acts and may impose disciplinary measures (taʿzīr).
  • Forcing others to break the fast or compelling the community is especially serious and unlawful.
  • Private breaking of the fast
  • If an individual (or people in a town sharing the same dawn) is firmly convinced by direct, trustworthy sighting or by a credible report, that person may break the fast privately.
  • However, if the official authority has not accepted the sighting, such reliance should be kept private to avoid public confusion and conflict.
  • Morning (dawn) sightings
  • Testimony that the crescent was seen “in front of the sun” (i.e., before sunrise on the 29th) is widely rejected by the majority of classical scholars; such reports are treated as false and do not produce legal consequences.
  • Role of astronomical evidence
  • When reliable calculation or expert consensus demonstrates the physical impossibility of a sighting, eyewitness testimony claiming the contrary is to be rejected.
  • There is debate over whether calculation alone suffices or whether many eyewitnesses/judicial acceptance can outweigh calculation; many scholars accept decisive astronomical proof (or tawātur of works) as controlling.

Evidences and scholarly positions

  • Ibn Hajar: allowed a person who sincerely believes a sighting to break fast privately even if the judge did not accept, but forbade publicizing it and warned of punishment if done publicly.
  • Astronomical/technical arguments (Ibrāhīm ibn ʿUbay al‑Asbahī, al‑Yawāqīt): used to show some reported sightings (especially before sunrise) are impossible; such testimony is rejected.
  • Numerous classical transmitters and jurists refused to accept dawn-sighting testimonies (examples cited in lessons: Muḥammad ibn ʿAlī, ʿAbd al‑ʿAzīz, Aḥmad, ʿAlawī ibn ʿAbd Allāh, Ṭāhir ibn Muḥammad ibn Hāshim, ʿAbd Allāh ibn Qutn).
  • Usūl (methodology): balance witnesses’ quality and numbers against scientific/astronomical certainty; prefer rulings that preserve communal order.

Practical action items (moon-sighting)

  • For rulers/judges:
  • Prevent and stop public takbīr/Eid prayer based on unconfirmed or impossible sighting reports.
  • Discipline those who publicly announce Eid or coerce others without official confirmation.
  • Accept reliable astronomical evidence and expert consensus when it proves impossibility.
  • For individuals/witnesses:
  • Do not publicly announce Eid or call for Eid prayer unless the competent authority accepts the sighting or accepted evidentiary standards are met.
  • If personally convinced by trustworthy direct testimony from your same-dawn locality, you may break the fast privately but avoid publicizing it.
  • Treat morning-sighting claims (before sunrise on the 29th) with extreme caution; prefer the majority scholarly position rejecting them.
  • For scholars/muftīs:
  • Weigh eyewitness testimony against astronomical possibility and the number/quality of witnesses.
  • Prioritize community unity and avoid rulings that invite fitna.

Witness reliability (al-ʿadālah): definitions, criteria, and rulings

  • Definition: al-ʿadālah is the moral/legal uprightness required of a witness for their testimony to be accepted.
  • Core criteria commonly cited:
  • Muslim, adult, sane, and legally responsible.
  • Sound perception (ability to see/hear) and accurate reporting.
  • Upright character: consistent performance of obligatory acts (prayer), avoidance of major sins, absence of habitual disgraceful conduct (backbiting, slander, undermining scholars).
  • Major sins (kabāʾir)
  • Committing major sins disqualifies a person’s al‑ʿadālah outright.
  • Minor sins (saghāʾir)
  • Two positions:
  • Strict: persistent commission of a minor sin can render one like a major sinner and invalidate al‑ʿadālah.
  • Balanced (prevalent among classical commentators like al‑Bājūrī, Ibn Hajar): operative test is the overall balance — if acts of obedience predominate, al‑ʿadālah remains; if sins predominate (or are equal), it is lost.
  • Repetition of a minor sin may be treated as escalating its seriousness.
  • Practical approach for judges and communities:
  • Do not reject testimony wholesale on the assumption “everyone sins”; assess whether sinful actions actually dominate the person’s life.
  • If reliable proof shows sins outweigh obedience, disqualify; otherwise preserve trustworthiness and seek reform where needed.

Practical action items (witness reliability)

  • Individuals: avoid persistent minor sins; make good deeds observable; avoid aiding or normalizing sin (e.g., spreading forbidden images, slander).
  • Courts: examine overall moral balance; prefer corrections and rehabilitation rather than blanket exclusion; treat habitual repetition of sins seriously.
  • Communities: use caution before labeling people fāsiq; require clear evidence of predominance of disobedience.

Food, impurity, and small creatures

  • General rulings
  • Small worms/insects mixed in foods (vinegar, fruit, cheese, honey) that are difficult to separate: generally excused (maʿfūw) and permissible to eat with the food.
  • Deliberately removing and eating those insects alone is not permitted.
  • Dead ants or tiny dispersed insects in honey are excused and not treated as impurity preventing its eating.
  • Fish and innards
  • Small fish eaten whole (sardines, tiny local species): generally permissible to eat without removing entrails.
  • Disputed cases:
  • Lenient view: fish with excreta or limited filth can be eaten; oil used in frying remains pure.
  • Stringent view: if fish contains filth or is decomposed (e.g., feseekh), the entire fish (and possibly frying oil) is impure and forbidden.
  • Feseekh (salted/fermented fish): scholars differ — some permit after washing, others prohibit as irrecoverably impure.
  • Vessels and small impurity
  • Be cautious about accepting novel modern assertions that a vessel or its contents are excused without classical precedent; prefer transmitted opinions from established scholars.

Practical action items (food)

  • If tiny creatures are mixed with food and separation is burdensome, eat the food including the creatures.
  • Do not deliberately collect/eat the creatures alone.
  • For suspicious or clearly decomposed fish (feseekh), prefer washing and avoid if significant doubt; consult local scholarly opinion if necessary.
  • Prefer rulings traceable to established authorities; be skeptical of isolated modern claims without precedent.

Use of apocalyptic hadiths and claims of the Mahdī

  • Basis and evidences
  • The idea of the Mahdī is derived mainly from hadith literature; the Qurʾān does not explicitly mention him.
  • Hadiths about the Mahdī and eschatological signs vary in authenticity (some are weak or disputed).
  • Rulings and principles
  • Do not accept or pledge allegiance to someone claiming to be the Mahdī without clear, strong evidence and scholarly verification.
  • Do not derive routine legal rulings or public policy decisions solely from ambiguous apocalyptic narrations.
  • Enjoining good and forbidding wrong remains obligatory when the ruling is clear and safe to act on; but avoid using shaky eschatological signs to justify sectarian action.
  • Practical action items
  • Remain skeptical of contemporary Mahdī claims; consult qualified, reputable scholars and institutions before reacting.
  • Avoid spreading unverified claims on social media; prevent fitna.
  • If a claimant incites sedition or unlawful acts, refuse cooperation and notify appropriate authorities and learned scholars.

Differences of opinion — summary

  • Moon-sighting: private reliance vs. public proclamation; acceptance of morning sightings; weight of astronomical calculation vs. eyewitness testimony.
  • Al-ʿadālah: whether persistence in minor sins alone invalidates reliability vs. balance-of-deeds test.
  • Food/impurity: permissibility of small creatures mixed with food; permissibility of fish with innards or fermented fish (feseekh).
  • Apocalyptic evidence: appropriate scope and weight of end-times hadith in everyday legal decisions.

Brief practical takeaway (concise)

  • Do not publicly announce Eid, perform communal takbīr, or coerce others based on unconfirmed or astronomically impossible sightings; rulers must prevent and may discipline such acts.
  • Private reliance on a reliable local sighting may be permitted, but keep it private if authorities haven’t confirmed it.
  • Reject reports of crescent sighting before sunrise on the 29th in light of the majority classical position and when astronomical proof shows impossibility.
  • Assess witnesses’ reliability by overall moral balance; major sins disqualify, persistent minor sins may disqualify if they predominate.
  • Small insects/worms mixed with foods are generally excused when separation is impractical; eating such creatures alone is not permitted.
  • Treat claims of the Mahdī and apocalyptic signs with caution; require strong, scholarly evidence before taking action or pledging support.
