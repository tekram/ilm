Musaqat: Conditions, Obligations & Rulings on Irrigation Contracts - April 02, 2026

Overview

  • Section covered: the final three pillars (arkān, أركان) of musaqat (مساقاة — irrigation/cultivation contract), its binding nature, and the ruling when the worker (ʿāmil, عامل) flees
  • Also covered: mughārasa (مغارسة — tree-planting contract), conditions of validity, division of obligations between owner and worker, and the contract's formula (ṣīgha, صيغة)
  • Session concluded with a practical fiqh question on multi-storey building reconstruction

1. Condition: Exclusive Allocation of Fruit to Both Parties (Takhṣīṣ al-Thamar bihimā — تخصيص الثمر بهما)

The ruling

  • It is a condition of musaqat that the fruit be exclusively for the two contracting parties (owner and worker) — no portion may be stipulated for a third party
  • Exception: if the third party is a slave (raqīq, رقيق) owned by one of the two parties, his share counts as the owner's, so the contract remains valid
  • If a portion is assigned to a hired employee (ajīr, أجير) of one party, this is not treated like the slave exception — the contract is invalid

Textual note on wording

  • Some scholars objected that the phrase "specialisation of the fruit to both" (takhṣīṣ al-thamar bihimā) is inverted and should read "specialisation of both to the fruit" (takhṣīṣuhumā bil-thamar)
  • Sheikh Salem explained both phrasings are grammatically acceptable in Arabic — the preposition bā (ب) can attach to either the restricted or the restrictor — so no actual error exists

Key principles

  • The fruit must be shared by fraction (juzʾiyya, جزئية), not by a fixed quantity — a fixed-quantity payment converts the contract from musaqat into a hire (ijāra, إجارة)
  • Stipulating the whole fruit for the owner alone, or for the worker alone, invalidates the contract — parallel to the rule in qirāḍ (قراض — profit-sharing investment)
  • If the worker knew of the invalidity and that he was owed nothing, he receives no compensation; if he did not know, he receives ujrat al-mithl (أجرة المثل — fair market wage)
  • Stipulating dates (tamr, تمر) for one party and grapes (ʿinab, عنب) for the other also invalidates the contract

Differences of opinion

  • Al-Rūyānī held that even stipulating the whole fruit for the owner is valid because the worker acted in hope of what the law makes possible; Ibn Hajar rejected this, saying the contract structure requires mutual sharing

2. Prohibition on Concluding Musaqat as a Liability on the Worker's Obligation (fī al-Dhimma — في الذمة)

The ruling

  • Musaqat must be contracted over a specific identified orchard — it cannot be assumed as an obligation in the worker's abstract liability (dhimma)
  • If the worker (first party) sub-contracted another worker without the owner's permission, or assigned the work to a specified substitute, the sub-contract is invalid
  • If the contract period elapsed: the contract is annulled; all fruit belongs to the owner; the first worker receives nothing at all; the second worker receives nothing if he knew of the invalidity, but receives ujrat al-mithl from the first worker if he did not know

3. Condition: Both Parties Must Know the Two Shares by Fraction

The ruling

  • Both the owner's and the worker's shares must be known, stated as fractions (e.g. one-third, two-thirds, half), parallel to qirāḍ
  • The word "between us" (baynanā, بيننا) in the contract formula is interpreted as equal halves
  • Variant readings in manuscripts: bayna al-sanayn (between the two years), al-shayʾayn (the two things), al-naṣībayn (the two shares) — Sheikh Salem noted all carry valid meanings: unequal proportions could be set per year

4. Validity of Musaqat After the Fruit Has Appeared (baʿd ẓuhūr al-thamara — بعد ظهور الثمرة)

The ruling (predominant view — al-aẓhar)

  • The predominant position in the Shafi'i school: musaqat is valid both before and after the fruit appears, and is in fact more appropriate after appearance because it is further from gharar (غرر — uncertainty) and from the risk of crop failure
  • Critical condition: the contract must be concluded before budū al-ṣalāḥ (بدو الصلاح — the onset of ripeness), because most of the work still remains at that stage
  • After budū al-ṣalāḥ — even in a portion of the crop — musaqat is absolutely impermissible, by near-consensus (qīla ijmāʿan), analogous to sale of ripe fruit

Key principles

  • The rationale for validity before ripeness: most of the labour (irrigation, pollination, thinning) is still required
  • The fruit is treated as non-existent (nuzzila manzilat al-maʿdūm) in terms of what has appeared, so stipulating a fraction of it is not like stipulating a fraction of the tree itself

5. Mughārasa (المغارسة — Tree-Planting Contract): Ruling and Scholarly Dispute

Definition

  • Mughārasa (also mughāla): owner provides young, unplanted palm shoots (wadiyy, ودي) and land; worker plants, cultivates, and tends them; upon fruiting the tree or its fruit is shared
  • Very common in agricultural communities — Sheikh Salem noted it is practiced extensively in Hadramawt under the name mukhālaʿa (مخالعة) or mughāla

Ruling in the Shafi'i school

  • Standard position: not permissible, because musaqat is a rukhṣa (رخصة — dispensation from the norm) and dispensations are restricted to their textual scope; mughārasa is not covered by the permission
  • Al-Subkī transmitted from the four schools that they prohibit it; some claimed scholarly consensus (ijmāʿ al-umma) on prohibition
  • Counter-view: a number of early scholars (salaf) permitted it; this is the operative practice in Hadramawt
  • Sheikh Salem: the claim of consensus is contested; the Hadrami scholarly tradition follows the view allowing it

Consequences if contracted invalidly

  • The trees remain the property of their original owner (whoever provided the shoots or the land)
  • The land owner owes the worker ujrat al-mithl for his labour and tools; the worker owes the land owner rent for the land (ujrat al-arḍ)

Valid form: pre-planted tree with a stipulated period

  • If the tree is already planted and the owner stipulates a term for the worker with a fraction of the fruit as compensation, this is valid provided the term is one during which fruiting is expected to occur — even if most of the term produces no fruit, it is treated like months within a single year
  • If fruiting is not expected in the stipulated term, the contract is invalid for lack of consideration — though the worker may receive ujrat al-mithl in cases of uncertainty where both possibilities were equal

Musaqat with a co-owner of the orchard

  • A co-owner may enter a musaqat with his partner in jointly-owned trees if he stipulates for the partner a share greater than the partner's ownership fraction as compensation for labour
  • If the stipulated share merely equals the partner's ownership fraction — no contract is valid, because there is no consideration
  • The worker receives no wage in that case either
  • Ibn Hajar's resolution: musaqat permits what a plain hire (ijāra) does not, because the latter strictly requires the worker's effort to be applied to the hirer's exclusive property

6. Conditions of Validity: What May Not Be Stipulated on the Worker

The ruling

  • The worker must not be burdened with tasks outside the category of normal musaqat work (e.g. building a wall around the orchard) — such a stipulation invalidates the contract because it is a hire without consideration for that extra work
  • Conversely, stipulating the worker's obligations on the owner also invalidates the contract (with the exception noted in al-Buwayṭī's text, which is held weak)

Sole possession of the orchard by the worker

  • It is a condition that the worker have sole possession (yad, يد) of the orchard so he may work whenever he wishes
  • Stipulating that the orchard remain in the owner's hand, or in the owner's slave's hand, or even jointly with the worker, corrupts the contract
  • Exception: requiring the owner's slave to assist the worker does not invalidate the contract — parallel to the rule in qirāḍ, and especially appropriate here since some musaqat duties are incumbent on the owner by default

Knowing the work in general, and specifying the term

  • Knowing the work in summary form suffices — detailed specification is not required in the contract formula; custom (ʿurf, عرف) governs whatever is left unspecified
  • The term must be specified — the contract may not be open-ended (muṭlaqa) or perpetual (muʾabbada), because musaqat is a binding contract analogous to hire; minimum term: the period in which fruit ordinarily appears and the work is substantially complete; maximum: as long as the tree typically survives
  • Stipulating a term exceeding the likely lifespan of the trees is also invalid
  • The default calendar is the Arabic/Hijri year; other calendars (Gregorian, Roman, etc.) may be stipulated if both parties know them
  • Timing the term by "until the fruit ripens" is invalid in the sounder view, because ripening is uncertain — it may advance or delay due to rain, heat or cold

7. What Happens When the Term Ends or the Fruit Ripens Early

Fruit ripens before end of term

  • The worker must continue to work the remainder of the term without additional wages
  • The partnership aspect is incidental (tābiʿa) rather than intended, so the owner bears no obligation from it

Term expires while fruit is still developing

  • If the fruit has appeared but not yet ripened (e.g., is still at the green balah stage), the worker receives his stipulated fraction of it
  • The owner must then continue to tend and maintain the orchard until harvest (jadād, جداد) and deliver the worker's full share at that time

No right to future growth after contract ends

  • The worker has no entitlement to fruit that develops after the contract term

8. Formula of the Contract (Ṣīgha — الصيغة)

Explicit (ṣarīḥ) formulas

  • Sāqaytuka ʿalā hādhā al-nakhl bi-kadhā min al-thamara — "I contract with you for this palm grove for such-and-such fraction of the fruit" — this is explicitly the term of the contract
  • Sallamtuhu ilayka litataʿahhadahu — "I hand it to you to tend it [for such a fraction]"
  • Iʿmal ʿalayhi / taʿahhadhu bi-kadhā — "Work on it / tend it for such a fraction"

Differences of opinion on these phrases

  • Ibn Rifʿa considered all three explicitly clear (ṣarīḥ) because each conveys the meaning of the contract
  • Al-Subkī and al-Adhraʿī held the latter two are allusive (kināya); Ibn Hajar appears to follow this, requiring niyya (intention) to activate them
  • The relied-upon view according to al-Mughnī and al-Nihāya: the first formula is explicit; the status of the others is disputed but they suffice for the contract

Acceptance (qabūl)

  • Verbal acceptance immediately following the offer is required — analogous to the requirement in sale
  • A mute's gesture or written acceptance with intention is valid even from one who can speak
  • Detailed specification of the work is not required in the acceptance formula
  • The contract may be expressed in words other than the technical term musaqat — the controlling factor is custom (ʿurf)
  • Whatever is unspecified in the contract is governed by the locally prevalent custom; if no prevalent custom exists, detailed specification becomes obligatory

9. Obligations of the Worker (ʿĀmil)

Principle

  • The worker is responsible — whether doing the work himself or through a deputy — for everything needed for the fruit's welfare and increase that recurs annually
  • He bears the obligation of labour (ʿamal), not of providing physical supplies (ʿayn)

Specific duties on the worker

  • Irrigation (saqy, سقي): watering if the trees do not drink through their roots (i.e., are not of the baʿl type which draws naturally from groundwater), and all appurtenances: repairing the water channel mouth (ṭawq al-māʾ), turning the water wheel (dawlab, دولاب), opening and closing the canal head (rās al-sāqiya)
  • Clearing the water channel (tanbiyyat al-nahr): removing silt, clay, weeds, or harmful branches that obstruct flow
  • Repairing the basins (iṣlāḥ al-ajājīn, إصلاح الأجاجين): the circular earthen basins around palm trunks that hold irrigation water, named after the ijāna (wash basin)
  • Pollination (talqīḥ, تلقيح): placing male pollen (from the male palm's ṭalʿ) onto the female palm's spadix
  • Removing harmful grass and branches (tanḥiyat ḥashīsh wa-quḍbān muḍirra): clearing weeds — even green ones — and branches that hinder the fruit or consume water, as required by custom
  • Guarding the fruit (ḥifẓ al-thamar): on the tree, in the orchard, and from birds and thieves; if the orchard is too large or thieves too numerous for the worker to guard alone, the cost falls on him (the relied-upon position, contra al-Adhraʿī)
  • Harvesting (jadād, جداد) and drying (tajfīf): cutting the fruit and drying it — the sounder view obliges both, absolutely, not only when customary or stipulated; this is because both are part of "setting the fruit right" (iṣlāḥ al-thamar); al-Adhraʿī's contrary opinion (that drying costs fall on the owner) is considered weak
  • The worker's obligation when he fails to perform a duty: a proportionate reduction is made from his share, analogous to the rule in jiʿāla (جعالة)

10. Obligations of the Owner (Mālik)

Principle

  • The owner is responsible for whatever serves the tree and its permanence (as opposed to what serves the fruit)

Specific duties on the owner

  • Providing pollen material (ṭalʿ): the physical male palm pollen used in pollination — the worker performs the act, but the owner supplies the material; similarly, the wicker baskets (qawṣara, قوصرة — called ʿabiyya in Hadramawt, woven from palm fronds) that protect grape clusters from birds
  • Trellising (taʿrīsh, تعريش): installing vine supports/trellises if this is the local custom, so the vine can spread; laying grass over grape clusters to protect them from sun
  • Repairing permanent infrastructure (major walls, original water channels) falls on the owner, not the worker

The owner hiring the worker for the owner's own duties

  • The owner may hire the worker to perform duties that are the owner's own responsibility — this hire is valid
  • If the worker performs the owner's duties with the owner's permission (even without a separate hire agreement), the worker is entitled to ujrat al-mithl — likened to the rule: "pay my debt [and I owe you]"

Role of custom regarding division of duties

  • The textual division of duties (as set out in the classical texts) was itself derived from a prior custom; where the texts have fixed a duty on a party without referencing custom, that assignment cannot be overridden by a later contrary custom
  • This is based on the principle that a supervening custom (ʿurf ṭāriʾ) does not displace an earlier established one, as stated by al-Zarkashī in his legal maxims (qawāʿid)

11. Digression: Ruling on Extracting Healthy Teeth for a Full Denture

The question

  • Raised by Nizar: some dentists advise elderly patients to extract remaining healthy teeth so a full denture can be fitted — claiming it is more stable and reduces future complications

Ruling

  • Sheikh Salem: if the only way to fit a stable full denture is by removing the remaining healthy teeth — and the dentist's professional judgement confirms this provides genuine benefit (better function, stability, avoidance of future problems) — then extracting them is permissible
  • This is because the extraction is not mere transgression (taʿaddī) but is driven by something resembling necessity or serious need (ḥāja shādīda / ḍarūra) in order to achieve a proper prosthetic fit
  • The classical prohibition on extracting a healthy tooth was understood in the context of no benefit; here a clear benefit exists
  • Sheikh Salem added: if the purpose is purely cosmetic savings (cheaper long-term), that factor alone does not justify extraction — the benefit must be medical/functional

12. Practical Question: Multi-Storey Building Reconstruction by One Floor Owner

The question

  • A multi-storey building collapsed. The ground-floor owner wants to rebuild; the upper-floor owners do not wish to cooperate. If the ground-floor owner rebuilds on his own, the upper-floor owners will later want to build on the foundations he lays — is that permissible?

Ruling

  • The upper-floor owners have a right to build above, since that is their entitlement from the original ownership structure
  • However, they must compensate the ground-floor owner appropriately — either by paying rent (ujra) for use of his structural work, purchasing their share of it, or entering a partnership agreement (shirka) in which his construction is counted as a contribution
  • If the ground-floor owner built without their permission, he retains the right to keep his construction in place but may charge them ujrat al-mithl or its equivalent value, since they are benefiting from what he built and they have a right to build above it

Allah knows best.
