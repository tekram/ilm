Bayʿ al‑ʿahdah (sale with a covenant / bayʿ al‑wafāʾ / bayʿ al‑nās)

Definition and typical form

  • A sale in which the seller transfers title of an asset (commonly a house) to a buyer for a cash amount usually below market, while the seller remains in possession and use — effectively securing liquidity like a loan without an explicit loan contract.
  • Also called bayʿ al‑wafāʾ or bayʿ al‑nās (local name used in Mecca).
  • Legally essential: the right of the seller to redeem/return the asset must be embedded in the sale’s legal characterization (treated as a recognized sale type). Framing the transaction as an ordinary sale plus an external condition (“I sell on condition I may return it later”) is not the valid form.

Practical motivations

  • Provide liquidity for the owner without creating a recorded loan or interest.
  • Avoid practical difficulties of debt recovery (delay, insolvency, litigation).
  • Tax/zakat and regulatory avoidance in some contexts.
  • Often conducted privately to avoid scrutiny.

Key rulings and principles

  • Origin and schools: associated historically with Shāfiʿī usage; some elements resemble Mālikī treatment of «wafāʾ».
  • Redemption must be intrinsic to the sale type (bayʿ al‑ʿahdah/bayʿ al‑wafāʾ), not a separable contractual condition.
  • No separate system of arsh (compensation) applies simply by virtue of bayʿ al‑ʿahdah; rights are attached to the specific ʿayn (the particular asset). If the asset perishes in the buyer’s possession, the seller does not automatically retain ongoing proprietary rights absent stipulation.
  • If the seller’s heirs or partners later seek annulment (faskh) or redemption, rescission generally affects only the share of the person who acts/consents—not the whole estate automatically.
  • A judge (qāḍī/hakim) does not stand in as a proxy for absent heirs to consent to rescission or accept redemption, except in narrow custodial/necessitous cases aimed at preserving the asset (e.g., to prevent loss or discharge debts). This distinguishes the hakim’s custodial role from a guardian’s (walī) discretionary role for an orphan’s interest.

Differences of opinion / transmitted positions

  • Some transmitted reports allow one present heir to redeem the whole property on behalf of all; other authoritative positions restrict rescission to the acting heir’s share.
  • Transmitters/scholars cited include Sharīf Muḥammad ibn al‑Ḥasan, Sheikh ʿAbdallāh Bā Faḍl, Abu Khamīs (transmission unclear), Abdullah ibn al‑Hajj (Muqaddimah al‑Hadramiyyah), and Shāfiʿī and Mālikī references — reflecting divergence in transmission and opinion.
  • Ibn Ḥajar criticized overly lax juristic endorsements of the practice; overall the lesson favored the restrictive view limiting faskh to individual shares.

Evidences

  • Rulings in the lessons are derived from classical juristic reasoning and precedent; no explicit Qurʾān or ḥadīth passages were cited in the provided segments.

Practical action items

  • Draft bayʿ al‑ʿahdah explicitly as that sale type; avoid framing it as a conditional sale.
  • If heirs/partners may be absent later, secure written consents or powers of attorney beforehand; do not rely on a judge to act as their proxy except to preserve property under necessity.
  • When multiple heirs/partners exist, record whether redemption/annulment applies to specific shares; get unanimous written agreement if intended to bind all.
  • Include contractual clauses addressing risk of loss (perishing) and compensation mechanisms.
  • For litigators: cite mainstream positions that limit faskh to the individual’s share and the non‑proxy role of the hakim (sources named above).

Rescission (faskh) and representation of absent heirs

Scenario and core legal issues

  • Common fact pattern: property sold on covenant by or for a deceased (murith); some heirs present, some absent; the asset is ruined and present heirs seek rescission/repurchase (fakk / faskh).
  • Questions: Can a judge represent an absent heir in consenting to rescission? Can one present heir validly rescind the sale for all heirs?

Key rulings

  • The hakim/qāḍī does not normally represent absent heirs to consent to rescission; his duty is custodial (preservation), not developmental. He may act (sell/lease) only in necessity to prevent loss or secure debts.
  • A wali (guardian) of an orphan may exercise broader discretion for the orphan’s interest; this does not extend to a judge acting for absent adult heirs.
  • Rescission effected by one present heir generally affects only that heir’s share. Coercing transfers of other heirs’ shares without their consent is invalid.
  • Where one heir offers to redeem the whole, there are two practical outcomes/options:
  • The buyer may accept rescission only as to the paying heir’s share and keep the sale as to remaining shares; or
  • The buyer may require the rescinding party to gather/all heirs to effect full rescission, or negotiate purchase of the whole.

Differences of opinion

  • Some early reports permit one heir to rescind on behalf of all; many jurists restrict rescission to the acting heir’s share.
  • Practical jurisprudence in the lessons favors restricting unilateral rescission to the acting heir.

Practical action items

  • Attempt to resolve rescission collectively among all heirs.
  • Do not assume a judge will validly act for absent heirs unless preservation/necessity is clearly involved.
  • When an heir offers payment to redeem, clarify in writing whether the rescission is for his share only or for the entire property; require written authority from absent heirs if the intent is to bind them.
  • Courts should decide with focus on equitable treatment and preservation of absent heirs’ rights.

Trusts/undertakings (ʿahd / muʿhad) and subsequent transfers

Definitions and core rules

  • ʿAhd / muʿhad: custody/undertaking of property by a trustee/manager on behalf of others.
  • Valid transfer to third parties requires demonstration of a prior valid annulment, sale, or explicit authority from the rightful owners.
  • Payment of price to a single heir (even if righteous) does not, without further proof, transfer other heirs’ shares.
  • A deed naming other persons does not, by itself, establish their transfer/ownership absent authorization, payment, or recognized mode of transfer.

Rulings from the case studies

  • If valid annulment/transfer of heirs’ shares occurred prior to subsequent sales, later purchasers may have valid title.
  • If no such annulment/transfer is proven, later sales are valid only regarding the sellers’ actual shares; claimant heirs retain their shares.
  • Separation of one party’s share (fakk al‑naseeb) is permissible if it causes no harm to that party.

Practical action items

  • Buyers: verify chain of title, demand proof of authority to sell (annulment documents, receipts, powers of attorney).
  • Claimants/heirs: preserve documentary/witness evidence to contest unauthorized sales.
  • Sellers: document consents and the legal mode of transfer explicitly.
  • Courts: require clear proof before validating transfers involving absent heirs or trustees.

Family planning, contraceptives, and sterilization — rulings from the Fiqh seminar

Core rulings (unanimous positions summarized)

  • Deliberate, large‑scale limitation of the human species is impermissible; parents must not shirk parental responsibilities to limit family size for material motives.
  • Temporary spacing of pregnancies is permissible when necessary to protect the breastfeeding, upbringing, or care of an existing child.
  • Permanent sterilization is generally impermissible for men and women, except when competent medical opinion indicates a high probability (ghalabat al‑ẓann) that pregnancy would cause the woman’s death or loss of an essential organ/function; such irreversible interventions require trustworthy medical evidence.
  • Temporary contraceptive methods that do not permanently remove fertility and cause no harm are generally permitted (with some madhhab differences).

Methodological and evidential notes

  • Seminar decisions relied on juristic principles and medical consultation; no explicit Qurʾān or ḥadīth were cited in the segments provided.
  • Differences arose over the evidential threshold (mere suspicion ẓann vs. predominance ghālabah al‑ẓann) required to permit sterilization.

Differences of opinion

  • Threshold for permitting permanent sterilization: some require strong probability of grave harm; others may accept lower thresholds with competent medical testimony.
  • Some madhhabs (notably Shāfiʿī in the lesson) are stricter regarding contraceptive permissibility without medical necessity.

Practical action items

  • Prefer reversible, temporary contraceptive methods for spacing.
  • For permanent sterilization, obtain a detailed written medical report from competent physicians showing high probability of grave risk; consult a qualified jurist before proceeding.
  • Avoid contraception motivated solely by material convenience or deliberate population‑limiting intent.

Badal al‑khulū (compensation for vacating) and landlord‑tenant rulings

Definitions

  • Badal al‑khulū: a lump‑sum payment in addition to rent paid to the landlord (or tenant) as compensation for waiving the right to recover the property or for securing vacating.
  • Deposit/security (ʿaṣimah): amount held by landlord to secure obligations.

Seminar rulings (practical summary)

  • Landlord may retain a security deposit intact; if used lawfully (e.g., to cover arrears), any balance must be returned at tenancy end.
  • A lump sum (badal al‑khulū) paid as compensation for waiving recovery is permissible; a landlord may keep and benefit from it.
  • If tenancy was granted without badal and without a fixed duration, the landlord may request eviction with reasonable notice appropriate to local custom.
  • If tenancy was granted without badal, the tenant may not claim badal from the landlord upon eviction.
  • The outgoing tenant may receive payment from a new tenant in return for vacating (practical custom), but this practice raises madhhab issues (see below).

Shāfiʿī difference and procedural notes

  • Shāfiʿī position: a tenant cannot unilaterally assign his tenancy creating a new landlord–tenant relation with a third party without the landlord’s explicit consent; proper methods are subletting (tenant remains bound) or mutual termination followed by a new lease.
  • The seminar allowed compensation arrangements more liberally; the lecturer warned this may conflict with Shāfiʿī procedure and cited Sheikh Muhammad al‑Khatib supporting the need for a new contract.

Practical action items (rental contracts)

  • Specify lease duration, deposit terms, any badal al‑khulū, and assignment/subletting rules in writing.
  • If landlord wishes to reclaim the property early, negotiate and record any compensation.
  • Tenants: secure written rent‑assignment or landlord consent before accepting payment from a prospective tenant to avoid invalidating rights.
  • For waqf leases, follow local waqf authority regulations and administrative law.

Additional practical rulings (funeral prayer; dress in prayer)

Funeral prayer over public sinners

  • An imam may refrain from leading the communal funeral prayer for a deceased who committed a public major sin as a form of admonition/deterrence; but:
  • It is preferable for the imam to perform a discreet/private prayer (e.g., at the grave) so the deceased is not deprived of prayer entirely.
  • If communal obligation (fard kifāya) is otherwise fulfilled, withholding the imam’s public leadership as admonition is permissible but should be used sparingly.
  • If the sinner repented or the sin was not public, caution is urged before refusing prayer.

Dress in prayer — isbal vs. tashmir

  • Isbal (garment below the ankles) and tashmir (rolling up garments above the ankles) are both concerns; scholars differ on priority.
  • Practical guidance from the lesson:
  • Prefer avoidance of tashmir (do not roll up garments in prayer) if forced to choose.
  • Exceptions for necessity, cleanliness, or hardship are permitted.
  • Avoid ostentation and arrogance; maintain proper covering and modesty.

Practical action items

  • Imams: use omission of the public funeral prayer only with clear pedagogical purpose; if possible, offer a discreet prayer later.
  • Worshippers: avoid rolling up garments in prayer; if required by necessity, do so humbly and only as needed.

Overarching evidential note and methodological guidance

  • The lessons rely mainly on classical fiqh reasoning, transmitted juristic opinions, and contemporary medical/legal consultation; the provided segments did not quote Qurʾān or ḥadīth texts directly.
  • Where schools differ (Shāfiʿī, Mālikī, etc.), follow the applicable madhhab guidance and local legal practice; when in doubt, seek a qualified jurist and documentary proof (medical reports, powers of attorney, deeds).

Consolidated practical checklist (for students, lawyers, parties)

  • Bayʿ al‑ʿahdah: characterize clearly as such in contracts; record redemption terms and risk allocation; obtain consents from all co‑owners or powers of attorney where relevant.
  • Rescission/Heirs: do not assume a judge will act for absent heirs except to preserve assets; each heir controls his share unless explicit joint authority exists.
  • Trust/chain of title: require proof of prior annulment or authority before buying; payment to one heir alone is not conclusive.
  • Family planning: prefer reversible methods; permanent sterilization only with strong medical evidence and juristic approval.
  • Tenancy: specify duration, deposits, badal, and transfer/subletting rules in writing; follow madhhab rules on assignment vs. subletting.
  • Funeral and prayer matters: use discretion; balance community admonition with the duty to preserve rights of the deceased; avoid tashmir in prayer when possible.

Allah knows best.
