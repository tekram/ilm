Musaqat Rulings, Commercial Incentives, and Miscellaneous Fiqh Questions - April 23, 2026

Start: 2026-04-23T16:00:27

1. Musaqat (المساقاة) — Owner's Obligations: Preserving the Orchard Infrastructure

The Question

  • What expenses in a musaqat (crop-sharing) contract fall on the owner (مالك) vs. the worker (عامل)?
  • Specifically: building walls, installing gates, waterwheels (دولاب), axes (فأس), mattocks (معول), sickles (منجل), and oxen for plowing or turning the waterwheel.

Ruling

  • All expenses aimed at preserving the original asset (حفظ الأصل) and that do not recur annually fall on the owner — e.g., constructing walls, installing a gate, waterwheel, axe, sickle, and oxen.
  • The principle: non-recurring, asset-preserving expenditures are the owner's responsibility regardless of custom.

Key Principles

  • Custom (عرف) governs some matters (e.g., the tailor's thread in ijarah) but not where the jurists have explicitly assigned responsibility.
  • The text raises an ishkal (استشكال): if custom ordinarily determines who pays for the tailor's thread in hire contracts, why not here? The answer: what preserves the trade permanently (حالاً ودواماً) falls on the owner; what is transient (e.g., thread used once) follows custom.
  • Digging a new irrigation channel (حفر نهر جديد) falls on the owner because it is established custom that the owner bears this.
  • Sealing cracks in walls (سد الثلم): governed by custom — may fall on owner or worker depending on locality.
  • Placing thorns on top of walls (وضع الشوك على رأس الجدار): also governed by custom; purpose is to deter intruders, cats, and animals.

2. Musaqat — Disputes, Worker's Failure to Perform, and Liability

The Question

  • If the worker abandons his duties and the trees deteriorate, does he bear liability?
  • If the owner and worker dispute mid-term whether the worker performed his obligations, whose word is believed?

Ruling (Based on Abu Zur'ah's Discussion)

  • If work that was abandoned can still be remedied (تدارك), the owner's claim is believed (he alleges non-performance) and the worker is compelled to act — because the default is non-performance and the owner can still establish evidence.
  • If the term has ended and remediation is no longer possible, the worker's word is believed — because the owner's claim would entail rescission of the contract, and the default is against rescission.
  • A researcher (غير واحد) holds: if the worker abandoned his duties until the trees spoiled, he is liable (ضامن).

Key Principles

  • Musaqat is binding (لازمة) on both parties before and after work begins — analogous to ijarah (hire) over subsisting assets.
  • Even if all fruit is destroyed by a calamity or usurpation, the worker must complete the work — just as the worker in qirad (قراض/مضاربة) must continue despite no profit.

3. Musaqat — Worker Who Flees, Falls Ill, or Is Imprisoned

The Question

  • If the worker flees before completing the work and the owner (or a third party) completes it voluntarily (متبرعاً), does the worker retain his entitlement?

Ruling

  • If the owner or a stranger completes the work voluntarily (تبرعاً) on behalf of the worker — whether the worker is absent or present — the worker retains his stipulated share.
  • Al-Subki's view: if the owner worked in his own interest (not on behalf of the worker), or if a stranger worked on behalf of the owner rather than the worker, the worker loses his entitlement — and this view is sound (ظاهر).
  • The debate: can the owner's work on his own share be treated as fulfilling the worker's debt? The text answers: the owner's intent (قصده المالك) directs the work — it is not automatically treated as repaying the worker's obligation.

4. Musaqat — When No One Volunteers: Role of the Judge (Hakim)

The Question

  • If no one volunteers to complete the work and the worker has fled or refuses, what is the procedure?

Ruling — Hierarchy of Remedies

  • Step 1: Compel the worker to work if he can be brought before the judge.
  • Step 2: If he has fled and cannot be produced, the judge hires a substitute from the worker's assets (after establishing the musaqat and the flight).
  • Step 3: If no assets exist, hire someone willing to work for a deferred wage.
  • Step 4: If that too fails, borrow on the worker's account from the owner or others; repay from the worker's share of the fruit.
  • Step 5: If all of the above fail, the owner works himself — but only after the judge sets the wage and designates the worker, per Ibn Rif'ah's preferred view.
  • If the judge is inaccessible (distant beyond the 'adwa distance, or present but unresponsive): the owner witnesses his expenditure formally — witnessing substitutes for judicial authorization — but only if he intends to reclaim the costs.

Key Principles

  • The worker's obligation to complete the work is treated like a debt (كالدين) — the judge acts as deputy to extract performance.
  • If the musaqat was contracted on the worker's person (على العين) rather than in his liability (على الذمة), the contract is rescinded upon his flight and all fruit goes to the owner; no hiring on his behalf is permitted in this case per some scholars (al-Adhru'i, al-Subki, al-Nawawi, al-Nashā'ī).
  • Al-Subki's view: the owner has a choice between rescission and patience when musaqat is on the 'ayn.

5. Musaqat — Death of the Worker

Ruling

  • If the worker dies before completing work and leaves an estate: the heir completes the work from the estate — like any other debt of the deceased.
  • The heir may complete the work personally or with his own funds but cannot be forced to use the estate's specific assets.
  • The owner must allow the heir to work if he is trustworthy and knowledgeable; if the heir refuses entirely, the judge hires a substitute.
  • If the worker died leaving no estate: the heir may work voluntarily but is not obligated.
  • Death of the owner does not dissolve the musaqat (whether on 'ayn or dhimmah) — the worker continues and takes his share.

6. Musaqat — Proven Treachery of the Worker

Ruling

  • If treachery (خيانة) is established by admission, testimony, or a returned oath: a supervisor (مشرف) is added — the worker's hand is not removed since performing the work remains his obligation.
  • The supervisor's wages fall on the worker (when treachery is confirmed); if only suspicion (ريبة) exists, the wages fall on the owner.
  • If the supervisor cannot prevent the treachery: hire a replacement from the treacherous worker's assets and terminate his involvement.

7. Musaqat — Fruit Turns Out to Belong to a Third Party

Ruling

  • If the fruit turns out to be owned by someone other than the musaqat-party (i.e., the contract was made over something not owned): the worker who was ignorant of this receives ujrat al-mithl (fair market wage) — because he wasted his labor in exchange for a defective consideration.
  • If the worker was aware the fruit wasn't the contracting party's: he receives nothing — the ijarah is void and no wage is owed for work in an unlawful context.

8. Commercial Incentive Prizes (الجوائز التشجيعية) — Shafi'i Analysis

The Question

  • A store offers prizes or draws (سحب) to customers who purchase above a threshold. Is this permissible?
  • A fatwa from the Jordanian Dar al-Ifta' ruled the practice impermissible when the customer's intent in purchasing is to obtain the prize.

Discussion and Ruling

  • Sheikh Salem's position: in the Shafi'i madhhab, consideration is given to the contract's wording (الألفاظ), not the underlying intent (مقاصد). As long as no explicit or binding pre-contract condition is attached, the sale is valid.
  • The Dar al-Ifta' fatwa (leaning toward other schools) held it impermissible if the buyer's purpose is the prize — a standard more aligned with Hanbali/Maliki methodology which looks to maqasid and practical outcomes.
  • If there is no contractual condition and the prize offer is a mere promise (وعد), it is not binding as a legal obligation; however, the store may be sinful (آثم) for deceiving customers.
  • The prize offer can be analyzed as a ji'ala (جعالة): "Whoever purchases X amount receives a coupon/entry." On this analysis, the prize promise becomes binding through ji'ala rules.
  • Key distinction: if the prize entry (القسيمة) is the object of the ji'ala — not the prize itself — then the obligation is to deliver the entry, not a specific prize. The prize is distributed by lot from among entries.

Key Principles

  • Shafi'i rule: pre-contract informal agreements (مواطأة قبل العقد) do not affect the validity of the contract — unless explicitly incorporated as a contractual condition in the offer and acceptance.
  • Mixing madhhabs (تلفيق): impermissible when the combination produces a ruling no single scholar holds. One may follow a different madhhab entirely on a discrete question, but cannot splice together two conflicting views to produce a third position.
  • Entering a transaction under a legal system that treats pre-contract conditions as binding (e.g., Hanafi-aligned civil law) while following the Shafi'i view internally: Sheikh Salem's view is that the sale contract itself is valid under the Shafi'i standard, but any extra-contractual payment the other party makes believing it is legally owed is not the Shafi'i party's right to keep — they were paid due to the other party's belief in a legal obligation, not voluntary generosity.

Differences of Opinion

  • Shafi'i madhhab: Contract valid if no explicit condition; buyer's intent irrelevant to contract validity; mawa'id (promises) not legally enforceable in court.
  • Other schools (Hanbali, Maliki): Substantive intent and outcomes (maalat) are considered; such purchases with prize-seeking intent may be impermissible as a form of gharar or quasi-gambling.
  • Talfiq issue: Nizar raised the concern of transacting with a party whose contract is governed by Hanafi-leaning civil law while personally applying Shafi'i rules — Sheikh Salem's response: the contract is valid on your end; however, extra payments extracted by law may not be yours to keep.

9. Pre-Contract Conditions and the Riba Question (related digression)

The Question

  • If two parties informally agree before the contract that a loan of 1,000 will be repaid as 1,200, then execute a clean loan contract with no mention of the excess — is this riba?

Ruling

  • In the Shafi'i school: all pre-contract understandings (مواطأة قبل العقد) are non-operative — they do not affect the contract's validity.
  • The clean loan is thus valid; the 200 excess cannot be enforced as a legal obligation.
  • However, this practice is makruh (disliked) because it is a means (wasilah) toward riba, which other schools consider outright prohibited.
  • The related case of pledging land usufruct as a nazar (vow) alongside a loan: some scholars say the vow is invalid because it is a hila (trick) toward riba, which is makruh — and vows do not bind in makruh acts. Others say the vow is valid because it is structurally separate from the loan contract.

10. Sale of Insects and Animals with Newly Emerging Benefits

The Question

  • Is it permissible to sell worms (ديدان) farmed as feed for chickens?
  • Classical position: insects are generally impermissible to sell because they have no benefit (لا ينتفع بها).

Ruling

  • Sheikh Salem's view (with acknowledged uncertainty): the prohibition was grounded in the reason (ta'lil — لعدم وجود المنفعة). If a genuine benefit now exists, the prohibition falls away.
  • Evidence: jurists permitted selling silkworms (دود الحرير) for silk production, and leeches (العلق) for therapeutic bloodletting — both premised on usefulness.
  • Mice and the five fasiq animals (الفواسق الخمس): explicitly stated impermissible in classical texts because no benefit was known. Today, mice are used in laboratory research — if a real benefit exists, the Sheikh leans toward permissibility.
  • Scorpions: some farms breed scorpions to extract venom for pharmaceutical use — likely permissible on the same reasoning.
  • Distinction from ritual impurity (najasah): the prohibition on selling najis substances (e.g., contaminated oil, carrion) is ta'abbudi (ritual in nature), not conditioned on absence of benefit — so that category remains prohibited regardless.

Differences of Opinion

  • Nizar noted a methodological parallel: al-Ramli tended to adhere strictly to the transmitted text (منقول); Ibn Hajar tended to apply the underlying ta'lil more flexibly. This session's discussion mirrors that methodological tension.
  • Sheikh Salem affirmed: applying the ta'lil to new cases is a takhrij mu'asir (contemporary derivation) — valid in fatwa even if not a transmitted nass, as long as it does not contradict a definitive ruling.

11. Purchasing Human Breast Milk for Bodybuilding

The Question

  • Some bodybuilders claim breast milk from nursing women has muscle-building properties and wish to purchase it. Is this permissible?

Ruling

  • Sheikh Salem: permissible in principle — the milk is pure (طاهر), owned (مملوك), and there is genuine benefit (منفعة).
  • No nursing relationship (رضاع) that triggers mahram status is established for an adult over two years of age.
  • No established prohibition (حرمة) or dislike (كراهة) has been textually confirmed for such consumption in adults.
  • The sale is valid: an owned, pure substance with a recognized benefit is lawfully sold.
  • Nizar noted some Hanafi scholars analogize this to benefiting from a human body — Sheikh Salem responded that once the milk is expressed (محلوب), that analogy no longer applies; it is treated as a separate owned substance.

12. Music in Recordings — Pending Fatwa from Dar al-Mustafa

The Question

  • Is listening to music in recorded format (cassettes, digital) permissible, given that the original live act of playing may have already ended?

Discussion

  • Some scholars argued: a recording is not a real-time sin; the prohibited act has already passed and what remains is merely a representation.
  • Sheikh Salem's view: the form of the sin (صورة المعصية) remains present in the recording — analogous to a sinful image painted on a wall. Even if the original act has ended, approving and listening to the remaining form constitutes iqrar 'ala al-munkar (إقرار على المنكر — acquiescing to a wrong).
  • A fatwa on this was drafted by Sheikh Salem and was submitted for review; Habib 'Umar reportedly made amendments to the version bearing the Dar al-Mustafa stamp.
  • Nizar noted that a recently published book compiling fatwas of Sheikh Salem's grandfather included a fatwa on "echo of sounds and music" (صدى الأصوات والموسيقى) ruling it impermissible, which aligns with Sheikh Salem's own position.

Allah knows best.
