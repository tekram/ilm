Usufruct Bequest Rules - September 30, 2025

📅 2025-09-30 🕐 17:00

Summary of Lessons — combined translation and fiqh rulings

Case 1 — Jointly owned house with a bequeathed usufruct (manfaʿa)

Facts

  • A deceased bequeathed the usufruct/benefit of a house for a specified (limited) period.
  • The house itself is co‑owned by several heirs.
  • Question: Can one co‑owner sell his share (immediately and absolutely) to another co‑owner or a third party while the usufruct is due to a legatee? Is that sale valid/enforceable?

Key rulings and principles

  • Prohibition to defeat the legatee’s right:
  • A co‑owner may not validly sell his share in a way that defeats a clearly bequeathed usufruct granted to someone else. Such a sale is ineffective insofar as it harms the legatee’s right.
  • Exception: sale to the legatee (the person who was granted the usufruct) is permissible — this does not defeat the bequest.
  • Consent converts defect into valid transfer:
  • If all interested parties (heirs and the legatee) agree to a sale/transfer, the transaction is permissible and effective because the legatee’s right is not being harmed.
  • Ambiguity in the bequest:
  • A bequest with uncertain scope or unclear exclusion (jahālat al‑mustathnā’) — e.g., unclear duration or unclear objects excluded from the usufruct — may render the bequest defective or invalid. The legal problem is the uncertainty, not merely that the usufruct exists.
  • Relation to ordinary rented/sold property:
  • The lesson contrasts ordinary sale/lease rules (where sale does not necessarily nullify a tenancy) with the special protective rule for bequests: a testamentary usufruct is a sharʿī right that cannot be defeated by unilateral sale of a co‑owner.

Differences of opinion / authorities

  • Adopted position (lesson): protect the legatee; sale that defeats the bequest is invalid except to the legatee or with the legatee’s consent.
  • Minority/former view (attributed to al‑Ramli): sale by a partner to another partner or third party may be valid even if usufruct is due — the lesson rejects this as less sound here.

Evidences / citations

  • No Qurʾan or hadith excerpts were quoted in the lesson excerpt. Authorities referenced: al‑Ramli (minority view) and the adopted protecting view; general reliance on classical fiqh principles about protecting testamentary rights.

Practical action items

  • Do not effect or register a sale of a co‑owner’s share that would defeat a clearly specified testamentary usufruct unless the legatee gives explicit written consent.
  • When drafting a will/bequest of usufruct: make the duration and scope explicit and unambiguous.
  • If all heirs plus the legatee agree to a sale/transfer, document that agreement clearly (written consent from the legatee), then complete the sale.
  • If unclear or disputed, consult a qualified jurist in your madhhab and examine the will’s exact wording.

Case 2 — Sale of land contracted by a measured sowing/produce (shortfall in agreed measure)

Facts

  • Sale agreed on condition that the land/produce measures a specified quantity (e.g., “20 dhirāʿ”).
  • After cultivation/measurement the actual measure fell short by one unit.
  • Questions: Is the sale valid? Does the buyer get a price reduction or the option to rescind?

Key rulings and principles

  • Buyer’s option (khiyār) for deficiency:
  • If the sold quantity/measure falls short of the agreed amount, the buyer acquires the option to rescind the contract (khiyār).
  • If the buyer, after discovering the shortfall, chooses to affirm/ratify the sale, the full price remains due; no subsequent reduction is permitted.
  • Trivial shortfalls (customary tolerance):
  • A negligible shortfall that local custom regards as insignificant does not give the buyer khiyār; the sale remains binding at full price.
  • Ambiguous measurement units:
  • When a measurement word (e.g., dhirāʿ) is ambiguous between two standards (hand cubit vs iron‑rod cubit):
  • If one meaning predominates in local custom, use that predominant standard.
  • If neither meaning predominates (tie/uncertainty), the contract is indeterminate and void unless clarified.
  • Distinction: maʿīyun vs fi al‑dhimmah:
  • If the object sold is specific and visible (maʿīyun), buyer’s khiyār applies for discovered shortfall.
  • If the sale was of an obligation/indeterminate amount (fi al‑dhimmah) and the measurement unit is disputed with no prevailing custom, the sale may be invalid due to indeterminacy.

Differences of opinion / authorities

  • Classical texts (e.g., al‑Tuhfa) and commentators differ on resolving measurement words: some require specification or reliance on local custom; others treat sale of a specific visible plot as valid with khiyār even if parties held different understandings earlier.
  • Al‑Mawardi and others noted: if the intended standard is the iron rod but a party insists on the hand cubit without local predominance, the contract may be defective.

Evidences / citations

  • References to classical fiqh discussions (al‑Tuhfa, al‑Mawardi, al‑Barqīnī/Balghīnī). No Qurʾan or hadith quotations were provided in the excerpt.

Practical action items

  • Always specify measurement units precisely in land/produce sale contracts (state whether dhirāʿ means iron‑rod or hand cubit and, if possible, give the measure in meters/centimeters as well).
  • Specify boundaries (hadd) in addition to measured counts to avoid conflict between number of units vs physical limits.
  • If buyer discovers a shortfall, he may rescind (exercise khiyār) or accept the sale; if he accepts, no later price reduction.
  • Document agreed measure and actual measurement (surveys, witnesses, written reports) before and after transfer to support any claim.
  • Where local custom varies, state clearly which custom/standard the parties adopt.

Key definitions and technical terms

  • Dhirāʿ (ذراع): cubit/rod length; two types:
  • dhirāʿ al‑ḥadīd — iron‑rod (fixed standard)
  • dhirāʿ al‑yad — hand‑cubit (variable)
  • Maʿīyun (المعين): a specifically identified, visible thing/plot.
  • Fi al‑dhimmah (في الذمة): an obligation/amount owed (not a specific visible object).
  • Khiyār (خيار): contractual option — here, the buyer’s right to rescind upon defect or shortfall.
  • Jahālat al‑mustathnā’ (جهالة المستثنى): uncertainty about what was excluded or about the bequest’s scope/duration.

Fiqh councils, methodology, and conference procedures (lessons from Majma‘ and Indian practice)

Decisions vs. recommendations

  • Decision (qarar): a juristic ruling produced by the council; treated as authoritative in the council’s reading of Sharīʿa.
  • Recommendation (tawsiyah): advisory, non‑binding guidance.

Institutional notes and differences between councils

  • Indian Majma‘ al‑Fiqh al‑Islami:
  • Established to produce collective, multidisciplinary juristic responses in India; active for decades and addressed many contemporary issues.
  • Emphasis on group ijtihād with jurists and subject‑matter experts.
  • International councils (examples):
  • Al‑Azhar’s Islamic Research Academy
  • Majmaʿ al‑Fiqh al‑Islami affiliated with Muslim World League / Rabita
  • OIC‑linked Fiqh council variants
  • Observed differences:
  • Councils differ in methodology, representativeness, and openness to multiple madhāhib. Users should check the issuing body and its composition before relying on a ruling.

Standard procedure of a fiqh research conference (stepwise)

  • Topic selection by administrative/scientific council based on relevance and national conditions.
  • Draft question sheet prepared and clarified by secretary/general secretary.
  • Question sheet circulated to muftīs of Sunni schools and to relevant technical experts; foreign materials translated into Arabic.
  • Collection of written responses and research articles.
  • Scientific section summarizes submissions, records evidences (Qurʾan, Sunnah, fiqh texts), and distributes summaries and full articles to participants in advance.
  • Plenary discussion with recorded minutes; rapporteur presents the subject.
  • Formation of a drafting subcommittee (including authors and representatives of differing views) to prepare recommendations.
  • Higher committee of senior jurists reviews and refines subcommittee output, then presents to the general assembly.
  • Final recommendations printed as approved; minority opinions recorded and published alongside majority recommendations.
  • The council avoids imposing recommendations lacking broad agreement.

Treatment of differences and minority opinions

  • Disagreements are preserved, represented in drafting committees, and printed alongside majority views with names of dissenting scholars.
  • The council refrains from imposing recommendations where consensus is absent.

Practical action items for councils, scholars, and organizers

  • For councils/organizers:
  • Include multidisciplinary experts early; maintain transparent review cycles and named reviewers.
  • Send clear question sheets to muftīs of all relevant madhāhib and to technical experts when needed.
  • Translate and summarize incoming research; distribute to participants before conferences.
  • Record plenary sessions and appoint competent note‑takers; publish minority views with names.
  • When publishing decisions, indicate whether they are decisions or recommendations and the council’s representativeness.
  • For scholars/participants:
  • If asked as a technical expert, provide factual explanations; muftīship/fatwa issuance remains the jurist’s role.
  • Submit written proposals in advance; prepare evidences (Qurʾan, hadith, fiqh precedents) and references.
  • Verify transliterations/names and institutional data before citation.
  • For users of council outputs:
  • Check the issuing body, its composition, and whether the output is a decision or a recommendation.
  • Request official copies (PDFs) for study and citation.

Consolidated practical checklist (what to do in practice)

  • Estates / bequests:
  • Draft usufruct bequests with precise scope and duration.
  • Do not sell a co‑owner’s share if doing so would defeat a testamentary usufruct, unless the legatee consents in writing or the sale is to the legatee.
  • Consult a local qualified jurist for disputes.
  • Land/measure sales:
  • Specify measurement unit explicitly (type of dhirāʿ and metric equivalent), and state boundaries clearly.
  • Measure and document the land before sale (survey, witnesses, written report).
  • If shortfall discovered, buyer may rescind; if buyer affirms, full price stands.
  • Rely on local custom to resolve ambiguous measurement terms; if no dominant custom, avoid making indeterminate contracts.
  • Using fiqh council outputs:
  • Verify the issuing council and whether the output is a decision or recommendation.
  • Seek official documentation/PDFs and study minority views where present.
  • When organizing juristic study: include multidisciplinary experts, distribute summaries in advance, and publish minority opinions alongside majority rulings.
