Waqf scope — does general language include ʿāmir (cultivated) and ghāmir (uncultivated) land?

The question

  • A person made a waqf using the phrase "what is in my ownership and under my control" (ما هو في ملكي وتحت سطوتي), then specified: "and that is the garden which Kawkab built" (وذلك البستان الذي أنشأته كوكب).
  • The question: does the waqf cover all owned property — both cultivated/developed land (ʿāmir) and undeveloped/fallow land (ghāmir) — or only the specific garden?

Ruling

  • The waqf is restricted to the specifically described property (the garden and what is mentioned alongside it); the ghāmir (undeveloped land) is not included.

Four reasons given

  • Reason 1 — Taqyīd overrides iṭlāq: The phrase "and that is…" (وذلك) is a tafsīr (explanation) and taqyīd (restriction) of the earlier general phrase "what is in my ownership." The legal rule is: when the absolute (muṭlaq) and the restricted (muqayyad) share the same ruling and cause (ḥukm and mūjib), are both affirmative, and the restriction comes before one acts on the absolute, the muṭlaq is interpreted in light of the muqayyad. In this case the restriction appeared in the same deed before anyone acted on the general phrase, so it governs.
  • Reason 2 — The certain vs. the doubtful: The garden is certain (mutayaqqan); the ghāmir land's inclusion is doubtful (mashkūk fīh). The legal maxim applies: the doubtful is treated as non-existent (al-mashkūk ka-l-maʿdūm).
  • Reason 3 — Contradiction with notarial practice: If general ownership language in a waqf deed automatically included all one's property, the same logic would invalidate sale and gift deeds — which routinely say "sold/gave all he owns" and then specify the item. This would contradict established practice of notarial scribes (muwwarriqūn) and invalidate countless transactions.
  • Reason 4 — Uṣūl rule on specifying members of a general category: Mentioning specific members (afrād) of a general category does not specialize (yukhṣṣiṣ) the general if the mention is a laqab (proper noun/title), but it does specialize if it is not a laqab. "The garden" is not a laqab; therefore its mention restricts the general formula to that specific property.

Supporting precedent

  • Ibn Makhrama's fatwas contain a directly analogous case: a man declared waqf over "all he owns" (جميع ما أملكه) and then specified particular properties. When shops outside the enumerated properties were claimed under the general phrase, he ruled they do not enter the waqf — "the absolute is interpreted by what he specified last" (إطلاق الملك محمول على ما فسّر به أخيرًا).

Waqf income distribution when designated beneficiaries (widows/orphans) are absent

The question

  • A man made a waqf on his descendants, allocating 6 qīrāṭ of 24 (one quarter) to widows and orphans from his lineage who have no fathers or husbands. The remaining 18 qīrāṭ were further divided: 4.5 qīrāṭ for waqf servants and descendants, and 13.5 qīrāṭ for the founder's descendants with a substitution clause ("whoever dies out, their share goes to the nearest male relative").
  • The question: in years when no qualifying widows or orphans exist, where does that quarter go? Options proposed: to the nearest male relatives, to women who have fathers or husbands, shared equally or with male preference, or held until a qualifying beneficiary appears?

Ruling

  • When the qualifying condition (being a widow or orphan with no father/husband) is absent, the share is treated like a munqaṭiʿ al-wasaṭ/al-ākhir (interrupted middle/end endowment) and transferred to the closest relatives of the founder by degree of kinship (aqrab al-nās raḥiman lil-wāqif).
  • When the qualifying condition returns (i.e., a widow or orphan from the lineage exists), the share reverts to them.

Rules for distribution during absence of beneficiaries

  • Recipient must be poor (faqīr); wealthy relatives do not receive this share.
  • Males and females are equal in priority — unlike inheritance, where males receive double.
  • Priority order by degree of kinship: descendants first (however low in lineage), then ascendants (father), then collaterals (brothers), then grandparents — following closeness of degree, not the inheritance hierarchy.
  • Example: a daughter's son (ibn bint) takes priority over a son's son's son (ibn ibn ibn) because the former is closer in degree.
  • If multiple relatives are tied in degree, the share is divided equally among all poor relatives of that degree.
  • If the qualifying women (those lacking the conditions) happen to be the closest relatives and are poor, they receive from the perspective of kinship proximity — not as designated beneficiaries of the waqf.
  • If all closest relatives are wealthy: the share goes to maṣāliḥ al-muslimīn (public Muslim interest); some scholars say it goes to the fuqarāʾ wa-l-masākīn (the poor and needy in general). Both views are recorded.

5th Fiqh Seminar of Majmaʿ al-Fatāwā Bilhind — Resolution 63 (1/15): Bank cards

Background

  • The 5th seminar was held at Dār al-ʿUlūm al-Ṣiddīqiyya, Mysore (South India), 10–12 Dhū al-Qaʿda 1426 AH / 11–13 December 2005, attended by approximately 200 scholars, economists, and medical experts from across India (Kashmir to Kerala) as well as UAE, Iran, and Nepal.
  • Topics: bank cards, health insurance, genetic testing, DNA fingerprinting.

Rulings on bank cards

  • ATM cards (Automated Teller Machine / ṣarrāf ālī): Permissible. The card simply accesses one's own deposited balance. Default permissibility (al-aṣl fī l-ashyāʾ al-ibāḥa) applies.
  • Debit cards (bīṭāqat al-saḥb al-fawrī): Permissible. Used for withdrawals, purchases, and account-to-account transfers; the money drawn is one's own.
  • Fees for ATM and debit cards: Permissible. The fee is compensation for the card itself and the service rendered, not payment for a loan.
  • Credit cards (bīṭāqat al-iʾtimān / krīdīt): Not permissible in their common prevalent form, because that form involves ribā (interest charged when balances are not repaid within the grace period).

Extended discussion: credit card fee structures and potential ḥalāl formulations

  • The core problem with credit cards is the interest charged on outstanding balances after the grace period — this is ribā.
  • Islamic banks have attempted alternative structures, including charging an upfront annual fee in exchange for a credit line. Sheikh Salem's assessment: charging a fee merely for "readiness to lend" (al-istiʿdād lil-iqrāḍ) with no tangible item sold is impermissible — there is no ʿain (object) sold and no manfaʿa (usufruct) provided in return for the payment, so the fee is in substance a payment for a loan, which is ribā.
  • Proposed compliant structure: sell the physical card as an ʿain (a tangible object with independent utility beyond mere credit access) for a price. The loan/credit facility is then a separate arrangement. This separates the payment from the loan contract, avoiding the prohibition on "every loan that draws a benefit to the lender" (kull qard jarra manfaʿa). The card must have genuine material utility (even modest) so that it constitutes a valid object of sale.
  • Key condition for compliance: no explicit stipulation (sharṭ) in the sale contract linking card purchase to the loan. If the bank states "I will only lend you if you buy this card," it becomes a condition attached to a loan that benefits the lender — which is impermissible. If the two transactions are genuinely separate (the bank sells cards to anyone regardless of credit, and separately lends via the card mechanism), the separation is valid even if customers understand the practical connection — but this is still makrūh (disliked) if the prior mutual understanding (muwāṭaʾa) is present.
  • Analogy to letters of credit (iʿtimādāt mustanādiyya): scholars have permitted charging a study/assessment fee for financing applications where the fee is payable regardless of whether financing is approved, and the applicant retains the option not to proceed — because the fee is genuinely detached from the loan itself.
  • AAOIFI (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) approach was discussed: AAOIFI primarily follows the Ḥanbalī school on ribā-related issues; it focuses on regulating existing market practices rather than theorizing ideal structures; it revised its position on credit card fees (initially permitted certain costs, then moved to restrict them). Its standards reflect regulatory constraints of existing financial systems, not purely theoretical fiqh.

Side discussion: Zakat valuation of commercial inventory (ʿurūḍ al-tijāra) vs. compensation for destruction (ḍamān)

The question

  • Sheikh Salem's position (citing Sheikh Muḥammad Ḥasan Hīṭū): commercial goods should be valued for zakat purposes as a single bulk lot (dafʿa wāḥida) at the price achievable if all were sold together (wholesale/bulk pricing), not at individual retail unit prices.
  • The question arose: does the same bulk-lot valuation apply when goods are destroyed and compensation (ḍamān) is owed to the owner?

Ruling — distinction between zakat valuation and ḍamān valuation

  • Zakat: Valued as a bulk lot at the price achievable for the whole inventory sold together, because zakat is premised on muwāsāt (mutual solidarity/sharing of actual wealth). The wealth must be assessed as it truly stands — if the entire stock is what the merchant holds, the relevant market price is what a buyer would pay for all of it at once.
  • Ḍamān (compensation for destruction): Each item is valued individually at its own market price (retail), regardless of whether the whole stock was destroyed or only part. The destruction event attaches to each specific item destroyed; the fact that items might be cheaper in bulk is irrelevant. The compensating party owes the value of what was actually destroyed, item by item.
  • Note: the value for ḍamān is the market value at the place of destruction, not the cost price paid by the owner (e.g., if goods were transported at great expense from afar, only the item's market value at the location of destruction is owed, not transportation costs).
  • Nizar raised a further point: zakat attaches to the totality of stock (the whole is subject to the obligation); ḍamān attaches only to the specific items destroyed — reinforcing why valuation methods differ.

Overarching methodological note

  • On maqāṣid al-sharīʿa (objectives of Islamic law): Sheikh Salem cautioned against using maqāṣid as an independent source for deriving legal rulings. Maqāṣid explain the wisdom (ḥikma) behind the sharīʿa's rules; they are not themselves ʿilal (legal causes/rationale) from which new rulings are deduced. Rulings are derived from the established sources and their ʿilal; maqāṣid clarify meaning but cannot override or modify rules derived through proper legal methodology.

Allah knows best.
