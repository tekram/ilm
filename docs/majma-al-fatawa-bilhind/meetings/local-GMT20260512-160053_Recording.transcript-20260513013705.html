Mosque Expansion, Waqf Materials, Health Insurance & Genetic Testing - May 12, 2026

Start: 2026-05-12T16:00:53

Overview

This session from the Majma al-Fatawa Bilhind (Assembly of Indian Fatwas) study circle covers four major rulings: mosque expansion and demolition, handling leftover mosque building materials, waqf properties seized by unjust regimes, health insurance, and DNA/genetic testing in Islamic law.

1. Expanding and Demolishing a Mosque

Background

A mosque is severely overcrowded — in winter worshippers crowd the covered sections (maharib), in summer they spill outside. The walls are structurally weak and could collapse in heavy rains. The raised ablution pools (jawabi — large stone tanks connected at the base) cause elderly and visually impaired worshippers to slip and fall. Someone has offered to expand and rebuild the mosque entirely.

Terminology clarified:

  • Maharib (محاريب) = covered sections of the mosque (not the imam's niche)
  • Al-Dahiyah (الضاحية) = open, uncovered prayer area
  • Jawabi (جوابي) = interconnected stone ablution pools; the first, where water is poured, is called al-bakiriyyah

Ruling

It is permissible — indeed obligatory given necessity — to demolish and rebuild the mosque. Four conditions must be met:

1. The waqf designation ("mosque") must not be changed to anything else

2. All of the mosque's land must be included in the new construction — none may be left unused

3. Permission of the special trustee (nazir khass), if one exists, or the general authority (government/judge), is required

4. Demolishing a wall or opening a door always requires the judge's permission regardless of whether a special trustee exists

Evidence: Qur'an 9:18 — "Only those who believe in Allah and the Last Day shall maintain the mosques of Allah." Imam al-Nawawi included restoration of deteriorating mosques within this verse's scope.

Note: One who works toward this expansion is rewarded, and the original donor's reward does not end — the new mosque is an expansion of their original contribution.

2. Leftover Building Materials After Mosque Renovation

Question

After renovating a mosque with new clay, timber, and plaster, what should be done with the old leftover materials? May they be sold and the proceeds used for the mosque or another mosque?

Ruling

  • Old materials cannot be sold while the mosque might ever need them in the future
  • The trustee is obligated to preserve them until the mosque needs them (e.g., for flooring backfill, patching, or future repairs)
  • They may be transferred to a nearer mosque only if it is definitively certain the original mosque will never need them again — and only to the nearest mosque, prioritized by proximity
  • Sale is not permissible according to the relied-upon later Shafi'i position (even though the Minhaj and the two Shaykhs — al-Nawawi and al-Rafi'i — had indicated that broken/unusable pieces fit only for burning could be sold, later scholars like al-Subki and those after him rejected this and the prohibition is the mu'tamad)
  • Exception: If preservation is genuinely impossible and there is reasonable fear the materials will be lost, destroyed, or seized by unjust authorities (yaddi al-zalama), then sale becomes obligatory — and proceeds must be used to purchase a comparable durable asset for the mosque
  • Mosque soil/dirt also cannot be sold even if the mosque has no current need for it; it must be kept or used for another mosque — this is the mu'tamad of the Shafi'i school

Referenced scholars: Ibn Hajar al-Haytami (Fatawa), Sheikh Salim (Fatawa), Abdullah ibn Umar Makhrama, Shaykh Ali Ba Yazid, Naashiri (al-Idah), Khuwarizmi

3. Waqf Properties Seized by Unjust Regimes (Syria)

Question

The former Syrian regime seized waqf properties using a Hanafi legal pretext (istibdal — exchange of waqf), then sold them to private individuals or converted them to schools, associations, trade fair halls, and roads. How should Muslims deal with entering, transacting with, or inheriting such properties?

Ruling

  • These seizures constitute usurpation (ghasb) — they are unlawful
  • It is haram to enter or inhabit a property that is known to be usurped waqf, even if the current occupant purchased it innocently
  • A mosque converted to a school retains its mosque status; entering it as though it were a school (i.e., as private property) is haram
  • Properties converted from one waqf type to another are equally or more prohibited for unauthorized use
  • A waqf cannot be sold, transferred, or nullified even if an unjust authority seizes it — it remains waqf until the Day of Judgment
  • Where the regime turned waqf land into public roads, this creates ongoing hardship. Scholars note that even devout Muslims pass through such streets out of necessity, though some avoid using them for funeral processions out of respect

Practical note for Syria: The new government faces social resistance when attempting to reclaim waqf properties, since many people bought them in good faith. This is identified as a major ongoing crisis requiring careful handling.

4. Selling a Mosque to Buy a Larger One (America)

Question

A Muslim community in America built a mosque. After 10–20 years, they want to sell it and purchase a larger property. Is this permissible?

Ruling

Absolutely not permissible. A mosque can never be sold under any circumstances — not due to overcrowding, not if abandoned, not even if an unjust ruler threatens to seize it. Sheikh Salim's fatwa is cited: "We know of no disagreement among the Shafi'is on this" — a mosque remains a mosque until the Day of Judgment.

5. Health Insurance — Resolution #64

Assembly Resolution

Health insurance as commonly practiced today involves gambling (qimar) because it has transformed healthcare from a service into a profit-driven trade.

1. Health insurance is not permissible in ordinary circumstances — whether government or private

2. In cases of legal compulsion, it is permissible; however, if one receives benefits exceeding what one paid in, the excess must be given in charity without the intention of receiving reward

3. Muslims are encouraged to establish Islamic cooperative alternatives (ta'min takaful)

Discussion Points

Sheikh Mustafa al-Zarqa argued health insurance could constitute a new form of mutual aid. Most scholarly assemblies rejected this. The approved Islamic model (AAOIFI / Fiqh Academy standard): a separate charitable participants' fund (sandooq) into which premiums are paid as donations, managed by an independent insurance company that charges a management fee. Surplus is returned to participants, donated to charity, or carried forward per the fund's charter.

On excess benefits: if the payer is identifiable the excess must be returned; otherwise it goes to general public interest. Someone who evades taxes should not benefit from public services beyond what they contributed — a parallel principle.

6. Genetic Testing — Resolution #65

1. If genetic testing confirms a fetus has severe, untreatable deformity, abortion is permissible before 120 days (before ensoulment)

2. Genetic testing showing a possibility of transmitting deformity to offspring does not justify stopping reproduction altogether

3. Genetic testing showing a possibility of future mental illness is not sufficient by itself to annul an existing marriage

4. Genetic testing is permissible and encouraged for disease diagnosis and medical research

Clinical example: Four brothers in Tarim developed progressive balance disorders in adulthood — a hereditary late-onset neurological condition illustrating how genetic diseases can appear selectively and at different ages.

7. DNA Fingerprinting — Resolution #66

1. DNA fingerprinting cannot be used to deny established paternity — only li'an (formal oath procedure) can

2. DNA fingerprinting may be used in cases of disputed paternity (majhul al-nasab) when no other legal evidence is available

3. DNA fingerprinting cannot be used alone to establish criminal liability in hadd or qisas cases

4. DNA fingerprinting may be used in other criminal cases, and a judge may compel a suspect to submit to DNA testing

Referenced position: The Majma' al-Fikr al-Islami al-Hindi accepted DNA for establishing paternity in disputed cases, while other councils are more restrictive.
