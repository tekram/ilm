Kitāb al-Hibā: Shared Passageway Waqf; Family Trade Partnerships & Water Rights - July 7, 2026

📅 2026-07-07 🕐 16:00

Overview

This session from the Majmaʿ al-Fatāwā bil-Hind study circle covers three topics. The first is a fatwā from Kitāb al-Hibā on the revocability of ibāha (bare permission) and the validity of a waqf over an undivided shared passageway. The second and principal topic is Resolution 84/5 (Ḥukm Ishtirāk al-Abnāʾ maʿa Ābāʾihim fī al-Tijāra) — the legal rules governing sons and other family members who participate in their father's business. The third is a resolution on water rights (ḥuqūq al-miyāh), covering government responsibilities, ownership by containment (iḥrāz), and restrictions on hoarding and trading water.

1. Kitāb al-Hibā: Revocable Passageway and Waqf of Mushāʿ

Background / Question

Two men purchased a plot from a single seller. Each built on his own portion. They agreed on a shared 3-dhirāʿ passageway (ḥaram) which the original seller had granted them as ibāha (a bare non-contractual licence). One of the two later sought to sell his share of the passageway; the original seller then declared the entire 3-dhirāʿ passage a waqf for Allāh. The question: Was the waqf valid? May the seller dispose of it without prior division?

Ruling

  • Ibāha (bare licence) may be revoked by the grantor at any time and terminates at his death. It creates no property right in the grantee.
  • On this basis, the seller's waqf of the passageway is valid and binding (ṣaḥīḥ, nāfidh), even though he had previously granted permission over it.
  • An undivided share (mushāʿ) may be sold, gifted, or made waqf without prior division (qisma). The other co-owner cannot block the transaction.

2. Resolution 84/5 — Sons Partnering with Fathers in Trade

Background

A recurring source of family litigation: when a father starts a business and sons work alongside him, their legal status is often undefined from the outset. The Majmaʿ issued a resolution with seven sub-clauses.

Clause 1 — Prior clarity is obligatory

At the inception of any family commercial venture, participants must define whether they are (a) partners (shurakāʾ), (b) hired employees (ajīr), or (c) gratuitous helpers (muʿāwinūn ʿalā sabīl al-birr). Failure to specify is the root cause of most family disputes brought to fatwa councils.

Clause 2 — Father starts; sons join without capital

  • Sons still in the father's financial custody (kafāla): treated as assistants; no independent profit share accrues.
  • Sons who are financially independent: their remuneration is set by ʿurf (custom and prevailing market rates).

Clause 3 — Sons contribute known capital to father's existing business

  • They become partners in proportion to their capital contribution (ḥasab nisba mā sāhamū fī raʾs al-māl).
  • Exception: if a son intended his contribution as a gift to the father, not a partnership stake — the entire business remains the father's property.

Clause 4 — Son starts the business; father sits in the shop out of respect

  • If the son founded the business with his own capital and merely seated his father there out of filial respect, or registered it under his father's name, the business belongs exclusively to the son.
  • The father's presence is ʿāriya (gratuitous use), not a shareholding. The son's claim may be proved by testimony (bayyina).

Clause 5 — Family working under a mutual agreement (itifāqiyya)

Revenues are divided according to the agreed terms, regardless of whether participants work inside the home or outside it.

Clause 6 — Joint vs. separate family commerce

  • (a) Joint commerce: revenues split equally (bi-l-sawiyya) among all participants.
  • (b) Separate/independent commerce (no joint contract): outside worker owes nothing to the home worker and vice versa.

Clause 7 — Daughters' obligation to support parents

  • Per Shāfiʿī fiqh, daughters bear the same obligation to support (nafaqa) parents as sons, subject to their means. Custom in many communities imposes this only on sons — a departure from the legal ruling.
  • At inheritance, sons who alone bore parental expenses cannot legally deduct those expenses from the estate; their extra contributions count as ṣadaqa or birr, not a debt owed by the estate.

Supplementary questions discussed

Son too embarrassed to discuss his share with his father: He must raise the matter directly. Remaining silent does not entitle him to take his share by way of ẓafar (self-help/self-seizure). Ẓafar is only permitted where the other party would actually refuse.

Wife gave husband money without specifying "loan": If the money is still extant, it may be recovered based on the husband knowing his own intention. If spent, the husband is at fault for not clarifying.

Excess profit beyond what was agreed: Any profit above what was stipulated enters the shared pool of the partnership.

3. Water Rights Resolution

Ruling — nine sub-clauses

1. A responsible authority must be designated to monitor and maintain groundwater levels, assisted by government.

2. Population displacement for dam or water-infrastructure construction is permissible when necessary, provided fair compensation covering all losses and resettlement costs is paid.

3. Flood protection for all residents must be provided; the least-harmful available measures are to be adopted.

4. Water ownership is established by iḥrāz (containment in a vessel, intentionally), whether small or large — unless such containment causes harm to the public.

5. Water trade is permissible wherever ownership is validly established, provided it does not harm public interests.

6. Hoarding water from public taps, pumps, or channels beyond personal need in order to trade it is prohibited.

7. Subdividing land in areas that store or drain clean or wastewater is prohibited where it causes public harm — even if the government issues a permit.

8. Providing water to every citizen is among the government's core duties; it may levy service fees and may cut off those who refuse to pay.

9. The government must establish an adequate sewage and drainage system (majārī wa-judwal māʾiyya); citizens must respect applicable regulations.
