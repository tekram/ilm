Waqf for Women's Mosques, Work-Site Domicile & Prisoner Rights - June 16, 2026

📅 2026-06-16 🕐 16:00

Overview

This session from the Majmaʿ al-Fatāwā bil-Hind study circle covers three distinct topics: a fatwā on whether a general waqf endowment for "the mosques of ʿAynāt" includes small women's prayer spaces the founder likely did not know of; a Majmaʿ resolution on whether one's work city constitutes a second legal domicile (waṭan) for prayer purposes; and a resolution on the rights of accused persons and prisoners.

1. Waqf on Palm Trees for "Masājid ʿAynāt" — Does It Include Hidden Women's Mosques?

Background / Question

A man endowed (waqafa) palm trees on "the mosques of ʿAynāt" (masājid ʿAynāt) — a known locality in the Ḥaḍramawt region. After his death it became apparent that there are small, inconspicuous women's prayer spaces (masājid al-nisāʾ al-khafiyyāt) in ʿAynāt that the founder in all likelihood never knew of. The question: does the waqf include these women's mosques, or is it restricted by the founder's presumed lack of knowledge of them?

Side note on ʿAynāt: The region is associated above all with the great scholar Shaykh Abū Bakr ibn Sālim (d. 992 AH), who famously began his students directly on the Minhāj of al-Nawawī from the very first day, skipping introductory texts, on the grounds that "time is too short to read one book after another."

Ruling

The waqf includes all mosques in ʿAynāt, known and unknown to the founder. The operative principle is ʿumūm al-lafẓ (the generality of the wording). The founder said "the mosques of ʿAynāt" without restriction; there is no clear counter-indicator (qarīna ẓāhira) in his wording that would restrict the meaning to large or well-known mosques.

The rule: we do not restrict a general endowment deed based on what we assume about the founder's mental state, unless that assumption is anchored in an explicit textual indicator in the deed itself. The presumption that he "probably" did not intend the women's mosques is not itself a qarīna sufficient to override general language. Wa-Llāhu aʿlam.

2. Resolution 75/5: Work-Location as a Second Legal Domicile (Waṭan)

The Resolution's Position

Building a personal home at one's work location and residing there long-term constitutes a second true domicile (waṭan ḥaqīqī) in addition to one's original hometown. Accordingly, a person with such an arrangement may not shorten (qaṣr) prayers when in his work city.

Shāfiʿī Analysis and Tension

This conflicts in important respects with the Shāfiʿī definition of waṭan, which has a strict threshold:

  • A true domicile (waṭan aṣlī / ḥaqīqī) is a place where a person intends to remain permanently (nawyat al-iqāma al-dāʾima) — where his family lives, which he will never leave except for a need, and to which he returns.
  • A temporary work location — even one with family present and a rented house — does not become a waṭan under Shāfiʿī rules unless the person genuinely forms the intention of remaining there permanently.
  • The determinative factor under the Shāfiʿī school is personal permanent intention, not the fact of building a house or long-term physical presence.

Practical implication: a person working in one city while maintaining a family home in another city, with an intention to eventually return, would under the Shāfiʿī view still be a traveler in his work city and may shorten prayers there.

3. Resolution on Rights of the Accused and Prisoners

The Resolution's Positions

1. Police and security forces must operate strictly within legal boundaries; no person may be arrested without solid, court-grade evidence (adilla muḥkama).

2. Torture of detainees is categorically prohibited.

3. Guantanamo-type detention facilities established by the United States and some European states — where prisoners are subjected to inhuman torture in clear violation of international law — are condemned. The resolution calls on the United Nations and all international human-rights bodies to hold the relevant states accountable.

4. Right to legal defense: Every accused person has the right to be defended in court. Lawyers who refuse to represent persons accused of terrorism are condemned for failing their professional and human duty.

Supplementary Discussion

May a lawyer defend a client he knows with certainty to be guilty, if the defense will result in the client escaping punishment? The view discussed: probably not — if guilt is definitively established and legal defense will certainly secure the client's release from a deserved penalty, then assisting in this defense may constitute assistance in an ongoing injustice.
