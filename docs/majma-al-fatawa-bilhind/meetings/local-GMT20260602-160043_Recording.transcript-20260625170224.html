Waqf Nazarship Conditions & Sale of Seized Waqf Properties - June 2, 2026

📅 2026-06-02 🕐 16:00

Overview

This session from the Majmaʿ al-Fatāwā bil-Hind study circle covers two detailed legal opinions (fatāwā) on waqf law. The first addresses whether a waqf founder can alter the stipulated nazarship chain through a later will. The second addresses whether waqf properties seized by oppressors may be sold to preserve their value. The session closes with a live exchange on contemporary Syria and the new Syrian currency.

1. Waqf Nazarship: Does a Later Will Override the Original Waqf Deed?

Background / Question

A man endowed properties on a mosque and others (awqaf ʿalā masjid wa-ghayrih). In the original waqf deed he stipulated: nāẓir for himself during his lifetime, then for the most learned and pious (al-arshhad al-aʿlā min al-awraʿ) of his children, then their children, then the most learned of his father's descendants. He documented this and had it witnessed. Later, in a separate will (waṣiyya), he wrote: "I appoint my son [N] as nāẓir over the waqf, to consult his brother [M] and take his advice; and I appoint their paternal uncle [Z] as supervisor over them both."

Three questions: (1) Is the will's designation operative? (2) If operative, does it constitute a removal (ʿazl) of the previously stipulated post-death nāẓir, voiding his nazarship? (3) Or is it merely the addition of a joint partner to the original nāẓir?

Ruling

  • The Shāfiʿī scholars (aimmat al-Shāfiʿiyya) are unanimous: only conditions stipulated at the time of the waqf (ḥāl al-waqf) are legally effective. Nothing added afterward — even in the same session, let alone in a will written years later — has any weight.
  • The waqf becomes binding the moment the founder's words are uttered (bi-farāgh lafẓih). No new condition may be appended after that point.
  • The word jaʿaltu ("I assign") in the will is ambiguous: it may mean sharṭtu (I stipulated a condition) or fawwaḍtu (I delegated authority). On either reading, since the will post-dates the waqf, the named son acquires no nazarship rights whatsoever.
  • Conclusion: After the founder's death, nazarship passes automatically to whoever satisfies the conditions of being most learned and most pious among his descendants, as originally stipulated in the deed. The will has no legal effect on the waqf.

Referenced Texts

Ibn Ḥajar al-Haytamī (Tuḥfat al-Muḥtāj); al-Ramlī (Fatāwā al-Ramlī).

2. Sale of Waqf Properties Seized by Oppressors

Background / Question

Waqf properties endowed for several mosques have been seized by local oppressors (ẓalama). The situation breaks into three types: (a) some oppressors are offering to take the properties on very long-term leases of 100+ years; (b) some are seizing the properties outright with no agreement; (c) some are prepared to purchase the properties at fair or above-market prices. The assets include land, orchards, palm trees, buildings, and ruined structures. The nāẓir asks: can these waqf properties be sold?

Ruling: Two Categories

Category 1 — Land and permanent immovable structures:

These may not be sold under any circumstances, even under sustained and severe oppression. Land (arḍ) does not perish; when justice returns, the property can be reclaimed and the waqf restored. This is the Shāfiʿī position and is shared by all four schools on this point.

Observation: A long-term lease of 100+ years is worse than outright sale. In a century, the waqf documentation may be lost, and the lessee's descendants may claim ownership.

Category 2 — Perishable and destructible assets (buildings, timber, palm trees, animals):

Sale may be permitted when it is the only feasible means of preserving the asset's value (al-ḥifāẓ ʿalā qīmat al-waqf). Conditions:

1. The sale proceeds (athmān) must be used immediately to purchase equivalent or superior waqf assets.

2. The transaction must be conducted with evident benefit (ghubṭa ẓāhira wa-maṣlaḥa bayyana) to the waqf.

3. This is a last resort (ākhir al-ḥulūl); preference is always reclamation when justice becomes possible.

3. Supplementary Discussion: Contemporary Syria

Sheikh Nizar Mashal mentioned that a Syrian scholarly household had their properties and waqf assets seized by Hafez al-Assad's government by a specific decree (qarār) in which the family's names were explicitly listed. With the change of government in Syria, there is now hope that the documentation can be verified and assets recovered.

Currency exchange: Approximately 63% of the old Syrian currency in circulation has been exchanged for the new currency. The fatwa council (dīwān al-iftāʾ) issued guidance that the exchange option must remain available at all times — the mere prohibition on circulation of old currency should not prevent a holder from being able to convert it.
