Waqf on Owned Canal: Two Grounds of Invalidity; Cosmetic Surgery; Women's Work; Polar Prayer Times - June 23, 2026

📅 2026-06-23 🕐 16:00

Overview

This session from the Majmaʿ al-Fatāwā bil-Hind study circle covers four topics. First, a fatwā on a claimed waqf over an irrigation canal that is invalid on two independent grounds. Second, a Majmaʿ resolution on cosmetic surgery. Third, a resolution on women's work outside the home. Fourth, an exchange on determining prayer times for ʿishāʾ in extreme latitudes.

1. Waqf over a Shared Grove Claimed to Benefit an Owned Canal — Two Grounds of Invalidity

Background / Question

A mixed grove (ghayḍa) of palm trees and other plants is jointly owned by a group. In a sales dispute, one party claims that one-third of the grove is waqf endowed for the upkeep (ʿimāra) of the group's shared irrigation canal (sāqiya) — the very same canal that is owned by the same group. The property has been sold, cut, and dealt with freely for generations without any prior claim of waqf.

Ruling: Two Independent Grounds of Invalidity

Ground 1 — Waqf on oneself (waqf ʿalā nafsihi):

The purported waqf would benefit the irrigation canal (sāqiya), which belongs to the same people who owned the one-third — including the alleged endower. This makes it a waqf ʿalā al-nafs (endowment that benefits oneself), which the Shāfiʿī scholars explicitly hold to be void (bāṭil).

Ground 2 — Waqf on upkeep of owned property:

The text of Ḥusn al-Najwa is cited: Shaykh Jamāl al-Dīn Muḥammad ibn ʿUmar Riḍwān held that a waqf for the repair of a sāqiya is valid — but his student Abū Yazīd clarified that this applies only where the sāqiya is itself waqf (already endowed). Where the sāqiya is privately owned (mamlūka), a waqf for its upkeep is void, as the authoritative scholars explicitly stated (waqf ʿalā mamlūk). Here the sāqiya is privately owned → both grounds apply → the waqf is void on either basis independently.

2. Cosmetic Surgery — Resolution

Ruling

The Majmaʿ distinguishes between three categories:

Permissible — Remedying a defect causing harm: Surgery to correct a defect (ʿayb) that causes genuine physical or psychological harm is permissible — whether the defect is congenital, caused by injury, or the result of illness.

Using medications to reverse signs of aging: Using medications (as opposed to surgery) that contain no prohibited substance to reverse visible signs of aging — greying hair, wrinkles — appears to be permissible.

Prohibited — Pure aesthetic enhancement: Surgery performed solely for the purpose of cosmetic enhancement — where the organ is healthy and there is no defect — is not permissible. This involves unnecessary alteration of the human form (tabdīl khalq Allāh) without a justifying need.

Prohibited — Concealing identity: Surgery to alter one's physical features to hide one's identity is prohibited — except where a person faces genuine danger to life, in which case necessity may override the prohibition.

3. Women's Work Outside the Home — Resolution

Resolution (abridged)

1. Islam divides responsibilities between men and women: all external responsibilities — including earning a livelihood (kasb al-maʿāsh) — belong to men; internal domestic responsibilities belong to women.

2. Coercing women to earn income when there is no genuine need is a social injustice (ẓulm ijtimāʿī), because a woman already performs the dual labor of child-rearing and household management.

3. Islamic law does not impose the obligation of earning a livelihood on women under ordinary circumstances. However, earning income is permissible for women if conducted within the limits of the sharīʿa.

4. A man carries the financial obligation (nafaqa) of the family. If a woman works and earns income, she is not obligated to contribute to family expenses from her earnings unless she chooses to voluntarily.

4. Q&A: Prayer Times at Extreme Latitudes — ʿIshāʾ in Polar Regions

Question

A person is in a location where the red twilight (shafaq al-aḥmar) never disappears in summer. How does he determine the time for ʿishāʾ?

Ruling

The Shāfiʿī authorities hold: such a person determines ʿishāʾ time by reference to the nearest location where the red twilight does disappear (aqrab mawḍiʿ yaghibu fīh al-shafaq al-aḥmar), regardless of whether that location is an inhabited city or an uninhabited area. The word "city" (balad) used in some texts is an expression based on the typical case, not a strict condition — what matters is the closest area where twilight sets.

If two equidistant locations have different times for the disappearance of twilight, the majority view is that one uses the earlier of the two, since that corresponds to the more conservative determination of ʿishāʾ entry time.
